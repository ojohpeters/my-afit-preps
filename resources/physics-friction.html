🧱 What is Friction?

1. Definition

Friction is a force that opposes the relative motion (or attempted motion) between two surfaces in contact. It always acts in the direction opposite to the motion.

🌟 Real life: push a heavy box and it resists sliding — that resistance is friction. It is caused by the tiny bumps and roughness of the two surfaces interlocking.

2. Types of friction

TypeWhat it is
Static (limiting) frictionActs on a body still at rest; must be overcome before it starts to move. The maximum value just before sliding is the limiting friction.
Kinetic / dynamic (sliding) frictionActs on a body that is already sliding.
Rolling frictionActs when a body rolls (a wheel or ball). It is much smaller than sliding friction.
Fluid / viscous friction (drag)Resistance when moving through a liquid or gas (e.g. air resistance).
🧠 Two key facts: limiting (static) friction > kinetic friction (it is harder to START moving something than to keep it moving), and rolling friction < sliding friction (why wheels and ball bearings are used).

📐 The Coefficient of Friction (F = μR)

1. Normal reaction and the friction formula

The two surfaces press together with a force called the normal reaction R (it acts perpendicular to the surface). On a flat, horizontal surface, R equals the weight: R = mg.

Friction F = μ × R   ⇒   μ = F / R
  • F = friction force (N)
  • R = normal reaction (N)
  • μ (mu) = coefficient of friction — it has no unit (a ratio of two forces).

μ_s (static) is slightly larger than μ_k (kinetic) for the same surfaces.

2. The laws of friction

  • Friction is proportional to the normal reaction (F ∝ R).
  • Friction is independent of the area of contact.
  • Friction is independent of the speed once the body is moving.
  • It depends on the nature/roughness of the two surfaces.

3. Worked examples

Find the friction force: a 10 kg block rests on a floor with μ = 0.4 (g = 10).
R = mg = 10 × 10 = 100 N
F = μR = 0.4 × 100 = 40 N
So a force just above 40 N is needed to make it slide.
Find the coefficient: a friction force of 30 N acts when the normal reaction is 120 N.
μ = F ÷ R = 30 ÷ 120 = 0.25
Minimum force to move a block: a 2 kg block, μ = 0.5 (g = 10).
R = mg = 20 N; F = μR = 0.5 × 20 = 10 N
The block just moves when the applied force exceeds 10 N.

⛰️ Friction on an Inclined Plane

1. Resolving the weight on a slope

On a plane inclined at angle θ, split the weight W (= mg) into two parts:

Down the slope: mg·sin θ   |   Into the slope (normal): R = mg·cos θ

So the friction available is F = μR = μ·mg·cos θ.

⚠️ Trap: the pull that makes a body slide down is mg·sinθ, and the normal reaction (used for friction) is mg·cosθ — do not swap them.

2. Angle of repose (angle of friction)

The angle of repose is the slope angle at which a body is just about to slide. At that point the down-slope pull equals the friction:

mg·sin θ = μ·mg·cos θ  ⇒  μ = tan θ
Example: a block just begins to slide when the plane is tilted to 30°. Find μ.
μ = tan 30° = 0.577

3. Worked incline example

Friction on a slope: a 4 kg block on a 30° incline, μ = 0.5 (g = 10).
R = mg·cos θ = 4 × 10 × cos30° = 40 × 0.866 = 34.6 N
F = μR = 0.5 × 34.6 = 17.3 N

⚙️ Advantages, Disadvantages & Control

1. Friction: friend and foe

Advantages (why we need it)Disadvantages (why it is a nuisance)
Lets us walk without slippingCauses wear and tear of moving parts
Brakes stop cars; tyres grip the roadProduces unwanted heat
Writing (pen/pencil on paper)Wastes energy → lowers machine efficiency
Nails and screws hold; belt drives workReduces the speed of machines

2. Reducing and increasing friction

Ways to REDUCE friction
  • Lubrication — oil or grease between surfaces.
  • Ball / roller bearings — turn sliding friction into smaller rolling friction.
  • Smoothing / polishing the surfaces.
  • Streamlining — reduces fluid friction (air/water drag).
  • Using an air cushion (e.g. hovercraft).
Ways to INCREASE friction (for grip)
  • Treads / grooves on tyres and shoe soles.
  • Making surfaces rougher.
  • Spreading sand on slippery roads.

⭐ Exam reminders

  • Friction always opposes motion; it converts kinetic energy into heat.
  • F = μR; on a flat floor R = mg; on a slope R = mg·cos θ.
  • μ has no unit; limiting (static) friction > kinetic friction.
  • At the angle of repose, μ = tan θ.
