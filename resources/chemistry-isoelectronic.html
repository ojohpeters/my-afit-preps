🔬 What "Isoelectronic" Means

1. The definition

Isoelectronic species are atoms or ions that have the SAME number of electrons — and therefore the same electronic configuration.

The word breaks into iso (meaning "same" or "equal") + electronic (electrons). So it literally means "same electrons".

🌟 Think of electrons like passengers. Two different buses (different atoms/ions) are "isoelectronic" if they carry exactly the same number of passengers — even though the buses themselves (the nuclei) are different. Na⁺ and Mg²⁺ look different on paper, but each carries 10 electrons, so they are isoelectronic.

Key point: isoelectronic species need the SAME electron count. They usually have DIFFERENT numbers of protons (different atomic numbers).

2. Recap — how to count electrons

Neutral atom

A neutral atom has no charge because its protons and electrons balance. So:

electrons in a neutral atom = atomic number (Z)

Example: sodium (Na) has atomic number 11, so a neutral Na atom has 11 electrons.

Cation (positive ion)

A cation is positive because it LOST electrons. Losing negative electrons leaves extra positive charge. So it has fewer electrons than its atomic number:

electrons in a cation = atomic number − charge

Example: Na⁺ has charge +1, so electrons = 11 − 1 = 10.

Anion (negative ion)

An anion is negative because it GAINED electrons. Extra electrons add negative charge. So it has more electrons than its atomic number:

electrons in an anion = atomic number + charge

Example: O²⁻ has charge −2 (gained 2), so electrons = 8 + 2 = 10.

🌟 Easy memory trick: the charge tells you the direction. A + charge means electrons were removed (subtract). A − charge means electrons were added (add). "Plus means minus electrons, minus means plus electrons."

3. Worked examples — counting electrons in ions

Example 1 — Na⁺ (Na has Z = 11): It is a cation (+1), so it LOST 1 electron. Electrons = 11 − 1 = 10.
Example 2 — Mg²⁺ (Mg has Z = 12): Cation (+2), lost 2 electrons. Electrons = 12 − 2 = 10.
Example 3 — Al³⁺ (Al has Z = 13): Cation (+3), lost 3 electrons. Electrons = 13 − 3 = 10.
Example 4 — O²⁻ (O has Z = 8): Anion (−2), gained 2 electrons. Electrons = 8 + 2 = 10.
Example 5 — F⁻ (F has Z = 9): Anion (−1), gained 1 electron. Electrons = 9 + 1 = 10.
Example 6 — N³⁻ (N has Z = 7): Anion (−3), gained 3 electrons. Electrons = 7 + 3 = 10.
Example 7 — Cl⁻ (Cl has Z = 17): Anion (−1), gained 1 electron. Electrons = 17 + 1 = 18.
Example 8 — Ca²⁺ (Ca has Z = 20): Cation (+2), lost 2 electrons. Electrons = 20 − 2 = 18.

Notice how examples 1–6 all landed on 10 electrons. That is not a coincidence — it is the famous 10-electron family, coming up in the next tab.

🧮 Finding Isoelectronic Groups

1. The 10-electron family (the "neon family")

Any species that ends up with 10 electrons has the same electronic configuration as neon (Ne, Z = 10). This is the most-tested group in exams.

N³⁻7−3 (gained 3)7 + 3 = 10
O²⁻8−2 (gained 2)8 + 2 = 10
F⁻9−1 (gained 1)9 + 1 = 10

Na⁺11+1 (lost 1)11 − 1 = 10

So N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺ and Al³⁺ are all isoelectronic — each has 10 electrons.

2. The 18-electron family (the "argon family")

Any species with 18 electrons matches argon (Ar, Z = 18).

So P³⁻, S²⁻, Cl⁻, Ar, K⁺ and Ca²⁺ are all isoelectronic with 18 electrons each.

3. The 2-electron family (the "helium family")

The smallest common group matches helium (He, Z = 2) with just 2 electrons.

SpeciesAtomic number (Z)ChargeElectron count
H⁻1−1 (gained 1)1 + 1 = 2

Li⁺3+1 (lost 1)3 − 1 = 2
Be²⁺4+2 (lost 2)4 − 2 = 2

So H⁻, He, Li⁺ and Be²⁺ are all isoelectronic with 2 electrons each.

4. Method box — how to test if two species are isoelectronic

Step 1: count electrons of species A · Step 2: count electrons of species B · Step 3: compare
  • ① Count the electrons of the first species (use the rules: neutral = Z, cation = Z − charge, anion = Z + charge).
  • ② Count the electrons of the second species the same way.
  • ③ If the two counts are equal, they are isoelectronic. If not, they are not.
🌟 The nuclei can be totally different — it is ONLY the final electron count that decides. Ignore the protons when comparing; only the electrons matter for "isoelectronic".

5. Worked examples — testing pairs

Are Na⁺ and F⁻ isoelectronic? Na⁺ = 11 − 1 = 10 electrons. F⁻ = 9 + 1 = 10 electrons. Same → YES, isoelectronic (both 10).
Are K⁺ and Cl⁻ isoelectronic? K⁺ = 19 − 1 = 18. Cl⁻ = 17 + 1 = 18. Same → YES (both 18).
Are Na⁺ and Cl⁻ isoelectronic? Na⁺ = 10 electrons, Cl⁻ = 18 electrons. Different → NO.
Which ion is isoelectronic with Ne (10 e⁻)? Test the options — Mg²⁺ = 12 − 2 = 10. Match → Mg²⁺.
Which species is isoelectronic with Ar (18 e⁻)? S²⁻ = 16 + 2 = 18. Match → S²⁻.

📏 Ionic Size Trend in an Isoelectronic Series

1. Same electrons, different protons

In an isoelectronic series, every species has the SAME number of electrons but a DIFFERENT number of protons (different nuclear charge). This difference in protons is what changes the size.

More protons → stronger inward pull on the same electron cloud → SMALLER ion

The nucleus and the electrons attract each other. When there are more protons in the nucleus but the same number of electrons to hold, the pull on each electron is stronger, so the whole electron cloud is dragged in tighter and the ion shrinks.

🌟 Imagine the same number of dogs on leashes. One owner (few protons) holds them loosely — they spread out wide. A stronger owner (more protons) pulls all the leashes tight — the same dogs now huddle in a smaller space. Same "electrons", tighter grip = smaller size.

2. The rule for the whole series

Across an isoelectronic series, ionic radius DECREASES as nuclear charge (proton / atomic number) INCREASES

So the species with the fewest protons is the biggest, and the one with the most protons is the smallest.

3. Worked ordering — the 10-electron series

Arrange N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ (all 10 electrons) in order of size.

Order of decreasing size: N³⁻ > O²⁻ > F⁻ > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺. The more protons, the smaller — so N³⁻ (7 protons) is biggest and Al³⁺ (13 protons) is smallest.

Why is Al³⁺ smaller than Na⁺? Both have 10 electrons, but Al³⁺ has 13 protons versus Na⁺'s 11. The extra positive charge in Al³⁺ pulls those 10 electrons in much tighter, so Al³⁺ is the smaller ion.

4. Why anions are bigger and cations smaller than their parent atom

Anion vs its parent atom

An anion gained electrons. Now there are more electrons than protons, so the protons cannot hold them as tightly, and the extra electron–electron repulsion pushes the cloud outward. The electron cloud swells, so an anion is LARGER than its parent atom (e.g. Cl⁻ is bigger than a Cl atom).

Cation vs its parent atom

A cation lost electrons. Often it loses its entire outer shell, so it may even have fewer shells. The same number of protons now pulls fewer electrons, holding them tighter. So a cation is SMALLER than its parent atom (e.g. Na⁺ is much smaller than a Na atom).

🌟 Add passengers to a bus (anion) and it feels crowded and bulges; remove passengers (cation) and the remaining few are pulled to the middle, leaving a smaller, tighter group.
