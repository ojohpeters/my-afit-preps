🌈 Dispersion of Light

1. Splitting white light

When white light passes through a glass prism it spreads out into a band of seven colours called the spectrum. This splitting of white light into its colours is called dispersion.

Order of the spectrum: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet (ROYGBIV)
🌟 A rainbow is dispersion in nature — raindrops act like tiny prisms, splitting sunlight into its colours.

2. Why does dispersion happen?

White light is a mixture of colours, each with a different wavelength. When they enter glass they slow down and bend (refract) by different amounts:

  • Red has the longest wavelength and bends (refracts) the least.
  • Violet has the shortest wavelength and bends (refracts) the most.

Because each colour bends differently, they separate — that is dispersion.

3. Recombining the spectrum

The colours can be joined back into white light using:

  • A second, inverted prism that bends the colours back together, or
  • Newton's colour disc (a disc painted with the 7 colours) — when spun fast it appears white.

This proves white light is truly a mixture of the spectrum colours.

🎨 Colour

1. Primary and secondary colours of light

The three primary colours of light are Red, Green and Blue. Mixing (adding) coloured lights gives:

Adding lightsResult
Red + Green + BlueWhite
Red + GreenYellow
Green + BlueCyan
Red + BlueMagenta

Yellow, cyan and magenta are the secondary colours of light. (Note: mixing PAINTS/pigments works differently — pigments subtract colours.)

2. Why objects have colour

An object's colour is the colour of light it reflects to your eye; it absorbs the rest.

ObjectUnder white light it…
Red objectreflects red, absorbs all other colours
White objectreflects ALL colours
Black objectabsorbs ALL colours (reflects none)
💡 Trick: a red object seen under pure blue light looks black — there is no red light for it to reflect.

3. Colour filters

A colour filter only lets its own colour pass through and absorbs the others. A red filter transmits only red light; a green filter transmits only green.

Example: white light through a green filter comes out green (all other colours are absorbed).

👁️ The Human Eye

1. Parts of the eye and their jobs

PartFunction
CorneaTransparent front; does most of the bending (refraction) of light.
IrisThe coloured part; controls the size of the pupil.
PupilThe hole that lets light in (widens in dim light, narrows in bright light).
LensFine-focuses light onto the retina; changes shape (accommodation).
Ciliary musclesChange the thickness/shape of the lens.
RetinaLight-sensitive screen where the image forms (real & inverted).
Rods & conesRods detect dim light; cones detect colour.
Optic nerveCarries signals to the brain.
Blind spotWhere the optic nerve leaves — has no light receptors.

2. Accommodation

Accommodation is the eye's ability to focus on objects at different distances by changing the shape of the lens (using the ciliary muscles):

  • For a near object, the lens becomes thicker (more curved, more powerful).
  • For a distant object, the lens becomes thinner (flatter).

The near point of a normal eye is about 25 cm; the far point is infinity.

3. The eye compared with a camera

In both, the image formed is real, inverted and smaller than the object.

🔭 Eye Defects & Optical Instruments

1. Common eye defects and their correction

DefectProblemCorrection
Short sight (myopia)Can't see distant objects; image forms IN FRONT of the retinaA concave (diverging) lens
Long sight (hypermetropia)Can't see near objects; image forms BEHIND the retinaA convex (converging) lens
PresbyopiaOld-age loss of accommodation (lens stiffens)Reading / bifocal lenses
AstigmatismUneven curvature of the cornea/lens → blurredA cylindrical lens
🧠 Memory: short sight → the image falls SHORT of the retina (in front), fixed with a coNCave lens; long sight → convex lens.
