🌍 PART 1 — Basic Concepts & Key Terms

1. What is ecology?

Ecology is the study of the relationships between living organisms and their environment — how living things affect, and are affected by, each other and by their non-living surroundings.

To talk about ecology clearly, you must know a small set of key terms. Learn these first — most exam questions test them directly.

The key ecological terms (learn this table)
TermMeaning
EcologyThe study of the relationships between living organisms and their environment.
EnvironmentAll the surrounding conditions — living and non-living — that affect an organism.
EcosystemA community of living things together with their non-living environment, interacting as one unit (e.g. a pond, a forest).
HabitatThe specific place where an organism lives — its natural home or address (e.g. a stream for a fish).
PopulationAll the organisms of the same species living in the same area at the same time.
CommunityAll the different populations (species) living and interacting together in an area.
BiosphereThe part of the Earth — land, water and air — where living things exist.
Ecological nicheThe role or function of an organism in its ecosystem — how and what it feeds on, where it lives, its job in the community.
🌟 Memory: the habitat is the organism's address (where it lives); the niche is its profession (what it does for a living). Do not confuse them.

2. Biotic and abiotic factors

An environment is made of two kinds of factors:

  • Biotic factors — the living parts of the environment (plants, animals, bacteria, fungi).
  • Abiotic factors — the non-living parts (temperature, light, water, soil, air, pH).
Examples side by side
Biotic (living) factorsAbiotic (non-living) factors
Green plants (producers)Temperature
Animals (consumers)Light / sunlight
Bacteria and fungi (decomposers)Water and humidity
Predators, prey and parasitesSoil, its minerals and pH
Competitors of the same or other speciesAir and gases (oxygen, carbon dioxide), wind
🌟 Quick test: if it was ever alive, it is biotic. If it was never alive (heat, water, rock, gas), it is abiotic.

3. Levels of ecological organisation

Ecology is organised from the smallest unit up to the largest, each level built from the one before it:

Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biosphere
  • Organism — a single living thing (one grasshopper).
  • Population — many organisms of the same species in one area (all the grasshoppers in a field).
  • Community — all the different populations living together (grasshoppers + grass + birds + soil bacteria).
  • Ecosystem — the community plus its non-living environment (the field community + soil, air, water, sunlight).
  • Biosphere — all ecosystems on Earth; the whole living zone of the planet.
🌟 Think of it like an address book: one person → a family → a town → the town with its land and weather → the whole planet.

🍃 PART 2 — Feeding Relationships & Energy Flow

1. Producers, consumers and decomposers

Every organism has a feeding role. There are three main groups:

  • Producers (autotrophs) — green plants. They make their own food from sunlight by photosynthesis. They are the base of every food chain.
  • Consumers (heterotrophs) — animals that cannot make their own food, so they eat other organisms:
    • Primary consumers — herbivores that eat plants (e.g. a grasshopper, a cow).
    • Secondary consumers — carnivores that eat the herbivores (e.g. a lizard eating the grasshopper).
    • Tertiary consumers — carnivores that eat the secondary consumers (e.g. a hawk eating the lizard).
  • Decomposers — bacteria and fungi that break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to the soil.
🌟 Producers make food, consumers take food, decomposers break food (down again). Nothing is wasted.

2. Food chains and food webs

A food chain shows the flow of food (energy) from one organism to the next. It always begins with a producer (green plant) because only plants can capture the Sun's energy.

A simple food chain:

Grass → Grasshopper → Lizard → Hawk

(producer → primary consumer → secondary consumer → tertiary consumer). The arrows point in the direction the energy flows — from the food to the feeder.

In real life, most animals eat more than one kind of food, and most foods are eaten by more than one kind of animal. When many food chains link together we get a food web — a more realistic, branching picture of feeding in an ecosystem.

3. Trophic levels and the flow of energy

Each feeding stage in a food chain is a trophic level:

  • 1st trophic level — producers (green plants)
  • 2nd trophic level — primary consumers (herbivores)
  • 3rd trophic level — secondary consumers (carnivores)
  • 4th trophic level — tertiary consumers

Energy enters the chain as sunlight, is trapped by plants, and passes up the levels as one organism eats another. But energy is lost at every step (as heat, in movement, respiration and undigested waste).

The 10% rule
Only about 10% of energy passes to the next trophic level (≈ 90% is lost)

Because roughly 90% of the energy is lost at each step, there is little energy left after a few links. This is why food chains are short — usually only 3 to 5 trophic levels.

🌟 If plants trap 10 000 units, the herbivores get ≈ 1 000, the carnivores ≈ 100, and the top carnivores ≈ 10 — the energy shrinks tenfold each step.

4. Ecological pyramids

An ecological pyramid is a diagram that shows how a measured quantity changes from producers up to top consumers. There are three kinds:

PyramidWhat it measuresNote
Pyramid of numbersThe number of organisms at each levelCan sometimes be inverted (e.g. one big tree feeding many insects).
Pyramid of biomassThe total mass of living matter at each levelUsually upright; can occasionally be inverted.
Pyramid of energyThe energy stored at each levelAlways upright — energy always decreases upward and can never increase.
🌟 The pyramid of energy is the most reliable: because of the 10% rule, energy only goes DOWN as you climb, so it is never upside down.

♻️ PART 3 — Nutrient Cycles

1. The carbon cycle

Carbon moves round and round between the air, living things and the soil. Some processes remove carbon dioxide from the air; others return it.

Removing CO₂ from the air
  • Photosynthesis — green plants take in carbon dioxide and use it (with sunlight and water) to make food. This is the main way carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere.
Returning CO₂ to the air
  • Respiration — plants and animals release carbon dioxide as they breathe out / respire.
  • Combustion — burning wood and fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) releases carbon dioxide.
  • Decomposition — bacteria and fungi break down dead matter, giving off carbon dioxide.
🌟 One process takes CO₂ IN (photosynthesis); three put it BACK (respiration, combustion, decomposition). Feeding also passes carbon from plants to animals.

2. The nitrogen cycle

Plants need nitrogen to make proteins, but they cannot use nitrogen gas (N₂) from the air directly — it must first be turned into nitrates. The nitrogen cycle is the set of steps that do this and later return nitrogen to the air.

Nitrogen fixation

The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen gas into nitrogen compounds plants can use. It is done by:

  • Nitrogen-fixing bacteria — especially Rhizobium, which lives in the root nodules of leguminous plants (beans, groundnut, cowpea).
  • Lightning — its energy combines nitrogen and oxygen, which rain washes into the soil.
Nitrification

The conversion of ammonia → nitrites → nitrates by nitrifying bacteria in the soil (Nitrosomonas makes nitrites; Nitrobacter makes nitrates). Nitrates are the form plants absorb through their roots.

Denitrification

The conversion of nitrates back into nitrogen gas by denitrifying bacteria. This removes usable nitrogen from the soil and returns it to the air — the opposite of fixation.

Follow the loop: N₂ in air → (fixation) → ammonia → (nitrification) → nitrates → taken up by plants → passed to animals → death and decay return ammonia → (denitrification) → back to N₂ gas. Decomposers recycle nitrogen from dead bodies and waste.

3. The water cycle

Water is used again and again as it moves between the sea, the air, the land and living things:

  • Evaporation — the Sun heats seas, rivers and lakes, turning liquid water into water vapour (gas).
  • Transpiration — plants also lose water vapour from their leaves, adding to the vapour in the air.
  • Condensation — the rising vapour cools and turns back into tiny water droplets, forming clouds.
  • Precipitation — the droplets join and fall as rain (or snow), returning water to the land and sea.
  • Run-off — water flows over land into rivers and back to the sea, and the cycle repeats.
🌟 Two words do the heavy lifting: evaporation sends water UP (liquid → vapour); condensation brings it DOWN (vapour → liquid → rain).

🤝 PART 4 — Interactions & Ecological Factors

1. Symbiosis and other relationships

Symbiosis is a close living-together association between two organisms of different species. Depending on who gains and who loses, we recognise several types, plus two other important relationships (competition and predation):

RelationshipWho benefits?Example
MutualismBoth organisms benefitRhizobium bacteria and a legume; the bacteria get food and shelter, the plant gets nitrogen.
CommensalismOne benefits; the other is unaffectedAn epiphyte (small plant) growing on a big tree for support and light; the tree is unharmed.
ParasitismThe parasite benefits; the host is harmedA tapeworm in the gut, or Plasmodium (malaria) in a human.
CompetitionBoth may be harmed (they struggle for a shared resource)Two plants competing for light, water or minerals; lions competing for prey.
PredationPredator benefits; prey is killedA lion (predator) hunting and eating an antelope (prey).
🌟 Score it: mutualism = win–win; commensalism = win–neutral; parasitism = win–lose; competition = struggle for the same thing; predation = hunter and hunted.

2. Adaptation

An adaptation is a feature that helps an organism survive and reproduce in its environment. Adaptations can be:

  • Structural — body features (thick fur for cold, webbed feet for swimming, deep roots for finding water).
  • Behavioural — actions (birds migrating in a dry season, animals feeding at night to avoid heat).
  • Physiological — internal workings (a camel storing fat and losing little water in the desert).

Well-adapted organisms are more likely to survive in their habitat and pass on their features to their offspring.

3. Population factors

The size of a population is not fixed — it rises and falls depending on factors that add or remove individuals:

  • Factors that increase a population: births (natality) and immigration (individuals moving in).
  • Factors that decrease a population: deaths (mortality) and emigration (individuals moving out).
  • Limiting factors hold a population in check: shortage of food, water or space; disease; predators; competition; and climate.
🌟 Population change = (births + immigration) − (deaths + emigration). More in than out means the population grows.

4. Pollution and conservation

Pollution is the release of harmful substances into the environment, damaging living things. It comes in three main forms:

TypeCommon causesEffects
Air pollutionSmoke, exhaust fumes, factory gases (carbon dioxide, sulphur dioxide)Breathing diseases, acid rain, global warming.
Water pollutionSewage, oil spills, farm chemicals, industrial wasteKills fish and aquatic life; spreads water-borne disease.
Land pollutionRefuse dumps, plastics, pesticides, chemical wasteDamages the soil, harms crops and wildlife, spreads disease.

Conservation is the wise use and protection of natural resources so they are not destroyed and are available for the future. It includes:

  • Creating national parks and game reserves to protect wildlife.
  • Reforestation (planting trees) and controlled harvesting to avoid over-cutting.
  • Recycling and proper waste disposal to reduce pollution.
  • Laws against poaching, over-fishing and bush burning.
🌟 Pollution spoils the environment; conservation saves it. The two are opposites.
