🪞 PART 1 — Reflection & Mirrors

1. What is reflection? The two laws

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it hits a surface. A smooth, shiny surface (a mirror) bounces the rays back in an orderly way; this is regular reflection and it forms clear images. A rough surface scatters the rays in all directions — that is diffuse reflection, which is why you can see this page but not your face in it.

The two laws of reflection
  • 1st law: the angle of incidence (i) equals the angle of reflection (r). Both angles are measured from the normal (the line drawn at 90° to the surface), NOT from the surface itself.
  • 2nd law: the incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
Law of reflection:  angle of incidence i = angle of reflection r
🌟 Memory: light "bounces like a ball off a wall" — it leaves at the same angle it arrived, both measured from the normal.

2. Images in a plane (flat) mirror

Stand in front of a flat mirror and study your image. It has four fixed properties you must know:

  • Virtual — it appears to be behind the mirror and cannot be caught on a screen (no rays actually meet there; they only seem to come from there).
  • Upright (erect) — the same way up as you.
  • Same size as the object (magnification = 1).
  • Laterally inverted — left and right are swapped (raise your right hand, the image raises its "left"). This is why AMBULANCE is written back-to-front on the front of the vehicle.

The image is also formed as far behind the mirror as the object is in front, on a line perpendicular to the mirror.

🌟 Four words for a plane-mirror image: Virtual, Upright, Same-size, Laterally-inverted.

3. Curved mirrors — concave and convex

Curved (spherical) mirrors are cut from a sphere. There are two kinds:

  • Concave (converging) mirror — curves inward, like the inside of a spoon. It gathers parallel rays to a real focus, so it converges light.
  • Convex (diverging) mirror — curves outward, like the back of a spoon. It spreads parallel rays out so they seem to come from a focus behind it, so it diverges light.
Key words on a curved mirror
TermMeaning
Pole (P)The centre of the mirror surface
Centre of curvature (C)The centre of the sphere the mirror came from
Radius of curvature (R)Distance from the pole to C
Principal focus (F)Where rays parallel to the axis meet (concave) or seem to come from (convex)
Focal length (f)Distance from the pole to F
Focal length is half the radius:  f = R / 2
Quick example: a concave mirror of radius of curvature 40 cm has focal length
f = R / 2 = 40 / 2 = 20 cm

4. The mirror formula & the sign convention

To find where an image forms and how big it is, use the mirror formula together with magnification:

1/f = 1/u + 1/v
magnification  m = v / u = image height / object height
  • u = object distance from the mirror   v = image distance   f = focal length
Real-is-positive convention (use this throughout)
  • Distances to real objects and real images (in front of the mirror) are POSITIVE (+).
  • Distances to virtual images (behind the mirror) are NEGATIVE (−).
  • A concave mirror is converging → f is POSITIVE (+).
  • A convex mirror is diverging → f is NEGATIVE (−).

Read the sign of the answer: a positive v means a real image (in front); a negative v means a virtual image (behind).

🌟 Remember it as "real is positive": anything real and in front counts as +, anything virtual and behind counts as −.

5. Worked mirror calculations

Example 1 — concave, real image. An object is 30 cm from a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm. Find the image distance and magnification.
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/10 − 1/30 = 3/30 − 1/30 = 2/30 = 1/15
v = 15 cm (positive → REAL image, in front)
m = v / u = 15 / 30 = 0.5
The image is real, inverted and diminished (half size), 15 cm from the mirror.
Example 2 — concave used as a shaving/make-up mirror. Object 8 cm from a concave mirror, f = 12 cm (object inside the focus).
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/12 − 1/8 = 2/24 − 3/24 = −1/24
v = −24 cm (negative → VIRTUAL image, behind)
m = |v| / u = 24 / 8 = 3
The image is virtual, upright and magnified (3× bigger) — exactly why a concave mirror magnifies your face when held close.
Example 3 — convex mirror. Object 10 cm from a convex mirror of focal length 10 cm. For a convex mirror f is NEGATIVE, so f = −10 cm.
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = −1/10 − 1/10 = −2/10 = −1/5
v = −5 cm (negative → VIRTUAL, behind the mirror)
m = |v| / u = 5 / 10 = 0.5
The image is virtual, upright and diminished. A convex mirror ALWAYS does this — that wide, small view is why it is used as a car wing-mirror and a shop security mirror.

6. Nature of image in a concave mirror (summary)

Object positionImage formed
Beyond C (far away)Real, inverted, diminished — between F and C
At CReal, inverted, same size — at C
Between C and FReal, inverted, magnified — beyond C
At FNo image (rays come out parallel)
Between F and pole (inside focus)Virtual, upright, magnified — behind the mirror
🌟 A concave mirror can give BOTH real and virtual images depending on where the object sits. A convex mirror can only ever give a virtual, upright, diminished image.

🌊 PART 2 — Refraction of Light

1. What is refraction, and why does it happen?

Refraction is the change in direction (bending) of light as it passes from one transparent medium into another.

The cause is a change in the speed of light. Light travels fastest in a vacuum/air and slower in denser media like water or glass. When it slows down on entering a denser medium it bends; when it speeds up on leaving it bends back.

Which way does it bend?
  • Going from a less dense to a denser medium (air → glass): light slows down and bends TOWARDS the normal (i > r).
  • Going from a denser to a less dense medium (glass → air): light speeds up and bends AWAY from the normal (r > i).
  • A ray hitting the surface along the normal (i = 0°) goes straight through, un-bent.
🌟 Picture a car driving off tarmac onto sand at an angle: the wheel that hits the sand first slows, so the car swings towards the sand. Light "swings towards the normal" when it enters a slower (denser) medium.

2. The laws of refraction & refractive index

  • 1st law: the incident ray, the refracted ray and the normal all lie in the same plane.
  • 2nd law (Snell's law): the ratio sin i / sin r is a constant for a given pair of media. That constant is the refractive index (n).
n = sin i / sin r  (i in the first medium, r in the second)

Refractive index has no unit (it is a ratio). It tells you how strongly a medium bends light: air ≈ 1.00, water ≈ 1.33, glass ≈ 1.50, diamond ≈ 2.42. The bigger the n, the more the light slows and bends.

3. Three ways to write the refractive index

The same number n can be found three ways — pick whichever matches the data in the question:

n = sin i / sin r
n = real depth / apparent depth
n = speed of light in vacuum (c) / speed of light in medium (v)
  • Real vs apparent depth: refraction makes water look shallower than it really is, so real depth is always bigger than apparent depth (n > 1).
  • Speed form: because n = c / v, a large n means the light travels much slower in that medium.

4. Worked refractive-index calculations

Example 1 — from angles. A ray enters glass at an angle of incidence of 45° and refracts at 30°. (sin 45° = 0.71, sin 30° = 0.50.)
n = sin i / sin r = sin 45° / sin 30° = 0.71 / 0.50 = 1.41
The refractive index of the glass is about 1.41.
Example 2 — from depth. A coin at the bottom of a beaker of water is really 12 cm deep but appears to be 9 cm deep. Find n.
n = real depth / apparent depth = 12 / 9 = 1.33
The refractive index of the water is 1.33.
Example 3 — from speed. Light travels at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum and at 2 × 10⁸ m/s in glass. Find n.
n = c / v = (3 × 10⁸) / (2 × 10⁸) = 1.5
The refractive index of the glass is 1.5.
Example 4 — find a missing angle. Light hits glass (n = 1.5) at an angle of incidence of 30°. Find the angle of refraction. (sin 30° = 0.50.)
sin r = sin i / n = 0.50 / 1.5 = 0.333
r = sin⁻¹(0.333) ≈ 19.5°
The ray bends towards the normal (r < i, as expected going into a denser medium).

5. Everyday refraction effects

  • A straight stick dipped in water looks bent at the surface.
  • A swimming pool looks shallower than it truly is (real depth > apparent depth).
  • A coin in a bowl seems to rise when water is poured in.
  • Twinkling stars — starlight refracts through moving layers of air of different density.
🌟 All four are the SAME idea: light changes speed and bends as it crosses the water/air (or air/air) boundary, so objects appear shifted from where they really are.

🔦 PART 3 — Total Internal Reflection

1. Setting the scene

Send light from a denser medium (glass or water) towards a less dense one (air). The ray bends away from the normal, so the angle of refraction is bigger than the angle of incidence. As you increase the angle of incidence, the refracted ray bends more and more until it lies flat along the surface (r = 90°).

The angle of incidence that makes r = 90° is called the critical angle (C). Push the angle of incidence past C and the light can no longer escape — it is all reflected back inside the denser medium. This is total internal reflection (TIR).

2. Critical angle & the condition for TIR

Definition

The critical angle is the angle of incidence (in the denser medium) for which the angle of refraction in the less dense medium is exactly 90°.

Two conditions — BOTH must be true
  • Light must be going from a denser medium to a less dense medium (e.g. glass → air).
  • The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > C).
🌟 If either rule is broken, TIR cannot happen: light going into a denser medium always refracts, and below the critical angle it just escapes with partial reflection.

3. Linking n and the critical angle

At the critical angle, i = C in the denser medium and r = 90° in air. Snell's law gives the neat result:

n = 1 / sin C   (so sin C = 1 / n)

A bigger refractive index means a smaller critical angle — light is trapped more easily. Diamond (n = 2.42) has a tiny critical angle of about 24°, which is why diamonds sparkle: light bounces around inside before it can leave.

4. Worked critical-angle calculations

Example 1 — glass. Find the critical angle for glass of refractive index 1.5.
sin C = 1 / n = 1 / 1.5 = 0.667
C = sin⁻¹(0.667) ≈ 41.8° ≈ 42°
The critical angle of the glass is about 42°.
Example 2 — water. Water has refractive index 1.33. Find its critical angle.
sin C = 1 / 1.33 = 0.75
C = sin⁻¹(0.75) ≈ 48.6° ≈ 49°
The critical angle of water is about 49°.
Example 3 — find n from C. A liquid has a critical angle of 40°. Find its refractive index. (sin 40° = 0.643.)
n = 1 / sin C = 1 / 0.643 = 1.56
The refractive index of the liquid is about 1.56.

5. Applications of total internal reflection

  • Optical fibres: light signals bounce along a thin glass fibre by TIR at the walls, carrying phone/internet data and endoscope images over long distances with little loss.
  • Prisms in periscopes & binoculars: right-angled (45°) glass prisms turn light through 90° or 180° by TIR — sharper and brighter than ordinary silvered mirrors.
  • Mirage: on a hot day the air near the road is less dense than the air above; light from the sky bends and undergoes TIR in these air layers, making the road look wet.
  • Sparkle of diamonds and cut glass: the small critical angle traps light inside so it flashes out the top.
🌟 One idea, many uses: whenever light is "trapped and bounced" inside glass, water or air layers, think total internal reflection.

🔍 PART 4 — Lenses

1. The two types of lens

A lens is a shaped piece of glass or plastic that bends light by refraction to form images.

  • Converging (convex) lens — thicker at the centre than at the edges. It bends parallel rays together to a real principal focus. Focal length f is POSITIVE.
  • Diverging (concave) lens — thinner at the centre than at the edges. It spreads parallel rays apart so they seem to come from a virtual focus. Focal length f is NEGATIVE.

The principal focus (F) is where rays parallel to the principal axis meet (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave). The focal length (f) is the distance from the lens to F.

🌟 ConVex conVerges (comes together); a diverging lens spreads light apart.

2. The lens formula & magnification

Lenses use the same formula as mirrors and the same real-is-positive convention:

1/f = 1/u + 1/v
magnification  m = v / u = image height / object height
Sign convention (real-is-positive)
  • Real object and real image distances are POSITIVE (+); a virtual image gives a NEGATIVE (−) v.
  • Converging (convex) lens → f is POSITIVE (+).
  • Diverging (concave) lens → f is NEGATIVE (−).

3. Power of a lens

The power (P) of a lens measures how strongly it bends light. It is the reciprocal of the focal length in metres:

P = 1 / f  (f in metres, P in dioptres, D)
  • A short focal length means a strong lens (large power).
  • A converging lens has positive power; a diverging lens has negative power.
Quick example: a converging lens of focal length 50 cm (0.50 m) has power
P = 1 / f = 1 / 0.50 = +2.0 D

4. Worked lens calculations

Example 1 — converging lens, real image. An object is 15 cm from a converging lens of focal length 10 cm. Find the image distance and magnification.
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/10 − 1/15 = 3/30 − 2/30 = 1/30
v = 30 cm (positive → REAL image)
m = v / u = 30 / 15 = 2
The image is real, inverted and magnified (2× bigger) — the kind of image a projector throws on a screen.
Example 2 — converging lens as a magnifying glass. Object 6 cm from a converging lens of focal length 10 cm (object inside the focus).
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/10 − 1/6 = 3/30 − 5/30 = −2/30 = −1/15
v = −15 cm (negative → VIRTUAL image)
m = |v| / u = 15 / 6 = 2.5
The image is virtual, upright and magnified — exactly how a hand lens magnifies small print.
Example 3 — power. A converging lens has a focal length of 20 cm (0.20 m). Find its power.
P = 1 / f = 1 / 0.20 = +5.0 D
The lens has a power of +5.0 dioptres.
Example 4 — diverging lens power. A diverging lens has a focal length of 25 cm. For a diverging lens f is NEGATIVE, so f = −0.25 m.
P = 1 / f = 1 / (−0.25) = −4.0 D
The lens has a power of −4.0 dioptres (the minus sign shows it is diverging).

5. Images in a converging lens & uses

Object positionImage formedUse
Beyond 2F (far object)Real, inverted, diminishedCamera, eye
At 2FReal, inverted, same sizePhotocopier (1:1)
Between F and 2FReal, inverted, magnifiedProjector, film
At FNo image (rays emerge parallel)Searchlight/spotlight
Inside F (nearer than F)Virtual, upright, magnifiedMagnifying glass

Other uses of lenses: spectacles correct sight (convex lenses for long-sight/hypermetropia, concave lenses for short-sight/myopia), and compound lenses make microscopes and telescopes.

🌟 A converging lens gives a real image whenever the object is beyond F, but a virtual, magnified image once the object comes inside F — that inside-F position is the magnifying-glass mode.
