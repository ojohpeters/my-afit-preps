💧 PART 1 — Solutions & The Key Terms

1. Solute, solvent and solution

When you stir sugar into water, the sugar seems to disappear — it has dissolved. What you end up with is a clear, uniform liquid. Chemistry gives three names to what is happening:

  • Solute — the substance that is dissolved (usually the smaller amount). Example: the sugar, or the salt.
  • Solvent — the substance that does the dissolving (usually the larger amount, very often water). Example: the water.
  • Solution — the uniform (homogeneous) mixture you get when the solute has fully dissolved in the solvent.
Solute + Solvent → Solution

A solution looks the same all the way through — you cannot see the solute any more, and it will not settle out on standing. When water is the solvent, we call it an aqueous solution.

🌟 Memory: the sOLute is the little bit that gets lost inside; the sOLVent is the very big amount that swallows it (usually water).

2. Dilute vs concentrated

These two words tell us how much solute is dissolved compared with the amount of solvent:

  • Dilute solution — contains only a little solute in a large amount of solvent (weak). Example: a pinch of salt in a full glass of water.
  • Concentrated solution — contains a large amount of solute in the same amount of solvent (strong). Example: many spoons of salt in the same glass.

Notice these are relative, everyday words — they compare amounts but do not give an exact figure. To be exact we need the term solubility (coming up in lesson 4).

🌟 Think of squash/juice concentrate: add lots of water and it is dilute; use little water and it is concentrated.

3. Unsaturated, saturated and supersaturated

Keep adding solute to a fixed amount of solvent (at a fixed temperature) and eventually no more will dissolve. This gives us three important states:

The three states of a solution
StateWhat it meansCan it dissolve more?
UnsaturatedHolds less solute than its maximum at that temperatureYes — more will still dissolve
SaturatedHolds the maximum solute it possibly can at that temperature (extra just sits undissolved at the bottom)No — it is full
SupersaturatedHolds more dissolved solute than a normal saturated solution — an unstable, over-full stateNo — it is over-full and easily crystallises

Saturated solution — a solution that holds the maximum mass of solute it can dissolve at that temperature, with any undissolved solute in contact with it.

Supersaturated solution — made by dissolving a lot of solute in hot solvent then cooling it very gently so the extra stays dissolved. It is unstable: one tiny seed crystal or a shake makes the excess solute crash out at once.

🌟 Picture a bus: unsaturated = seats still free; saturated = every seat taken (full); supersaturated = people squeezed in past full — one push and they tumble out.

4. What is solubility?

Solubility puts an exact number on how much solute a solvent can hold.

Definition

The solubility of a solute is the maximum mass of it (in grams) that dissolves in 100 g of solvent (usually water) at a given temperature to form a saturated solution.

Solubility = grams of solute per 100 g of water (at a stated temperature)

Two things you must always state

  • The solvent — nearly always 100 g of water unless told otherwise.
  • The temperature — because solubility changes with temperature, a value is meaningless without it. Saying only that a solubility is 36 g is incomplete; you must say 36 g per 100 g of water at 25°C.
Units

Solubility is a mass compared to a fixed 100 g of solvent, so its unit is g per 100 g of water (grams of solute for every 100 g of solvent). It has no volume or time in it — it is an amount, not a rate.

🌟 Solubility is the exact number behind the word saturated: it is the most a 100 g dose of water can hold at that temperature.

🧮 PART 2 — Solubility Calculations

1. The master formula

Solubility is scaled to 100 g of water, so whatever mass of water you actually use, you convert to a per-100 g figure:

Solubility = (mass of solute ÷ mass of solvent) × 100

The answer comes out in g per 100 g of water. Rearranged, the same formula lets you find any one quantity if you know the others:

Mass of solute = (solubility ÷ 100) × mass of solvent
🌟 Golden habit: always check whether the water given is 100 g. If it is not, use the formula to scale up or down to 100 g.

2. Worked example — find the solubility

Example 1: 40 g of potassium nitrate just dissolves in 80 g of water to make a saturated solution at 40°C. Find its solubility.
Solubility = (mass of solute ÷ mass of solvent) × 100
Solubility = (40 ÷ 80) × 100 = 0.5 × 100 = 50 g per 100 g water
So the solubility at 40°C is 50 g per 100 g of water.
Example 2: 18 g of a salt saturates 75 g of water. Find the solubility.
Solubility = (18 ÷ 75) × 100 = 0.24 × 100 = 24 g per 100 g water
Answer = 24 g per 100 g of water.

3. Worked example — find the mass of solute

If you already know the solubility, you can work out how much solute a different mass of water will hold.

Example 3: The solubility of a salt is 36 g per 100 g of water at 25°C. What mass will saturate 250 g of water at the same temperature?
Mass of solute = (solubility ÷ 100) × mass of water
= (36 ÷ 100) × 250 = 0.36 × 250 = 90 g
So 90 g of the salt saturates 250 g of water.
Example 4: Solubility is 20 g per 100 g water. What mass dissolves in 25 g of water?
= (20 ÷ 100) × 25 = 0.20 × 25 = 5 g
Answer = 5 g.

4. Worked example — crystals that separate on cooling

Most solids dissolve more in hot water than in cold. So when a hot saturated solution is cooled, the water can no longer hold all the solute, and the excess crystallises out. The trick:

Crystals deposited = (mass dissolved at high temp) − (mass dissolved at low temp)
Example 5 (per 100 g water): A saturated solution made with 100 g of water holds 110 g of salt at 60°C. The solubility falls to 30 g per 100 g water at 20°C. How much crystallises out on cooling from 60°C to 20°C?
At 60°C, dissolved = 110 g (in 100 g water)
At 20°C, dissolved = 30 g (in 100 g water)
Crystals = 110 − 30 = 80 g
So 80 g of crystals separate out.
Example 6 (water is NOT 100 g): A salt has solubility 60 g/100 g water at 80°C and 20 g/100 g water at 30°C. A saturated solution was made with only 50 g of water. Find the crystals deposited on cooling from 80°C to 30°C.
At 80°C: mass dissolved = (60 ÷ 100) × 50 = 30 g
At 30°C: mass dissolved = (20 ÷ 100) × 50 = 10 g
Crystals = 30 − 10 = 20 g
So 20 g of crystals separate. (Always scale each solubility to the actual mass of water first.)

5. Molar solubility (a quick look)

Sometimes solubility is given in moles per dm³ instead of grams per 100 g. This is called molar solubility. Convert grams to moles using moles = mass ÷ molar mass:

Molar solubility (mol/dm³) = solubility in g/dm³ ÷ molar mass (g/mol)
Example 7: A salt dissolves to 20 g in 1 dm³ of water and has a molar mass of 40 g/mol. Find its molar solubility.
Molar solubility = 20 ÷ 40 = 0.5 mol/dm³
Answer = 0.5 mol/dm³.
🌟 Grams answer how HEAVY; moles answer how MANY particles. Divide grams by molar mass to switch from mass to moles.

📈 PART 3 — Solubility Curves & Temperature

1. What a solubility curve shows

A solubility curve is a graph of solubility (y-axis) against temperature (x-axis) for a particular substance. Each point on the line tells you the maximum grams of solute that 100 g of water can hold at that temperature.

  • Any point on the curve = a saturated solution at that temperature.
  • Any point below the curve = an unsaturated solution (it could still dissolve more).
  • Any point above the curve = a supersaturated (unstable) solution.
🌟 Read a curve like a map: go up from the temperature on the bottom axis until you hit the line, then across to read the solubility on the side axis.

2. Solids — solubility rises with temperature

For most solid solutes (like KNO₃, sugar, most salts), solubility increases as temperature increases — hot water dissolves more. Their curves slope upward to the right.

Most solids: temperature ↑ ⇒ solubility ↑

A few solids barely change (e.g. common salt, NaCl, is almost flat), and a very small number decrease — but the general rule for solids is an upward slope. This rising behaviour is exactly why cooling a hot saturated solution forces crystals out.

3. Gases — the opposite behaviour

Gases behave the reverse of solids:

  • Temperature: the solubility of a gas decreases as temperature rises — warm water holds less dissolved gas, so its curve slopes downward. (That is why a warm fizzy drink loses its gas fast, and why warm rivers hold less dissolved oxygen for fish.)
  • Pressure: the solubility of a gas increases with pressure — the more you press the gas onto the liquid, the more dissolves. (Fizzy drinks are bottled under high CO₂ pressure.)
Gases: temperature ↑ ⇒ solubility ↓ · pressure ↑ ⇒ solubility ↑
🌟 Remember: heat drives gas OUT of water, pressure pushes gas IN. Solids do the opposite of gases with temperature.

4. Reading a curve to find crystals deposited

The most common exam use of a curve is: read the solubility at the higher temperature, read it again at the lower temperature, and subtract to get the crystals that separate on cooling.

Worked example: From the curve, potassium nitrate has a solubility of 100 g/100 g water at 60°C and 40 g/100 g water at 20°C. A saturated solution is made with 100 g of water at 60°C, then cooled to 20°C. How many grams of crystals form?
Solubility read at 60°C = 100 g per 100 g water
Solubility read at 20°C = 40 g per 100 g water
Crystals = 100 − 40 = 60 g
So 60 g of KNO₃ crystals are deposited. (If the water were not 100 g, scale each value to the actual mass of water first, as in Part 2 Example 6.)

⚗️ PART 4 — Factors Affecting Solubility & Applications

1. Temperature

Temperature is the biggest single factor:

  • Solids: higher temperature usually means higher solubility.
  • Gases: higher temperature means lower solubility.

This is why sugar dissolves faster and in greater amount in hot tea, and why a warm soft drink goes flat quickly.

2. Pressure (for gases only)

Pressure has almost no effect on the solubility of solids and liquids, but a big effect on gases: more pressure ⇒ more gas dissolves. Fizzy drinks are sealed under high CO₂ pressure so plenty of gas stays dissolved; opening the bottle drops the pressure, so the gas fizzes out.

More pressure on a gas ⇒ more gas dissolves

3. Nature of solute and solvent — ‘like dissolves like’

Whether something dissolves depends on how well the solute and solvent match in structure. The rule is ‘like dissolves like’:

  • Polar / ionic solutes (salt NaCl, sugar, copper(II) sulfate) dissolve well in polar solvents like water — the polar water molecules pull the ions apart and surround them.
  • Non-polar solutes (oil, fat, wax, iodine) dissolve in non-polar / organic solvents (petrol, kerosene, tetrachloromethane, ethanol) but hardly at all in water.

This is why oil and water do not mix, but oil dissolves easily in petrol.

🌟 ‘Like dissolves like’: polar with polar (salt in water), non-polar with non-polar (oil in petrol). Water and oil are opposites, so they refuse to mix.

4. Stirring and particle size — rate, not amount

Stirring the mixture and grinding the solute into a fine powder make it dissolve faster, but they do NOT change the maximum amount that can dissolve.

Very common exam trap
  • Stirring and smaller particle size (more surface area) and gentle warming speed up the rate of dissolving.
  • The final solubility (maximum amount) is fixed by the solute, the solvent and the temperature only.
🌟 Stirring changes how FAST it dissolves, not how MUCH. Only temperature (and, for gases, pressure) changes the amount.

5. Water of crystallisation

Some crystals contain a fixed number of water molecules chemically built into their structure — this is called water of crystallisation, and such crystals are hydrated.

  • Hydrated copper(II) sulfate: CuSO₄·5H₂O (blue crystals).
  • Heating drives off the water to give the white anhydrous salt CuSO₄; adding water turns it blue again.

This built-in water comes out of the saturated solution as it crystallises, which is why the crystals have a definite shape and composition.

6. Fractional crystallisation

Fractional crystallisation separates two dissolved salts that have different solubilities. A hot solution saturated with both is cooled slowly: the salt whose solubility falls most drops out (crystallises) first and can be filtered off, leaving the more soluble salt in solution.

It is used to purify salts and, industrially, to obtain pure substances from mixtures such as sea water or crude potassium salts.

🌟 Same idea as cooling a saturated solution to get crystals — but here two salts are present and the less soluble one leaves first.
