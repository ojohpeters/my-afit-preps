🫁 PART 1 — Breathing vs Respiration

1. The two words people confuse

In everyday English we say we “breathe” and we “respire” as if they mean the same thing. In biology they are two different processes, and exam questions love to test this difference.

The one-line difference
  • Breathing (external / physical process) = the physical movement of gases in and out of the body — taking in oxygen and giving out carbon dioxide. It is a mechanical action (muscles, ribs, lungs). No energy is released here.
  • Respiration (cellular / chemical process) = the release of energy from food (glucose) inside living cells. It is a chemical reaction that happens in the mitochondria of every cell.
🌟 Memory: Breathing brings in the fuel and air; Respiration burns the fuel to release energy. Breathing is a pump; respiration is the engine.

2. What is respiration really doing?

Respiration is the breakdown of food substances (mainly glucose) in cells to release energy for life processes such as growth, movement, active transport, keeping warm and making new substances.

The energy released is not used directly — it is first stored in a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell’s energy currency, which then powers the cell’s work.

Where it happens

Most respiration happens in tiny structures inside the cell called mitochondria (often called the “powerhouse of the cell”). This is why very active cells, such as muscle and liver cells, contain many mitochondria.

3. Aerobic respiration — the full, energy-rich kind

Aerobic respiration is respiration that takes place in the presence of oxygen. It completely breaks down glucose and releases a large amount of energy (about 38 ATP per glucose molecule).

The word equation (learn this!)
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy

In words: glucose reacts with oxygen to give out carbon dioxide, water and a lot of energy. This is the process happening in your cells right now.

🌟 Notice the products: carbon dioxide and water — the same waste gases and vapour you breathe out. That is the link between respiration (in cells) and breathing (in lungs).

4. Anaerobic respiration — the emergency, low-energy kind

Anaerobic respiration is respiration that takes place without oxygen. Glucose is only partly broken down, so it releases much less energy (only about 2 ATP per glucose) and leaves behind an incompletely broken-down product.

In animal muscle (hard exercise)
Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy (a little)

During vigorous exercise oxygen cannot reach the muscles fast enough, so they respire anaerobically and build up lactic acid. This causes muscle fatigue and cramp, and creates an oxygen debt that is repaid by panting after exercise.

In yeast and some microbes (fermentation)
Glucose → Ethanol (alcohol) + Carbon dioxide + Energy (a little)

This is alcoholic fermentation — the basis of brewing and baking (the carbon dioxide makes bread rise).

🐟 PART 2 — Respiratory Organs Across Organisms

1. Different organisms, different organs

All living things must exchange gases, but they do it through different structures depending on their size, body plan and habitat. The structure used is called the respiratory (gaseous exchange) surface.

Organism → respiratory organ (master table)
Respiratory organ / surfaceOrganisms that use itNote
Body (general) surfaceAmoeba, Hydra, earthworm, flatwormGases diffuse straight across the moist skin/membrane; works only for small or thin bodies
GillsFish, tadpoles, crustaceans (crab, prawn)Feathery organs rich in blood; extract dissolved oxygen from water
Tracheae & spiraclesInsects (grasshopper, cockroach, housefly)Air enters tiny holes called spiracles and travels through branching air tubes (tracheae) directly to the tissues
LungsMammals, birds, reptiles, adult amphibians (frog, toad)Internal, moist, folded air sacs; the organ of land vertebrates
Stomata & lenticelsGreen plantsStomata (tiny pores on leaves) and lenticels (openings on woody stems) let gases diffuse in and out
🌟 Exam trap: a frog uses gills as a tadpole but lungs (and moist skin) as an adult. Read the stage of life the question is asking about.

2. Breathing through the body surface

Very small or thin-bodied animals (amoeba, earthworm) have no special organs. Oxygen simply diffuses across the moist body surface into the cells, and carbon dioxide diffuses out. The earthworm keeps its skin moist with mucus so gases can dissolve and cross.

This works only because these animals are small, so no cell is far from the surface, and their surface-area-to-volume ratio is high.

3. Gills, tracheae and lungs

  • Gills (fish): water flows over thin, blood-filled gill filaments. Dissolved oxygen diffuses from the water into the blood, and carbon dioxide passes out into the water.
  • Tracheae (insects): air enters through spiracles along the body and is piped straight to the cells through fine tubes called tracheae — the blood does not carry the oxygen.
  • Lungs (land vertebrates): air is drawn into internal, moist, folded sacs where oxygen enters the blood. Keeping the surface inside the body stops it from drying out on land.

4. Features of a good respiratory surface

Whatever the organ, an efficient gaseous exchange surface shares the same four features:

  • Large surface area — more room for gases to cross at once (alveoli, gill filaments and folded surfaces achieve this).
  • Thin (one or few cells thick) — so gases have only a very short distance to diffuse across.
  • Moist — gases must dissolve first before they can diffuse across the membrane.
  • Well supplied with blood (in blood-based systems) — a rich network of capillaries carries gases away quickly, keeping a steep diffusion gradient.
🌟 Memory word — the surface must be L-T-M-B: Large, Thin, Moist, well supplied with Blood.

👤 PART 3 — The Human Respiratory System

1. The pathway of air — from nose to alveoli

When you breathe in, air follows one fixed route down into the lungs. Learn this order exactly:

Nostrils → Nasal cavity → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli
  • Nostrils & nasal cavity: air is warmed, moistened and filtered. Hairs and mucus trap dust; a good reason to breathe through the nose, not the mouth.
  • Pharynx (throat): a shared passage for air and food.
  • Larynx (voice box): contains the vocal cords; the epiglottis is a flap that covers it during swallowing so food goes down the gullet, not the windpipe.
  • Trachea (windpipe): held open by C-shaped rings of cartilage; lined with cilia and mucus that sweep dust back up.
  • Bronchi: the trachea divides into two bronchi, one entering each lung.
  • Bronchioles: the bronchi branch into finer and finer tubes.
  • Alveoli: tiny air sacs at the very end where gaseous exchange actually takes place.
🌟 Picture an upside-down tree: the trachea is the trunk, the bronchi are big branches, bronchioles are twigs, and the alveoli are the leaves.

2. The breathing muscles — diaphragm & intercostals

The lungs cannot move by themselves; they are worked by two sets of muscles that change the size of the chest (thoracic cavity):

  • Diaphragm: a large dome-shaped sheet of muscle that separates the thorax (chest) from the abdomen. When it contracts it flattens and moves down, enlarging the chest.
  • Intercostal muscles: muscles between the ribs. When the external intercostals contract, the ribs move up and out, enlarging the chest.

The lungs themselves are protected by the ribs (rib cage) and are covered by thin pleural membranes filled with fluid that let them slide smoothly as they inflate and deflate.

3. The alveoli — the site of gaseous exchange

The alveoli are millions of tiny, thin-walled air sacs. This is the only place where oxygen actually enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves it. Their design is a perfect example of the four features of a good respiratory surface:

How the alveoli are adapted
AdaptationWhy it helps
Very many, giving a huge surface areaMillions of alveoli give a large area for gases to cross quickly
Extremely thin walls (one cell thick)Gases diffuse across a very short distance
Moist inner liningOxygen dissolves in the film of moisture before diffusing in
Surrounded by a dense network of capillariesBlood carries gases away fast, keeping a steep concentration gradient
🌟 Remember: the alveolus is where gas exchange happens — not the trachea, not the bronchi. Those are just delivery pipes.

💨 PART 4 — Breathing Mechanism & Gas Exchange

1. Inhalation vs Exhalation — the muscle movements

Breathing works on a simple pressure rule: gas moves from high pressure to low pressure. When the chest gets bigger the pressure inside drops and air rushes IN; when the chest gets smaller the pressure rises and air is pushed OUT.

StepInhalation (breathing IN)Exhalation (breathing OUT)
DiaphragmContracts — flattens and moves downRelaxes — domes and moves up
External intercostal musclesContractRelax
RibsMove up and outMove down and in
Volume of thorax (chest)IncreasesDecreases
Pressure in thoraxDecreases (below outside air)Increases (above outside air)
Air movementAir rushes IN to the lungsAir is forced OUT of the lungs
🌟 Key fact examiners love: during inhalation the diaphragm contracts and flattens (it does NOT relax or dome up). Bigger box → lower pressure → air in.

2. Gaseous exchange in the alveoli

At the alveoli, the two gases swap places by diffusion (each gas moves from where it is more concentrated to where it is less concentrated):

  • Oxygen (O₂) is high in the fresh air of the alveolus and low in the blood, so it diffuses IN — from alveolus into the blood, where it binds to haemoglobin in red blood cells.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is high in the blood returning from the body and low in the alveolus, so it diffuses OUT — from blood into the alveolus to be breathed away.
O₂: alveolus → blood   |   CO₂: blood → alveolus

No energy is used for this exchange — it is pure diffusion, which is why the thin, moist, well-supplied alveolar wall matters so much.

3. Composition of inhaled vs exhaled air

Comparing the air you breathe in with the air you breathe out shows what your body has taken and added:

GasInhaled (in) airExhaled (out) air
OxygenAbout 21%About 16% (less — some was absorbed)
Carbon dioxideAbout 0.04%About 4% (much more — added by respiration)
NitrogenAbout 78%About 78% (unchanged)
Water vapourVariable / lowHigher (air is saturated with moisture)
TemperatureCoolerWarmer (body heat added)
🌟 Summary: exhaled air has less oxygen, more carbon dioxide, more water vapour and is warmer. Nitrogen does not change.

4. Respiratory diseases (brief)

  • Asthma: the bronchioles narrow and become inflamed, making breathing difficult with wheezing and shortness of breath. Often triggered by dust, pollen, cold air or exercise; relieved with an inhaler.
  • Tuberculosis (TB): a bacterial disease (caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis) that mainly damages the lungs, giving a persistent cough, chest pain and weight loss. It spreads through droplets in the air.
  • Effects of smoking: tobacco smoke damages the lungs and airways. Tar paralyses and destroys the cilia (so mucus and dirt build up), causing smoker’s cough, bronchitis and emphysema (destroyed alveoli → less surface area). Smoke also contains carbon monoxide (reduces oxygen carried by blood) and cancer-causing chemicals that lead to lung cancer.
🌟 Link back to the alveoli: emphysema destroys alveolar walls, cutting the surface area for gas exchange — the smoker slowly loses the very feature that makes lungs work.
