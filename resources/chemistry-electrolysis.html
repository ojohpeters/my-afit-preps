⚡ PART 1 — Electrolytes & the Ionic Theory

1. What is an electrolyte?

An electrolyte is a substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in water, and is chemically decomposed (broken down) by the current.

Electrolytes are made of ions (charged particles). When they are molten or in solution, these ions are free to move and carry the current — and the current tears the compound apart.

Electrolytes vs non-electrolytes
  • Electrolytes (contain ions): acids (HCl, H₂SO₄), alkalis (NaOH, KOH), and salts (NaCl, CuSO₄) — either molten or in aqueous solution.
  • Non-electrolytes (no free ions): sugar (sucrose) solution, ethanol, petrol, pure water, and most molecular/covalent compounds. They dissolve as whole molecules, so nothing carries the current.
🌟 Memory: a metal wire conducts using electrons, but it is NOT an electrolyte because it is not decomposed. An electrolyte conducts using moving ions AND gets broken down.

2. Strong and weak electrolytes

How well an electrolyte conducts depends on how many ions it produces in solution.

TypeIonisationConductsExamples
Strong electrolyteCompletely (or almost completely) ionisedVery well (many ions)HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, NaOH, KOH, NaCl, CuSO₄
Weak electrolyteOnly partly ionised (most stays as molecules)Poorly (few ions)Ethanoic (acetic) acid CH₃COOH, carbonic acid H₂CO₃, ammonia solution NH₃(aq)
🌟 Idea: a strong electrolyte is like a class where everyone puts up their hand (all ionised); a weak one is a class where only a few hands go up (partly ionised).

3. The ionic theory — how conduction happens

Ions are charged particles: cations are positive (e.g. Na⁺, Cu²⁺, H⁺) and anions are negative (e.g. Cl⁻, OH⁻, SO₄²⁻).

  • As a solid the ions are locked in a fixed lattice — they cannot move — so a solid ionic compound does NOT conduct.
  • When molten or dissolved the ions break free and can move, so the substance conducts.
  • Inside the electrolyte the current is carried by the movement of ions, not electrons.
Cations (+) drift to the CATHODE · Anions (−) drift to the ANODE

4. Electrolysis and the electrolytic cell

Electrolysis is the chemical decomposition of an electrolyte by the passage of an electric current.

Parts of an electrolytic cell
  • A d.c. source (battery) that pushes the current.
  • Two electrodes dipped into the electrolyte — the anode and the cathode. They may be inert (platinum, carbon/graphite) or active (e.g. copper).
  • The electrolyte itself (molten or aqueous).
Anode vs cathode (learn this cold)
ElectrodeSign (electrolytic cell)ReactionIons attracted
AnodePositive (+)Oxidation (loss of e⁻)Anions (−)
CathodeNegative (−)Reduction (gain of e⁻)Cations (+)

Mnemonics: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain. AN OX (ANode = OXidation) and RED CAT (REDuction = CAThode). PANIC — Positive is Anode, Negative Is Cathode.

🌟 Warning: this "anode = +, cathode = −" is for an electrolytic cell (using a battery). In a galvanic/voltaic cell (which makes electricity, like the Daniell cell) the signs flip — anode is −, cathode is +. But in BOTH cells oxidation is always at the anode and reduction always at the cathode.

🧪 PART 2 — Electrolysis of Common Substances

1. Molten sodium chloride (no water)

When NaCl is molten, only Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are present (no water) — so there is no competition.

Cathode (−):  Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na   (molten sodium metal)
Anode (+):  2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻   (chlorine gas)

This is how sodium metal and chlorine are made industrially (the Downs cell).

2. Dilute / acidified water

Pure water barely conducts, so a little dilute H₂SO₄ is added to help — this is acidified water. Ions present: H⁺, OH⁻ and SO₄²⁻.

Cathode (−):  2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂   (hydrogen)
Anode (+):  4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻   (oxygen)
Overall, water is split:
2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
The SO₄²⁻ is not discharged, so effectively only water is used up and the acid gets more concentrated.
Volume ratio  H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1  (twice as much hydrogen as oxygen)

3. Brine — concentrated sodium chloride solution

Brine contains Na⁺, H⁺, Cl⁻ and OH⁻. Now there IS competition, and selective discharge decides the winners.

Cathode (−):  2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂  (H⁺ discharged — Na is too reactive)
Anode (+):  2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻  (Cl⁻ discharged — it is very concentrated)

Na⁺ and OH⁻ are left behind, so the solution becomes sodium hydroxide (NaOH).

🌟 Three useful products from brine: hydrogen (H₂), chlorine (Cl₂) and caustic soda (NaOH) — the basis of the chlor-alkali industry.

4. Copper(II) sulphate solution — inert vs copper electrodes

The electrodes you choose completely change the result. CuSO₄(aq) contains Cu²⁺, H⁺, SO₄²⁻ and OH⁻.

(a) Inert electrodes (platinum or carbon)

Cathode (−):  Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu  (reddish-brown copper deposited)
Anode (+):  4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻  (oxygen; SO₄²⁻ not discharged)

The blue colour fades as Cu²⁺ is removed, and the solution turns acidic (H₂SO₄ is left).

(b) Copper electrodes (active)

Cathode (−):  Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu  (copper deposited)
Anode (+):  Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻  (the copper anode dissolves)

Here the anode loses mass, the cathode gains mass, and the blue colour stays the same (Cu²⁺ is replaced as fast as it is used). This is the basis of electroplating and the purification of copper.

🎯 PART 3 — Preferential (Selective) Discharge

1. Why do we need selective discharge?

Many solutions contain more than one kind of cation and anion. But usually only one ion is discharged at each electrode — the one that is easiest to discharge. Choosing the winner is called preferential (selective) discharge.

2. The three factors that decide the winner

  • Position in the electrochemical / discharge series — the ion higher in ease of discharge is chosen. For cations, the less reactive metal ion (lower in the reactivity series) is discharged first.
  • Concentration — a very concentrated ion can be discharged in preference, even if the series does not favour it (e.g. concentrated Cl⁻ is discharged before OH⁻).
  • Nature of the electrode — an active electrode changes the product (e.g. a copper anode dissolves instead of giving oxygen; a mercury cathode lets Na⁺ discharge).

3. The discharge series (ease of discharge)

Cations at the cathode (easiest at the bottom)
K⁺ < Ca²⁺ < Na⁺ < Mg²⁺ < Al³⁺ < Zn²⁺ < Fe²⁺ < H⁺ < Cu²⁺ < Ag⁺ < Au³⁺

So Ag⁺ and Cu²⁺ are discharged before H⁺. But K⁺, Na⁺, Ca²⁺ are so hard to discharge that in a water solution H⁺ is discharged instead of them.

Anions at the anode (easiest at the bottom)
SO₄²⁻ < NO₃⁻ < Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻ < OH⁻

So OH⁻ (giving oxygen) is discharged before SO₄²⁻ or NO₃⁻; and I⁻ is discharged more readily than Cl⁻.

4. Worked predictions

(a) Dilute CuSO₄, inert electrodes: Cu²⁺ vs H⁺ at cathode → Cu (copper is below H⁺, easier). OH⁻ vs SO₄²⁻ at anode → O₂ (OH⁻ easier). Products: copper + oxygen.
(b) Dilute NaCl, inert electrodes: Na⁺ vs H⁺ → H₂ (Na too reactive). Cl⁻ vs OH⁻, dilute → O₂ (OH⁻ preferred when dilute). Products: hydrogen + oxygen.
(c) Concentrated NaCl (brine): cathode H₂; anode Cl₂ — the high concentration of Cl⁻ overrides the series. Products: hydrogen + chlorine (with NaOH left).
(d) Dilute H₂SO₄ (acidified water): cathode H₂; anode O₂ (SO₄²⁻ not discharged). Products: hydrogen + oxygen, ratio 2 : 1.
(e) Molten NaCl: no water, so no competition — cathode Na; anode Cl₂.
🌟 Rule of thumb: at the cathode the least reactive metal ion wins; at the anode the easiest anion wins — unless a rival anion is so concentrated that it takes over.

🔢 PART 4 — Faraday's Laws, Calculations & Uses

1. Charge, current and time

The amount of electricity (charge) that flows is given by:

Q = I × t   (Q in coulombs C · I in amperes A · t in seconds s)
Quick example: a current of 2 A flowing for 5 minutes.
t = 5 × 60 = 300 s
Q = I × t = 2 × 300 = 600 C
🌟 Always change minutes/hours to seconds before using Q = It.

2. Faraday's FIRST law

The mass of a substance deposited (or liberated) at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed.

m ∝ Q  (double the charge → double the mass)
The magic number
1 faraday (F) = 96500 C = the charge on 1 mole of electrons

How to find the mass deposited:

  • Step 1: Find the charge Q = It (in coulombs).
  • Step 2: Moles of electrons = Q ÷ 96500.
  • Step 3: Use the electrode half-equation to convert moles of electrons → moles of the metal.
  • Step 4: Mass = moles × molar mass.

3. Faraday's SECOND law

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses deposited are proportional to their chemical equivalents (equivalent mass = molar mass ÷ charge on the ion).

Why 1 faraday gives different masses:
Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag needs 1 e⁻ → 1 F deposits 1 mol Ag = 108 g
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu needs 2 e⁻ → 1 F deposits only ½ mol Cu = 32 g
Copper needs twice the electrons, so the same charge deposits half as many moles.

4. Worked calculations

Example 1 — find the charge. A current of 2 A flows for 30 minutes. How much charge passes?
t = 30 × 60 = 1800 s
Q = I × t = 2 × 1800 = 3600 C
Answer = 3600 C.
Example 2 — mass deposited. How much copper is deposited when 9650 C is passed through CuSO₄? (Cu = 64, Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu)
Moles of e⁻ = 9650 ÷ 96500 = 0.1 mol
Cu needs 2 e⁻ → moles Cu = 0.1 ÷ 2 = 0.05 mol
Mass = 0.05 × 64 = 3.2 g
Answer = 3.2 g.
Example 3 — find the time. How long will a current of 0.5 A take to deposit 1.08 g of silver? (Ag = 108, Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag)
Moles Ag = 1.08 ÷ 108 = 0.01 mol
Ag needs 1 e⁻ → charge = 0.01 × 96500 = 965 C
t = Q ÷ I = 965 ÷ 0.5 = 1930 s
Answer = 1930 s (about 32 minutes).
Example 4 — charge for 1 mole. How much charge is needed to deposit 1 mole of aluminium? (Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al)
1 mol Al needs 3 mol e⁻ = 3 faradays
Q = 3 × 96500 = 289500 C
Answer = 289500 C.

5. Applications of electrolysis

  • Electroplating: coating a cheap metal with a thin layer of a more attractive/expensive metal (e.g. silver-plating a spoon). The object is the cathode, the plating metal is the anode, and the electrolyte is a salt of the plating metal.
  • Purification of copper: the impure copper is the anode (it dissolves), a thin sheet of pure copper is the cathode (pure Cu deposits), in a CuSO₄ electrolyte. Impurities drop off as anode mud.
  • Extraction of reactive metals: very reactive metals cannot be reduced by carbon, so electrolysis of their molten compounds is used — e.g. sodium from molten NaCl, and aluminium from molten aluminium oxide (bauxite) dissolved in cryolite.

6. Electrochemical (galvanic) cells — the Daniell cell

A galvanic/voltaic cell does the opposite of electrolysis: it turns chemical energy into electrical energy (it produces a current).

The classic example is the Daniell cell: a zinc rod in ZnSO₄ solution joined by a salt bridge to a copper rod in CuSO₄ solution.

Anode (Zn, negative):  Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻  (oxidation)
Cathode (Cu, positive):  Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu  (reduction)

Electrons flow from Zn to Cu through the external wire.

e.m.f. of the cell

The e.m.f. is the driving voltage of the cell: e.m.f. = E°(cathode) − E°(anode).

Daniell cell e.m.f.:
e.m.f. = E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) − E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = (+0.34) − (−0.76) = 1.10 V
🌟 Trap: in a galvanic cell the anode is NEGATIVE and the cathode POSITIVE — the reverse of an electrolytic cell. But oxidation is still at the anode and reduction at the cathode.
