🌊 PART 1 — What a Wave Is & the Types

1. What is a wave?

A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium (or through space), transferring energy from one point to another without transferring matter.

The key idea: the energy moves along, but the material stays put. When a wave passes, each particle only vibrates about its own fixed position — it does not travel with the wave.

The three things a wave carries and does not carry
  • It transfers ENERGY — that is the whole point of a wave.
  • It transfers INFORMATION — e.g. sound and radio carry signals.
  • It does NOT transfer MATTER — the particles just wobble in place and return.
🌟 Picture a Mexican wave in a stadium: the "wave" runs right round the ground, but each person only stands up and sits down on the spot. Energy moves; the people do not.

2. Transverse waves

A transverse wave is one in which the particles of the medium vibrate at right angles (perpendicular) to the direction in which the wave travels.

A transverse wave has crests (the high points) and troughs (the low points).

Examples of transverse waves
  • Light and all electromagnetic waves (radio, X-rays, etc.).
  • Water waves / ripples on a pond.
  • A wave on a rope or string flicked up and down.
🌟 Flick one end of a rope up and down: the wave runs FORWARD along the rope, but every part of the rope moves UP and DOWN — across the direction of travel. That is transverse.

3. Longitudinal waves

A longitudinal wave is one in which the particles of the medium vibrate back and forth ALONG the same direction that the wave travels.

Instead of crests and troughs, a longitudinal wave has compressions and rarefactions:

  • Compression — a region where the particles are squeezed close together (high pressure).
  • Rarefaction — a region where the particles are spread far apart (low pressure).
Main example

Sound is the classic longitudinal wave. As a loudspeaker cone pushes forward it squeezes the air (compression); as it pulls back it stretches the air (rarefaction). These pressure changes race outward to your ear.

🌟 A slinky (spring) shows both: push it front-to-back and a squeeze travels ALONG it — longitudinal (like sound). Shake it side-to-side and the wave crosses it — transverse.

5. Mechanical vs electromagnetic waves

Waves are also grouped by whether they need a medium to travel through.

Mechanical waves
  • Need a material medium (solid, liquid or gas) — they cannot travel through empty space.
  • The medium's particles pass the vibration on from one to the next.
  • Examples: sound, water waves, waves on a string, seismic waves.
Electromagnetic (EM) waves
  • Need no medium — they can travel through a vacuum (empty space).
  • All are transverse and travel at the speed of light, 3 × 10⁸ m/s, in a vacuum.
  • Examples: light, radio, microwaves, infrared, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays.
🌟 Sound cannot travel through space (no air), but sunlight reaches us across the vacuum — that is the difference between mechanical (needs a medium) and EM (does not).

6. Progressive vs stationary waves

  • Progressive (travelling) wave: the wave moves along, carrying energy from one place to another — e.g. a ripple spreading across water.
  • Stationary (standing) wave: the wave pattern stays in one place and does not move along; it is formed when two identical waves travel in opposite directions and overlap — e.g. a plucked guitar string. (Full details in Part 4.)

📏 PART 2 — Wave Terms & the Wave Equation

1. The words you must know

  • Amplitude (A): the maximum displacement of a particle from its rest (middle) position. Measured in metres. Bigger amplitude = more energy.
  • Wavelength (λ): the distance for one complete wave — measured crest-to-crest, trough-to-trough, or compression-to-compression. Unit: metre (m). (λ is the Greek letter "lambda".)
  • Frequency (f): the number of complete waves passing a point each second. Unit: hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 wave per second.
  • Period (T): the time taken for one complete wave (one full oscillation). Unit: second (s).
  • Wave speed (v): how fast the wave (the energy) travels. Unit: metre per second (m/s).

2. Frequency and period are opposites

Frequency and period are the reciprocal of each other. If 5 waves pass each second (f = 5 Hz), then each wave takes 1/5 = 0.2 s (T = 0.2 s).

f = 1 / T   and   T = 1 / f
Worked example: A wave has a period of 0.02 s. Find its frequency.
f = 1 ÷ T = 1 ÷ 0.02
f = 50 Hz
So 50 complete waves pass every second.
Worked example (the other way): A tuning fork has a frequency of 250 Hz. Find its period.
T = 1 ÷ f = 1 ÷ 250
T = 0.004 s
Each vibration lasts 0.004 s.

3. The WAVE EQUATION: v = fλ

This is the single most important equation for waves. It links speed, frequency and wavelength:

v = f × λ   (speed = frequency × wavelength)

where v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

Rearranged versions you will need:

λ = v / f   ·   f = v / λ
🌟 Why it works: if f waves each of length λ pass every second, then the total length that passes each second is f × λ — and length per second IS the speed.

4. Worked examples on v = fλ

Example 1 — find the speed. A wave has a frequency of 50 Hz and a wavelength of 4 m. Find its speed.
v = f × λ = 50 × 4
v = 200 m/s
Answer = 200 m/s.
Example 2 — find the wavelength. A sound wave travels at 340 m/s and has a frequency of 170 Hz. Find its wavelength.
λ = v ÷ f = 340 ÷ 170
λ = 2 m
Answer = 2 m.
Example 3 — find the frequency. A wave of wavelength 0.5 m travels at 300 m/s. Find its frequency.
f = v ÷ λ = 300 ÷ 0.5
f = 600 Hz
Answer = 600 Hz.
Example 4 — combine with T. Water waves of wavelength 2 m pass a post every 0.25 s (period). Find the wave speed.
First f = 1 ÷ T = 1 ÷ 0.25 = 4 Hz
Then v = f × λ = 4 × 2 = 8 m/s
Answer = 8 m/s.

5. Wavefront and phase (briefly)

  • Wavefront: a line (or surface) joining all the points of a wave that are in step — for example, the line along the top of one ripple. Wavefronts are always at right angles to the direction the wave travels.
  • Phase: describes the stage a vibrating point has reached in its cycle. Two points are in phase if they move together (e.g. two neighbouring crests, one wavelength apart) and out of phase if one is up while the other is down.

🔊 PART 3 — How Waves Behave, Sound & the EM Spectrum

1. The four behaviours of waves

  • Reflection: a wave bounces back when it hits a barrier or surface. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. (An echo is reflected sound; a mirror reflects light.)
  • Refraction: a wave changes direction (bends) when it passes from one medium into another and its speed changes. (Light bends as it enters water, making a straw look bent.)
  • Diffraction: a wave spreads out as it passes through a narrow gap or around an obstacle. It is most noticeable when the gap is about the same size as the wavelength.
  • Interference: when two waves meet, they add together. Crest-on-crest gives a bigger wave (constructive); crest-on-trough cancels out (destructive).
🌟 Memory hook: Reflect = return (bounce back). Refract = re-route (bend into a new medium). Diffract = spread through a gap. Interfere = two waves interact.

2. Sound waves — the basics

  • Sound is a longitudinal, mechanical wave: it needs a medium and cannot travel through a vacuum.
  • Its speed depends on the medium — sound is fastest in solids, slower in liquids, slowest in gases. In air it is about 340 m/s.
  • Sound travels much slower than light — that is why you see lightning before you hear the thunder.

3. Echoes — reflected sound

An echo is sound reflected off a hard surface (a wall or cliff) and heard again. Because the sound travels to the surface and back, the total path is twice the distance (2d).

2d = v × t  →  d = (v × t) / 2

where d = distance to the surface, v = speed of sound, t = time for the echo to return.

Worked example. A man shouts at a cliff and hears the echo 3 s later. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, how far away is the cliff?
2d = v × t = 340 × 3 = 1020 m
d = 1020 ÷ 2 = 510 m
Answer = 510 m. (Remember to halve — the 1020 m is the there-and-back distance.)
Worked example — find the speed. An echo returns from a wall 85 m away in 0.5 s. Find the speed of sound.
Total path = 2d = 2 × 85 = 170 m
v = 2d ÷ t = 170 ÷ 0.5 = 340 m/s
Answer = 340 m/s.

5. The electromagnetic (EM) spectrum

The EM spectrum is the full family of electromagnetic waves, arranged in order. All of them are transverse and all travel at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum — they differ only in frequency and wavelength.

Order (longest wavelength / lowest frequency → shortest wavelength / highest frequency)
Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma
  • Radio — longest wavelength, lowest frequency (TV and radio broadcasting).
  • Microwave — cooking, mobile phones, satellite links.
  • Infrared — heat radiation, remote controls, night-vision.
  • Visible light — the only part our eyes can see (red → violet).
  • Ultraviolet — from the Sun; causes sunburn, used in sterilisation.
  • X-ray — sees through soft tissue to image bones.
  • Gamma — shortest wavelength, highest frequency; from nuclei; kills cancer cells.
🌟 Mnemonic for the order: Rich Men In Vans Use eXpensive Gadgets — Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-ray, Gamma. As you go right, wavelength gets SHORTER and frequency gets HIGHER.

🎵 PART 4 — Standing Waves, Resonance & Harmonics

1. What is a stationary (standing) wave?

A stationary wave is formed when two identical progressive waves travelling in opposite directions overlap (superpose). This usually happens when a wave reflects back on itself — for example on a plucked string or in an air column.

Unlike a travelling wave, the pattern stays in one place and does not move along — so no net energy is transported along it.

2. Nodes and antinodes

  • Node (N): a point that is always at rest — zero displacement. Here the two waves always cancel.
  • Antinode (A): a point that vibrates with maximum amplitude. Here the two waves always reinforce.

Nodes and antinodes alternate along the wave. Useful distances:

Node → next Node = λ / 2  ·  Node → next Antinode = λ / 4
🌟 On a skipping rope shaken at the right speed you see loops that stand still: the still points where the rope crosses are NODES; the fat middle of each loop is an ANTINODE.

3. Resonance

Resonance is when an object is made to vibrate at its own natural frequency by a driving force of the same frequency, giving a large-amplitude vibration.

It is why a wine glass can shatter at exactly the right note, why a swing goes higher when you push in time with it, and how musical instruments produce loud, clear notes.

4. Vibrating strings

A string fixed at both ends must have a node at each end. The simplest pattern — one loop, one antinode in the middle — is the fundamental (1st harmonic).

For the fundamental, the string length holds half a wavelength, so λ = 2L, giving:

Fundamental frequency:  f₁ = v / (2L)

Higher notes (harmonics) are whole-number multiples of this: fₙ = n × f₁ (n = 1, 2, 3 …). A string can produce all harmonics.

Worked example. A string of length 0.5 m carries waves of speed 200 m/s. Find its fundamental frequency.
f₁ = v ÷ (2L) = 200 ÷ (2 × 0.5)
f₁ = 200 ÷ 1 = 200 Hz
Answer = 200 Hz. (The 2nd harmonic would be 400 Hz, the 3rd 600 Hz, and so on.)

5. Air columns — open and closed pipes

Sound in a pipe forms a standing wave too. The rule: an open end is an antinode and a closed end is a node.

Open pipe (both ends open)
  • Antinode at both ends. Fundamental: f₁ = v / (2L).
  • Produces all harmonics (1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th …).
Closed pipe (one end closed)
  • Node at the closed end, antinode at the open end. Fundamental: f₁ = v / (4L).
  • Produces only the ODD harmonics (1st, 3rd, 5th …).
Worked example. A pipe closed at one end is 0.25 m long. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, find its fundamental frequency.
f₁ = v ÷ (4L) = 340 ÷ (4 × 0.25)
f₁ = 340 ÷ 1 = 340 Hz
Answer = 340 Hz. (Next note is the 3rd harmonic, 3 × 340 = 1020 Hz — even harmonics are missing.)
🌟 Remember: Closed pipe = Cut in half the frequency (v/4L, not v/2L) and only odd harmonics survive.
