🔥 PART 1 — Heat Capacity & Specific Heat Capacity

0. What this guide is about (and what it is NOT)

Heat is energy, and like all energy it is measured in joules (J). When a body takes in heat and does not change state, its temperature rises. This guide is all about one question: how much heat energy is involved? — i.e. how to measure and calculate heat. That subject is called calorimetry.

🌟 Scope note: temperature scales, thermal expansion and the three ways heat travels (conduction, convection, radiation) live in the separate Heat & Temperature guide. Here we focus only on counting the joules: heat capacity, mixtures, electrical heating and latent heat.

1. Why does temperature rise depend on more than just heat?

Add the same amount of heat to different things and they warm up by different amounts. Three things control the temperature rise:

  • How much heat you add (Q) — more heat, bigger rise.
  • The mass of the body (m) — a big pot of water warms more slowly than a cupful.
  • The material — water is very hard to heat; a metal warms up easily. This material property is the specific heat capacity.
🌟 Everyday clue: a metal spoon in hot soup gets scalding fast, but the water itself takes ages to boil. Same heat source — different specific heat capacities.

2. Specific heat capacity (c)

Specific heat capacity is the heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

c = Q ÷ (m × ΔT)   unit: J/(kg·K)

The word specific always means per kilogram. Because the mass is fixed at 1 kg, c depends only on the material — it is the same for 1 g or 1 tonne of the substance.

Values worth knowing
SubstanceSpecific heat capacity c (J/kg·K)
Water4200 (very high — used in this guide)
Ice≈ 2100
Aluminium900
Iron / steel460
Copper400
Lead≈ 130
🌟 Water's huge c (4200) is why the sea takes so long to warm up and cool down, and why water is used in car radiators and hot-water bottles — it stores a lot of heat per degree.

3. Heat capacity (C) — the WHOLE body

Heat capacity (capital C) is the heat needed to raise the temperature of the WHOLE body by 1 K, no matter its mass.

C = Q ÷ ΔT   unit: J/K

Since a body of mass m made of a material of specific heat capacity c needs m lots of c for every degree:

C = m × c
The difference in one line

🌟 Memory: specific = per kg. Drop the word "specific" and you scale up to the whole body: C = mc.

4. The master equation: Q = mcΔT

Put it together and you get the single most useful equation in calorimetry:

Q = m c ΔT   (heat = mass × specific heat capacity × temperature change)

It rearranges three ways — learn all of them:

c = Q ÷ (m ΔT) · m = Q ÷ (c ΔT) · ΔT = Q ÷ (m c)

Here ΔT = final temperature − starting temperature. Because it is a change, a difference of 1°C is exactly the same size as a difference of 1 K — so you may leave temperatures in °C in this equation.

5. Worked examples

Example 1 — find the heat. How much heat raises 2 kg of water from 20°C to 70°C? (c = 4200 J/kg·K)
ΔT = 70 − 20 = 50 K
Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × 50 = 420 000 J = 420 kJ
Answer = 420 000 J.
Example 2 — find the specific heat capacity. 46 000 J raises 2 kg of a metal by 50 K. Find c.
c = Q ÷ (m ΔT) = 46 000 ÷ (2 × 50)
c = 46 000 ÷ 100 = 460 J/kg·K
c = 460 J/kg·K — the metal is iron/steel.
Example 3 — find the mass. 84 000 J warms some water by 10 K. What mass of water is it? (c = 4200)
m = Q ÷ (c ΔT) = 84 000 ÷ (4200 × 10)
m = 84 000 ÷ 42 000 = 2 kg
Answer = 2 kg.
Example 4 — find the temperature rise. 9000 J is given to 0.5 kg of aluminium (c = 900). By how much does it warm?
ΔT = Q ÷ (m c) = 9000 ÷ (0.5 × 900)
ΔT = 9000 ÷ 450 = 20 K
Answer = a 20 K (20°C) rise.
Example 5 — using heat capacity C directly. A copper block has heat capacity 1200 J/K. How much heat raises its temperature by 25 K?
Q = C ΔT = 1200 × 25 = 30 000 J
(Check with C = mc: if C = 1200 and c = 400, the mass is m = C ÷ c = 3 kg.) Answer = 30 000 J.

🧪 PART 2 — Method of Mixtures & Calorimetry

1. The big principle

Mix a hot thing with a cold thing and heat flows from hot to cold until they reach the same final temperature. If none of that heat escapes to the surroundings, energy is conserved:

Heat LOST by the hot body = Heat GAINED by the cold body

This one sentence is the method of mixtures. Written with the master equation:

mhot chot (Thot − Tfinal) = mcold ccold (Tfinal − Tcold)

Notice each ΔT is written so that both sides come out positive: the hot body cools from its temperature down to the final one, the cold body warms from its temperature up to the final one.

2. The calorimeter

A calorimeter is the apparatus used to make these measurements. It is a thin, polished copper can holding the liquid, fitted with a stirrer and a thermometer, and sitting inside an insulating (lagging) jacket.

  • Why copper? It is a good conductor, so the can quickly reaches the same temperature as its contents and the thermometer reads the true value.
  • Why thin and polished? A small mass of metal absorbs little heat itself, and a shiny surface radiates less heat away.
  • The lagging (wool/felt/foam jacket) is insulation that cuts heat leaking to the room.
Precautions (to get an accurate answer)
  • Lag the calorimeter and keep it out of draughts — reduce heat loss to the surroundings.
  • Stir so the temperature is the same all through before you read it.
  • Use a lid to cut evaporation, which secretly carries heat away.
  • Transfer the hot body quickly so it does not cool on the way in.
  • Cooling correction: start a little below room temperature and finish a little above it — heat gained from the room early roughly cancels heat lost late.

3. Worked example — mixing hot and cold water

Example 1. 2 kg of water at 80°C is mixed with 3 kg of water at 20°C. Find the final temperature (assume no heat lost). Both are water, so c cancels.
Heat lost = heat gained
2 × (80 − T) = 3 × (T − 20)
160 − 2T = 3T − 60
160 + 60 = 5T → 220 = 5T → T = 44°C
Answer = 44°C. Check: hot loses 2×4200×36 = 302 400 J; cold gains 3×4200×24 = 302 400 J ✓.
Example 2. 100 g (0.1 kg) of water at 90°C is added to 200 g (0.2 kg) of water at 30°C. Final temperature?
0.1 × (90 − T) = 0.2 × (T − 30)
9 − 0.1T = 0.2T − 6 → 15 = 0.3T
T = 50°C
Answer = 50°C (nearer the cold water, because there is more of it).

4. Worked example — finding a metal's specific heat capacity

This is the classic experiment: heat a metal in boiling water (so its start temperature is 100°C), drop it quickly into cold water in a calorimeter, stir, and read the final temperature.

Example 3. A 0.6 kg metal block at 100°C is dropped into 0.5 kg of water at 20°C. The mixture settles at 30°C. Find the specific heat capacity of the metal. (cwater = 4200 J/kg·K)
Heat gained by water = 0.5 × 4200 × (30 − 20) = 21 000 J
Heat lost by metal = 0.6 × c × (100 − 30) = 42 c
42 c = 21 000 → c = 500 J/kg·K
Answer = c = 500 J/kg·K.
🌟 Method every time: write "heat lost = heat gained", fill in mcΔT on each side, and make each ΔT positive (hot: start − final; cold: final − start). Then solve for the one unknown.

⚡ PART 3 — Electrical Method of Measuring Heat

1. The idea — turn electricity into a known amount of heat

An electric (immersion) heater gives a very clean way to supply a measured amount of heat. The electrical energy delivered in time t is:

Q = P t = V I t = I² R t
  • P = power (watts), t = time (seconds)
  • V = voltage across the heater (volts), I = current through it (amps)
  • R = resistance of the heater (ohms)

All three forms give energy in joules when time is in seconds. (Remember: V/I is resistance, not power, so "(V/I)t" is not an energy formula.)

2. The energy balance

If the apparatus is well lagged so little heat escapes, all the electrical energy becomes heat in the substance:

Electrical energy in = Heat gained
V I t = m c ΔT

Rearrange to find the specific heat capacity by experiment:

c = V I t ÷ (m ΔT)   (also = Pt ÷ (m ΔT))
🌟 A voltmeter reads V, an ammeter reads I, a stopwatch reads t, a balance gives m and a thermometer gives ΔT — five simple readings hand you c.

3. Worked examples

Example 1 — find c from power. A 60 W heater warms 0.4 kg of a liquid; in 300 s the temperature rises by 15 K. Find c (ignore losses).
Q = P t = 60 × 300 = 18 000 J
c = Q ÷ (m ΔT) = 18 000 ÷ (0.4 × 15) = 18 000 ÷ 6
c = 3000 J/kg·K
Answer = 3000 J/kg·K.
Example 2 — find c from V, I, t. A heater runs at 12 V and 4 A for 600 s, warming a 2 kg block by 18 K. Find c.
Q = V I t = 12 × 4 × 600 = 28 800 J
c = 28 800 ÷ (2 × 18) = 28 800 ÷ 36 = 800 J/kg·K
Answer = 800 J/kg·K.
Example 3 — find the heating time. How long will a 700 W heater take to raise 1 kg of water (c = 4200) from 20°C to 100°C? (ignore losses)
Q = mcΔT = 1 × 4200 × 80 = 336 000 J
t = Q ÷ P = 336 000 ÷ 700 = 480 s = 8 minutes
Answer = 8 minutes.
Example 4 — using I²Rt. A heater of resistance 20 Ω carries 3 A for 240 s. How much heat is produced?
Q = I² R t = 3² × 20 × 240
Q = 9 × 20 × 240 = 43 200 J
Answer = 43 200 J.

4. Why is the electrical value often a bit TOO HIGH?

In real experiments a little heat always leaks to the surroundings. But we count all the electrical energy VIt as if it went into the substance. So VIt overestimates the heat that actually raised the temperature, and the calculated c comes out slightly too high.

That is why we lag the apparatus, use a lid, and take readings quickly — every precaution is aimed at making "electrical energy in = heat gained" as true as possible.

❄️ PART 4 — Latent Heat & Change of State

1. The strange flat part of the heating graph

Heat a block of ice steadily and watch the thermometer. The temperature climbs — but at 0°C it stops rising while the ice melts, even though heat is still pouring in. It climbs again once all the ice is water, then stops again at 100°C while the water boils. These flat parts are changes of state.

🌟 During a change of state you are still adding heat, but the temperature refuses to change. That "hidden" heat is called latent heat (Latin latent = hidden).

2. Why the temperature stays constant

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the molecules. During melting or boiling the incoming heat is used to break the bonds holding the molecules together and pull them apart — that raises their potential energy, not their kinetic energy. Since the kinetic energy is unchanged, the temperature stays constant until the change of state is complete.

3. Specific latent heat and Q = mL

Specific latent heat (L) is the heat needed to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without any change in temperature. There are two kinds:

  • Latent heat of fusion (Lf) — for melting a solid to liquid (or freezing back). For ice: Lf ≈ 336 000 J/kg (3.36 × 10⁵).
  • Latent heat of vaporisation (Lv) — for boiling a liquid to gas (or condensing back). For water: Lv ≈ 2 260 000 J/kg (2.26 × 10⁶).
Q = m L   (notice: NO ΔT — temperature does not change)

Unit of L is J/kg. Vaporisation needs far more energy than fusion because boiling must separate the molecules completely into a gas, while melting only loosens a solid into a liquid.

4. Worked examples

Example 1 — melting. How much heat melts 2 kg of ice at 0°C into water at 0°C? (Lf = 336 000 J/kg)
Q = m L = 2 × 336 000 = 672 000 J
Answer = 672 000 J.
Example 2 — boiling. How much heat turns 0.5 kg of water at 100°C into steam at 100°C? (Lv = 2 260 000 J/kg)
Q = m L = 0.5 × 2 260 000 = 1 130 000 J
Answer = 1 130 000 J (1.13 MJ).
Example 3 — combined (ice → warm water). Convert 1 kg of ice at 0°C into water at 20°C. (Lf = 336 000; cwater = 4200)
Step 1, melt the ice: Q₁ = mL = 1 × 336 000 = 336 000 J
Step 2, warm the water 0→20: Q₂ = mcΔT = 1 × 4200 × 20 = 84 000 J
Total = 336 000 + 84 000 = 420 000 J
Answer = 420 000 J.
Example 4 — full journey (ice → water → steam). Turn 0.1 kg of ice at 0°C into steam at 100°C. (Lf = 336 000; c = 4200; Lv = 2 260 000)
1. Melt ice: 0.1 × 336 000 = 33 600 J
2. Warm water 0→100: 0.1 × 4200 × 100 = 42 000 J
3. Boil to steam: 0.1 × 2 260 000 = 226 000 J
Total = 33 600 + 42 000 + 226 000 = 301 600 J
Answer ≈ 301 600 J (≈ 302 kJ). Notice step 3 dwarfs the others — boiling is the expensive step.

5. Evaporation causes cooling

Evaporation happens at the surface of a liquid at any temperature. The fastest, most energetic molecules escape first, so the average kinetic energy of the molecules left behind falls — the liquid cools down.

🌟 This is why sweating cools you, why you feel cold stepping out of a pool, and how a clay pot keeps water cool: evaporating liquid steals energy from what is left behind.
