🧬 The Basics — What Genetics Is All About

1. What is genetics?

Genetics is the branch of biology that studies heredity (how parents pass traits to their offspring) and variation (why offspring are not exactly identical to their parents or each other).

It explains questions like: "Why do I look like my parents?", "Why do some children have a feature neither parent shows?", and "Why can two brown-eyed parents have a child with blue eyes?"

🌟 Think of a gene as a tiny recipe instruction tucked inside your cells. Your parents each hand you a copy of the recipe book, and the combination of recipes you receive decides how you turn out.

2. Gregor Mendel — the Father of Genetics

Gregor Mendel was an Austrian monk who, in the 1860s, carried out careful breeding experiments on garden pea plants. By counting offspring over many generations, he discovered the basic rules of how traits are inherited.

He is called the Father of Genetics because he was the first to show that inheritance follows clear, predictable mathematical patterns — long before anyone knew DNA existed.

Why peas? Pea plants grow fast, have clear contrasting traits (tall/short, round/wrinkled seeds), and can be easily cross-pollinated or self-pollinated — perfect for counting results.

3. The key terms — taught simply

  • DNA — the chemical (a long molecule) that carries all the genetic instructions, like the "ink" the recipes are written in.
  • Chromosome — a long thread of DNA. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in each body cell. Think of it as a whole book of recipes.
  • Gene — a short section of DNA on a chromosome that controls one trait. Think of it as one recipe in the book (e.g. the recipe for "eye colour").
  • Allele — a different version/form of the same gene. E.g. the eye-colour gene has a "brown" allele and a "blue" allele. You get one allele from each parent.
  • Trait / Character — a visible feature controlled by genes (height, eye colour, ability to roll the tongue).
  • Genotype — the actual genes/alleles an organism carries, written as letters (e.g. Tt, TT, tt). This is the "hidden code".
  • Phenotype — the physical appearance the genes produce (e.g. "tall", "brown eyes"). This is what you actually see.
🌟 Genotype is the recipe written down; phenotype is the finished cake you can see and taste.

4. Dominant vs recessive

Dominant allele: shows up in the phenotype even when only ONE copy is present. Written with a CAPITAL letter (e.g. T).
Recessive allele: only shows up in the phenotype when BOTH copies are recessive (i.e. it is "masked" if a dominant allele is present). Written with a small letter (e.g. t).

So a plant with genotype Tt looks tall, because the dominant T masks the recessive t. Only tt is short.

Real-life examples in humans: Tongue-rolling is dominant (rollers carry at least one rolling allele); inability to roll is recessive. Free (unattached) earlobes are dominant; attached earlobes are recessive.

5. Homozygous (pure) vs heterozygous (hybrid)

  • Homozygous (= "pure-breeding") — both alleles are the same: TT (homozygous dominant) or tt (homozygous recessive).
  • Heterozygous (= "hybrid") — the two alleles are different: Tt. It carries one dominant and one recessive allele but looks dominant.
🌟 "Homo" = same, "hetero" = different. A Tt hybrid looks tall but secretly carries the short allele, which can reappear in its children.

6. F1 and F2 generations

  • Parental (P) generation — the original parents you cross.
  • F1 generation (First Filial) — the children of the P cross.
  • F2 generation (Second Filial) — the offspring you get when two F1 individuals are crossed (the "grandchildren").

Mendel noticed a trait could "disappear" in F1 and then reappear in F2 — that was the clue that recessive alleles are hidden, not lost.

⚖️ Mendel's Laws & Genetic Crosses

1. Mendel's First Law — the Law of Segregation

Law of Segregation: the two alleles for a trait separate (segregate) during the formation of sex cells (gametes), so each gamete carries only one allele of the pair.

In plain words: you have two copies of each gene, but each egg or sperm carries only one of them. When egg and sperm join, the offspring gets one allele from each parent — back to two copies.

🌟 Imagine each parent has a pair of socks (Tt). When making a gamete they put just ONE sock in the bag. The child opens one bag from mum and one from dad to make a new pair.

2. Mendel's Second Law — the Law of Independent Assortment

Law of Independent Assortment: alleles of different genes separate independently of one another during gamete formation — the way one pair sorts does not affect another.

So the allele you pass on for height has nothing to do with the allele you pass on for seed shape; they are dealt out separately. This is why we get new combinations of traits in offspring.

Example: a TtRr plant (tall + round) can make four kinds of gamete equally: TR, Tr, tR, tr — height and seed shape are mixed independently.

3. The monohybrid cross — worked fully (Tt × Tt)

A monohybrid cross follows ONE trait. Let's cross two heterozygous (hybrid) tall pea plants: Tt × Tt.

Step 1 — gametes: each parent (Tt) makes two kinds of gamete: T and t.

Step 2 — Punnett square: put one parent's gametes across the top, the other's down the side, then fill the boxes.

×Tt
TTTTt
tTttt

Step 3 — read the results:

  • Genotype ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt → 1 : 2 : 1
  • Phenotype ratio: 3 tall (TT, Tt, Tt) : 1 short (tt) → 3 : 1
Key result to memorise: crossing two heterozygotes (Tt × Tt) always gives a 3 : 1 phenotype ratio and a 1 : 2 : 1 genotype ratio. This is the classic Mendelian monohybrid result.

4. The P → F1 → F2 picture

Mendel crossed a pure tall plant with a pure short one:

  • P: TT (tall) × tt (short)
  • F1: all Tt → all tall (the short trait vanishes — masked).
  • F2 (Tt × Tt): tall and short reappear in a 3 : 1 ratio.
The big lesson: the short (recessive) trait was never lost in F1 — it was just hidden, and it came back in F2. That is how Mendel discovered recessive alleles.

5. The test cross (back cross)

Test cross: cross the unknown individual with a homozygous recessive (tt). The results reveal the unknown's hidden genotype.

A tall plant could be TT or Tt — you can't tell by looking. So cross it with a short plant (tt):

  • If the unknown is TT: TT × tt → all Tt (all tall). No short offspring.
  • If the unknown is Tt: Tt × tt → half tall, half short (1 : 1). Any short offspring proves it was Tt.

A back cross is when an offspring is crossed back with one of its parents; a test cross specifically uses the homozygous recessive parent.

6. The dihybrid cross (two traits) — the 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 ratio

A dihybrid cross follows TWO traits at once. When two double-heterozygotes are crossed (e.g. RrYy × RrYy for round/wrinkled and yellow/green seeds), the F2 phenotype ratio is the famous:

9 : 3 : 3 : 1 — 9 showing both dominant traits : 3 showing the first dominant + second recessive : 3 showing first recessive + second dominant : 1 showing both recessive traits.

This ratio is a direct consequence of the Law of Independent Assortment — the two traits are inherited independently.

🩸 Inheritance Patterns & Variation

1. Sex determination (XX female, XY male)

One pair of human chromosomes are the sex chromosomes. A female is XX and a male is XY.

  • The mother (XX) can only give an X in her egg.
  • The father (XY) gives either an X or a Y in his sperm.

So it is the father's sperm that decides the baby's sex. Cross XX × XY:

×X (egg)X (egg)
X (sperm)XX (girl)XX (girl)
Y (sperm)XY (boy)XY (boy)

Result: 2 XX : 2 XY → 50% female : 50% male (1 : 1) — a 50:50 chance each time.

2. Blood groups — multiple alleles & codominance

The ABO blood group is controlled by three alleles of one gene: IA, IB and IO (often written A, B and O). Because there are more than two possible alleles, this is called multiple alleles.

  • IA and IB are both dominant over O.
  • When IA and IB are together, both show — this is codominance, giving blood group AB.
  • O (OO) is recessive — group O shows only when both alleles are O.

Codominance vs dominance: in normal dominance one allele hides the other; in codominance both alleles show fully at once (group AB shows both A and B markers).

3. Sex-linked traits (carried on the X chromosome)

Sex-linked trait: a trait whose gene sits on a sex chromosome (usually the X). Because males have only one X, a single recessive allele shows up in them.

Common examples: red–green colour blindness and haemophilia (a blood-clotting disorder). Their alleles are recessive and carried on the X chromosome.

  • A male (XY) with the faulty allele on his single X is affected — he has no second X to mask it.
  • A female (XX) needs the faulty allele on both X chromosomes to be affected; with just one she is a carrier (unaffected but can pass it on).

This is why colour blindness and haemophilia are far more common in males than females.

4. Variation — continuous vs discontinuous

  • Continuous variation: traits that vary smoothly over a range, controlled by many genes (and influenced by environment). Examples: height, body weight, skin colour. There are no clear-cut groups — you can be any value in between.
  • Discontinuous variation: traits that fall into clear, separate categories with no in-betweens, usually controlled by one or few genes. Examples: blood group (A, B, AB, O), tongue-rolling (can/can't), sex (male/female), attached vs free earlobes.
🌟 Continuous = a smooth dimmer switch (any brightness). Discontinuous = an on/off switch (only fixed options).

5. Mutation

Mutation: a sudden change in the genetic material (DNA) of an organism. Mutations are a source of new variation and can be passed on if they occur in sex cells.
  • Gene (point) mutation: a change within a single gene (the DNA "letters" change).
  • Chromosomal mutation: a change in the structure or number of whole chromosomes (e.g. an extra chromosome causes Down syndrome).

Mutations can be caused by radiation, certain chemicals, and some viruses (these are called mutagens).

6. Sickle cell anaemia — a genetic disorder

Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a recessive gene mutation that makes red blood cells take a curved sickle (crescent) shape instead of round. Sickled cells block blood vessels and carry less oxygen.

  • HbA HbA — normal blood.
  • HbA HbS — carrier (sickle-cell trait), usually healthy, and partly protected against malaria.
  • HbS HbS — has sickle-cell anaemia (both recessive alleles).
Cross of two carriers (HbA HbS × HbA HbS): gives a 1 : 2 : 1 genotype ratio, so on average 1 in 4 (25%) of children can have sickle-cell anaemia — the same maths as any Tt × Tt cross.

7. Probability in genetics

Genetic ratios are really probabilities. From a Tt × Tt cross, each child has a 3/4 (75%) chance of the dominant phenotype and a 1/4 (25%) chance of the recessive one.

Important: probability resets each time. If two carriers already have one affected child, the next child still has the same 1 in 4 chance — the offspring do not "share out" exactly.

🌟 It's like tossing a coin: getting heads once doesn't change the odds for the next toss. Each child is an independent "toss".
