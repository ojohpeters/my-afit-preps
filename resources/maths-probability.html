🎲 PART 1 — Basic Probability

1. What is probability?

Probability is a number that tells you how likely an event is to happen. We work it out by counting.

Probability = (number of favourable outcomes) ÷ (total number of possible outcomes)

A favourable outcome is a result you are asking about. The total outcomes are all the equally likely results that could happen.

Quick example: A fair die has 6 faces (1,2,3,4,5,6). What is the probability of rolling a 3?
Favourable outcomes = 1 (only the face "3")
Total outcomes = 6
P(3) = 1/6

2. Probability always lies between 0 and 1

A probability can never be smaller than 0 or bigger than 1.

0 ≤ P(event) ≤ 1
  • P = 0 means the event is impossible (it can never happen). Example: rolling a 7 on an ordinary die.
  • P = 1 means the event is certain (it must happen). Example: rolling a number less than 7 on a die.
  • Everything real sits in between — the closer to 1, the more likely; the closer to 0, the less likely.
🌟 Memory: a probability is like a mark out of 1. You can never score more than "full marks" (1) and never less than "zero".

Note: probabilities can be written as a fraction (1/6), a decimal (0.5) or a percentage (50%). In exams the fraction form is usually cleanest — always reduce it to lowest terms.

3. The sample space

The sample space is the list of ALL possible outcomes of an experiment. Writing it out makes counting easy.

  • Tossing a coin: sample space = {Head, Tail} → 2 outcomes.
  • Rolling a die: sample space = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} → 6 outcomes.
  • Drawing one card from a pack: sample space has 52 outcomes (4 suits × 13 cards).
🌟 Tip: when a question confuses you, quickly write the full sample space and just count. Probability is mostly careful counting.

4. The complement — P(event) + P(not event) = 1

For any event, either it happens or it does not happen. These two cover everything, so their probabilities add up to 1.

P(event) + P(not event) = 1
P(not event) = 1 − P(event)

This is a huge time-saver: sometimes the "not" is far easier to count than the event itself.

Worked example: The probability of passing an exam is 3/4. What is the probability of failing?
P(fail) = 1 − P(pass) = 1 − 3/4 = 1/4
Worked example: A die is rolled. What is the probability of NOT getting a 6?
P(6) = 1/6, so P(not 6) = 1 − 1/6 = 5/6

5. Worked examples — dice, coins, cards and bags

Die — even number: P(even) on a die.
Even faces = {2, 4, 6} → 3 favourable outcomes out of 6
P(even) = 3/6 = 1/2
Coin — a tail: A fair coin has {Head, Tail}.
P(tail) = 1/2
Cards — an ace: A pack has 4 aces out of 52 cards.
P(ace) = 4/52 = 1/13
Balls in a bag: A bag has 4 red, 3 green and 5 yellow balls (total = 12). Find P(green).
Favourable (green) = 3, total = 4 + 3 + 5 = 12
P(green) = 3/12 = 1/4
Balls — the "not" trick: Same bag. Find P(not red).
P(red) = 4/12 = 1/3, so P(not red) = 1 − 1/3 = 2/3
Check by counting: not red = green + yellow = 3 + 5 = 8, and 8/12 = 2/3. ✓

➕ PART 2 — The Addition Rule (OR)

1. "OR" means ADD

The addition rule is used when a question asks for the probability of one event OR another happening. The word OR is your signal to add.

But there is one thing to check first: can the two events happen at the same time or not? That decides which formula to use.

2. Mutually exclusive events

Mutually exclusive events are events that cannot happen together in a single trial. If one happens, the other cannot.

  • Rolling a 2 OR a 5 on one die — a single roll cannot be both a 2 and a 5.
  • A card being a king OR a queen — one card cannot be both.
If mutually exclusive:  P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
Worked example: A die is rolled. Find P(2 or 5).
P(2) = 1/6, P(5) = 1/6
P(2 or 5) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3
Worked example: A bag has 4 red, 3 green and 2 blue balls (total = 9). Find P(green or blue).
P(green) = 3/9, P(blue) = 2/9
P(green or blue) = 3/9 + 2/9 = 5/9

3. Non-mutually-exclusive events (they can overlap)

Some events can happen together. If you just add, you would count the overlap twice — so you must subtract the overlap once.

If they can overlap:  P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)

P(A and B) is the probability that both happen at once — that is the overlap.

The classic example — king OR heart: A card is drawn from 52. Find P(king or heart).
P(king) = 4/52   (4 kings)
P(heart) = 13/52   (13 hearts)
P(king AND heart) = 1/52   (only the king of hearts is both)
P(king or heart) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13
If we had NOT subtracted, we would have counted the king of hearts twice.
Worked example: A die is rolled. Find P(even or greater than 3).
Even = {2,4,6} → 3/6;   >3 = {4,5,6} → 3/6
Overlap (even AND >3) = {4,6} → 2/6
P(even or >3) = 3/6 + 3/6 − 2/6 = 4/6 = 2/3
Check the union {2,4,5,6} = 4 outcomes → 4/6 = 2/3. ✓
🌟 Rule of thumb: if the two events share NO outcomes, just add. If they can both happen, add then subtract the shared part once.

✖️ PART 3 — The Multiplication Rule (AND) & Independence

1. "AND" means MULTIPLY

When a question asks for the probability that one event AND another both happen (often in a sequence — this then that), you multiply.

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)  (when A and B are independent)

The word AND is your signal to multiply. The word OR was your signal to add — keep them straight.

2. Independent events

Independent events are events where the outcome of one does NOT affect the outcome of the other. The second probability stays the same no matter what happened first.

  • Tossing a coin twice — the first toss does not change the second.
  • Rolling a die and tossing a coin — completely separate.
Worked example 1 — two coins: Find P(two heads).
P(head) = 1/2 each, and the tosses are independent
P(HH) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
Worked example 2 — die and coin: Find P(a 6 and a head).
P(6) = 1/6, P(head) = 1/2
P(6 and head) = 1/6 × 1/2 = 1/12

3. With replacement vs WITHOUT replacement

This is a very common exam trap with balls in a bag. It decides whether the events are independent.

WITH replacementWITHOUT replacement
What you doPut the first ball back before the second drawKeep the first ball out — do not return it
Total for 2nd drawSame as before (unchanged)One less than before
Type of eventsIndependent — probabilities do not changeDependent — probabilities change on the 2nd draw
Worked example 3 — WITHOUT replacement: A bag has 5 red and 3 white balls (total = 8). Two balls are drawn one after another without replacement. Find P(both red).
1st draw: P(red) = 5/8
2nd draw: one red is gone, so 4 reds remain out of 7 → P(red) = 4/7
P(both red) = 5/8 × 4/7 = 20/56 = 5/14
Notice the second denominator became 7 (not 8) because a ball was removed.
Compare — WITH replacement: same bag, but the first ball is put back.
Both draws stay at 5/8 → P(both red) = 5/8 × 5/8 = 25/64
Different answer! Always check whether the ball is replaced.

4. Tree diagrams — the idea

A tree diagram draws each stage as branches. You multiply ALONG the branches (that is the AND) to get the probability of a path, and you add DOWN the finished paths (that is the OR) when several paths satisfy the question.

🌟 Golden pair: multiply along a branch (AND), add between branches (OR). Every tree question is just these two moves.

5. The "at least one" trick — use the complement

"At least one" means "one or more". Counting all those cases is slow. It is far quicker to find the probability of the opposite (none at all) and subtract from 1.

P(at least one) = 1 − P(none)
Worked example 4 — at least one head: A coin is tossed twice. Find P(at least one head).
P(no heads) = P(tail) × P(tail) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
P(at least one head) = 1 − 1/4 = 3/4
Another one — at least one six: A die is rolled twice. Find P(at least one six).
P(no six on a roll) = 5/6, so P(no six twice) = 5/6 × 5/6 = 25/36
P(at least one six) = 1 − 25/36 = 11/36

🧩 PART 4 — Exam-Style Problems (fully worked)

1. "Both" — coloured balls without replacement

Problem: A box contains 6 red and 4 blue balls. Two balls are picked at random without replacement. Find the probability that both are red.
Total = 6 + 4 = 10
1st red: 6/10   ;   2nd red (5 reds left of 9): 5/9
P(both red) = 6/10 × 5/9 = 30/90 = 1/3
Answer = 1/3.

2. "At least one" using the complement

Problem: A box contains 6 red and 4 blue balls. Two balls are picked without replacement. Find the probability that at least one is blue.
Opposite of "at least one blue" is "no blue" = both red
P(both red) = 6/10 × 5/9 = 1/3  (from Problem 1)
P(at least one blue) = 1 − 1/3 = 2/3
Answer = 2/3. Doing it the long way (BB + BR + RB) gives the same result but takes longer.

3. "One of each" — add the two orders

Problem: A bag has 3 white and 2 black balls. Two are drawn without replacement. Find the probability of getting one white and one black (in any order).
Total = 5. Two possible orders: white-then-black, OR black-then-white
P(WB) = 3/5 × 2/4 = 6/20
P(BW) = 2/5 × 3/4 = 6/20
P(one of each) = 6/20 + 6/20 = 12/20 = 3/5
Answer = 3/5. (Multiply along each order = AND; add the two orders = OR.)

4. "Neither" — both events fail

Problem: The probability that Ada passes maths is 0.7 and that she passes English is 0.6. The results are independent. Find the probability that she passes neither subject.
P(fail maths) = 1 − 0.7 = 0.3
P(fail English) = 1 − 0.6 = 0.4
P(neither) = P(fail maths) × P(fail English) = 0.3 × 0.4 = 0.12
Answer = 0.12.

5. Two dice — working with sums

Problem: Two fair dice are thrown together. Find the probability that the sum of the scores is 8.
Total outcomes = 6 × 6 = 36
Ways to get 8: (2,6)(3,5)(4,4)(5,3)(6,2) = 5 ways
P(sum 8) = 5/36
Answer = 5/36. For two dice always remember there are 36 equally likely pairs — list the ones that fit and count.
Follow-up: Same two dice — find P(sum is 7).
Ways: (1,6)(2,5)(3,4)(4,3)(5,2)(6,1) = 6 ways
P(sum 7) = 6/36 = 1/6
A sum of 7 is the most likely total on two dice.
