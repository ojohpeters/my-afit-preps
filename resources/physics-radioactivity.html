☢️ PART 1 — Basics & The Three Radiations

1. What is radioactivity?

Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration (decay) of unstable atomic nuclei with the emission of radiation.

Some nuclei have too many protons/neutrons and are unstable. To become stable they break down on their own and throw out particles or energy. We call these particles/energy nuclear radiation (alpha, beta or gamma).

The four key facts about radioactivity
  • It is a NUCLEAR process, not a chemical one. The change happens deep inside the nucleus — chemical reactions only involve outer electrons.
  • It is spontaneous — it happens by itself, with no trigger.
  • It is random — you cannot predict which nucleus will decay next or exactly when; you can only give the probability.
  • It is unaffected by temperature, pressure or chemical state. Heating it, squeezing it, or turning it into a compound does NOT change the decay rate. This is the big clue that it is nuclear, not chemical.
🌟 Memory: chemistry uses the OUTSIDE of the atom (electrons); radioactivity uses the CENTRE (the nucleus). That is why heat and pressure cannot touch it.

2. The three types of radiation

An unstable nucleus can give out three kinds of radiation, named after the first three Greek letters:

  • Alpha (α) — a heavy, positive helium nucleus, written ⁴₂He.
  • Beta (β) — a light, negative fast-moving electron, written ⁰₋₁e.
  • Gamma (γ) — a wave of energy (electromagnetic wave), no charge and no mass.

3. Penetrating power — how far it travels

Penetrating power is how deeply the radiation can pass through matter before being stopped.

Penetration:  Alpha < Beta < Gamma  (alpha weakest, gamma strongest)
  • Alpha — stopped by a sheet of paper or the top layer of skin. It travels only a few cm in air.
  • Beta — passes through paper but is stopped by a few mm of aluminium.
  • Gamma — passes through metal; you need thick lead or concrete to cut it down (it is never fully stopped, only reduced).
🌟 Picture it: alpha is a fat slow bowling ball (caught by paper), beta is a small fast bullet (needs metal), gamma is like an X-ray (goes through almost everything).

4. Ionising power — the opposite order

Ionising means knocking electrons off atoms it passes, leaving charged ions. The heavier and more charged the radiation, the more strongly it ionises.

Ionising power:  Alpha > Beta > Gamma  (alpha strongest, gamma weakest)

Notice: ionising power is the reverse of penetrating power. Alpha ionises the most because it is big and highly charged — but that is exactly why it loses its energy fast and cannot penetrate far.

🌟 Rule of thumb: the radiation that penetrates LEAST (alpha) ionises the MOST, and vice-versa.

5. Deflection in electric and magnetic fields

Because they carry charge, alpha and beta are bent by electric and magnetic fields; gamma is not.

  • Alpha (+2): deflected towards the negative plate; only a small deflection because it is heavy.
  • Beta (−1): deflected towards the positive plate — the opposite way to alpha; a large deflection because it is very light.
  • Gamma (0): not deflected at all — it has no charge, so it goes straight through.
🌟 In a field the beam splits three ways: alpha bends slightly one way, beta bends a lot the other way, gamma carries straight on.

⚛️ PART 2 — Balancing Nuclear Equations

1. Reading a nuclear symbol

Every nucleus is written with two numbers, like ²³⁸₉₂U:

  • Top number = mass number (A) = total protons + neutrons (also called the nucleon number). Here A = 238.
  • Bottom number = atomic number (Z) = number of protons (this decides which element it is). Here Z = 92, so it is uranium (U).
The golden rule (conservation)
Top numbers must BALANCE · Bottom numbers must BALANCE

In any nuclear equation, the sum of the mass numbers (A) on the left = sum on the right, AND the sum of the atomic numbers (Z) on the left = sum on the right. Use this to find any missing particle or daughter nucleus.

2. Alpha decay — lose an ⁴₂He

The nucleus throws out an alpha particle (⁴₂He). Since alpha carries away 4 mass units and 2 protons:

Alpha decay:  A decreases by 4 · Z decreases by 2
Worked example (the classic one): Uranium-238 decays by alpha emission.
²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He
Check top: 238 = 234 + 4 ✓   Check bottom: 92 = 90 + 2 ✓. The daughter is thorium-234.
Worked example — find the missing daughter: ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ? + ⁴₂He
Mass of daughter = 226 − 4 = 222
Atomic number of daughter = 88 − 2 = 86 → element with Z = 86 is radon (Rn)
So the daughter is ²²²₈₆Rn.

3. Beta decay — a neutron turns into a proton

Inside the nucleus a neutron changes into a proton + an electron. The electron shoots out as the beta particle (⁰₋₁e). The proton stays, so the number of protons goes UP by 1, but the total mass number does not change (a neutron simply became a proton).

Beta decay:  A stays the SAME · Z increases by 1
Worked example: Carbon-14 decays by beta emission.
¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e
Check top: 14 = 14 + 0 ✓   Check bottom: 6 = 7 + (−1) ✓. Carbon (Z=6) becomes nitrogen (Z=7).
Worked example — find the missing daughter: ²³⁴₉₀Th → ? + ⁰₋₁e
Mass of daughter = 234 − 0 = 234 (unchanged)
Atomic number = 90 + 1 = 91 → element with Z = 91 is protactinium (Pa)
So the daughter is ²³⁴₉₁Pa.

4. Gamma emission — pure energy

After alpha or beta decay the new nucleus is often left with extra energy. It releases this as a gamma ray (γ). Gamma has no mass and no charge, so:

Gamma emission:  NO change in A · NO change in Z

Only energy is lost. The element stays exactly the same element — it just calms down to a lower-energy state.

5. Method box — find any missing particle

Step-by-step method:
  • Step 1: Write the equation with the unknown as a symbol.
  • Step 2: Add up the TOP (mass) numbers on the known side and make both sides equal → gives the unknown mass number.
  • Step 3: Add up the BOTTOM (atomic) numbers and make both sides equal → gives the unknown atomic number.
  • Step 4: Use Z to name the particle: Z=2 & A=4 → alpha; Z=−1 & A=0 → beta; Z=0 & A=0 → gamma; otherwise look up the element with that Z.
Worked example — find the emitted particle: ²¹⁰₈₄Po → ²⁰⁶₈₂Pb + ?
Top: 210 = 206 + A → A = 210 − 206 = 4
Bottom: 84 = 82 + Z → Z = 84 − 82 = 2
A particle with A = 4 and Z = 2 is an alpha particle (⁴₂He).
Worked example — beta this time: ²⁴₁₁Na → ²⁴₁₂Mg + ?
Top: 24 = 24 + A → A = 0
Bottom: 11 = 12 + Z → Z = 11 − 12 = −1
A particle with A = 0 and Z = −1 is a beta particle (⁰₋₁e).

⏳ PART 3 — Half-life, Fission/Fusion & Uses

1. What is half-life?

Half-life (T½) is the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay (equivalently, the time for the activity or count rate to fall to half its value).

Because decay is random, we cannot say when one nucleus will go — but for a large sample, the time to lose half is always the same. Each isotope has its own fixed half-life (seconds for some, billions of years for others).

🌟 Idea: if T½ = 2 hours, then every 2 hours the amount left is cut in half — again and again: full → ½ → ¼ → ⅛ …

2. The halving formula

After each half-life the amount is halved. After n half-lives:

Fraction remaining = (½)ⁿ   where n = t / T½
N = N₀ × (½)^(t / T½)
  • N₀ = starting amount (or starting count rate/activity)
  • N = amount left after time t
  • n = t ÷ T½ = number of half-lives that have passed

Handy table of fractions: after 1 half-life ½ left · 2 → ¼ · 3 → ⅛ · 4 → 1/16 · 5 → 1/32.

3. Worked half-life calculations

Example 1 — how much is left? A sample of 800 g has a half-life of 5 years. How much remains after 15 years?
n = t ÷ T½ = 15 ÷ 5 = 3 half-lives
Remaining = 800 × (½)³ = 800 × 1/8 = 100 g
Step check: 800 → 400 → 200 → 100. Answer = 100 g.
Example 2 — how many half-lives? A sample falls from 240 g to 30 g. How many half-lives passed?
Halve step by step: 240 → 120 → 60 → 30
That is 3 halvings, so n = 3 half-lives. (Check: (½)³ = 1/8, and 240 ÷ 8 = 30 ✓)
Example 3 — find the time. An isotope has T½ = 8 days. How long until its activity falls to 1/16 of the original?
1/16 = (½)⁴ → so n = 4 half-lives
t = n × T½ = 4 × 8 = 32 days
Answer = 32 days.
Example 4 — find the original amount. After 4 half-lives, 5 g of a sample is left. What was the original mass?
Fraction left = (½)⁴ = 1/16, so 5 g = (1/16) × N₀
N₀ = 5 × 16 = 80 g
Answer = 80 g.
Example 5 — how much has DECAYED? A 160 g sample has T½ = 2 hours. How much has decayed after 6 hours?
n = 6 ÷ 2 = 3 half-lives → remaining = 160 × 1/8 = 20 g
Decayed = starting − remaining = 160 − 20 = 140 g
Answer = 140 g decayed. (Careful: the question asks how much has GONE, not how much is left.)

4. Activity, detection & background radiation

  • Activity = the number of nuclei decaying per second. Unit: the becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay per second. Activity also falls by half every half-life.
  • Geiger–Müller (G-M) tube: the standard instrument for detecting and counting radiation. Each particle that enters causes a pulse; connected to a counter it gives the count rate (counts per second).
  • Background radiation: the weak radiation always around us — from rocks/soil (radon gas), cosmic rays from space, food, and medical/industrial sources. You must subtract the background count from your reading to get the true count rate of a source.

6. Uses of radioactivity

  • Carbon dating: living things absorb carbon-14 (T½ ≈ 5730 years). After death no new C-14 is taken in, so by measuring how much is left we find the age of old bones, wood and fossils.
  • Medical tracers: a small amount of a gamma source (e.g. technetium-99m, iodine-131) is put into the body and followed with a detector to study organs, blood flow or find blockages/tumours.
  • Cancer treatment (radiotherapy): strong gamma rays from cobalt-60 are aimed at tumours to kill cancer cells.
  • Sterilisation: gamma rays kill bacteria on medical equipment, syringes and even food — without heat.
  • Power generation: nuclear fission in reactors produces heat to make steam and drive turbines for electricity.
  • Industry: checking thickness of paper/metal sheets, and detecting leaks or cracks in pipes/welds.

7. Hazards & safety

Radiation is dangerous because it ionises living cells — it can damage DNA, cause burns, radiation sickness and cancer. Safety is about reducing your dose:

  • Shielding: keep sources in lead-lined or thick-concrete containers.
  • Distance: handle sources with long tongs; intensity falls with distance.
  • Time: spend as little time as possible near a source.
  • Never point a source at people; store safely, wear film badges/dosimeters to monitor exposure, and dispose of waste carefully.
🌟 Remember the three safety words: Shielding, Distance, Time — reduce all three exposures to reduce the dose.
