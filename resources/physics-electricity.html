🔋 PART 1 — Electric Charges (Static Electricity / Electrostatics)

1. What is electric charge?

Everything is made of atoms. Inside each atom are tiny particles that carry electric charge:

  • Protons — carry positive (+) charge, sit in the nucleus, cannot move easily.
  • Electrons — carry negative (−) charge, orbit the nucleus, can move from atom to atom.
  • Neutrons — no charge (neutral).

A normal atom has equal protons and electrons, so it is neutral. When an object gains electrons it becomes negative; when it loses electrons it becomes positive. (Only electrons move — protons stay put.)

SI unit of charge

Charge is measured in coulombs (C). The smallest charge is on one electron: e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

Charge Q = number of electrons (n) × e  ⇒  Q = n × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹
Worked example: How many electrons make up a charge of 1 C?
n = Q ÷ e = 1 ÷ 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ = 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons
So 1 coulomb is a HUGE number of electrons.

2. The law of electric charges

This is the golden rule of electrostatics:

LIKE charges REPEL · UNLIKE charges ATTRACT
  • + and + → push apart (repel)
  • − and − → push apart (repel)
  • + and − → pull together (attract)
💡 Memory: opposites attract (like a boy and girl magnet), same kinds quarrel and push away.

Key test: Only repulsion proves an object is charged for sure. Attraction can happen even with a neutral object (because of induction), so attraction alone is not proof.

3. Conductors and insulators

ConductorsInsulators
Let charge/electrons flow freelyDo NOT let charge flow
Have free electronsElectrons are tightly held
Metals (copper, silver), graphite, the human body, salt waterRubber, plastic, glass, dry wood, air, pure water

Semiconductors (e.g. silicon) are in between — used in electronics.

4. Ways of charging an object

(a) Charging by friction (rubbing)

Rub two different materials together — electrons jump from one to the other. One becomes + (lost electrons), the other − (gained electrons).

🌟 Real life: rubbing a plastic ruler/comb on your hair lets it pick up tiny pieces of paper; lightning is charge built by friction in clouds.

(b) Charging by contact (conduction)

Touch a charged object to a neutral conductor — charge spreads onto it. Both end up with the same sign of charge.

(c) Charging by induction (no touching!)

Bring a charged rod NEAR (not touching) a conductor. The rod pushes/pulls the electrons in the conductor, so the near side gets one charge and the far side the opposite. Earth the far side, remove it, then remove the rod — the object keeps a charge that is opposite to the rod.

Conservation of charge

Charge is never created or destroyed — it only moves from one body to another. The total charge before = total charge after.

5. The gold-leaf electroscope

A device used to detect charge and tell if it is + or −. It has a metal cap connected to a metal rod with a thin gold leaf.

  • Detecting charge: a charged object near the cap makes the leaf diverge (rise/spread).
  • More divergence = more charge.
  • Finding the sign: bring a KNOWN charge close. If the leaf diverges MORE, the object has the SAME charge as the known one. If it falls, it’s opposite.

6. Coulomb’s Law (force between charges)

Two charges feel a force. Coulomb measured exactly how strong:

F = k Q₁Q₂ / r²   (k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²)
  • F = force in newtons (N)
  • Q₁, Q₂ = the two charges in coulombs (C)
  • r = distance between them in metres (m)

Notice the r²: double the distance → force becomes ¼ (inverse-square law). Bigger charges → bigger force.

Worked example: Find the force between +2 µC and +3 µC placed 0.1 m apart.
F = kQ₁Q₂/r² = 9×10⁹ × (2×10⁻⁶)(3×10⁻⁶) ÷ (0.1)²
= 9×10⁹ × 6×10⁻¹² ÷ 0.01 = 0.054 ÷ 0.01 = 5.4 N
Both are positive → the force is repulsive. (Tip: µC means ×10⁻⁶ — always convert!)

🌐 PART 2 — The Electric Field

1. What is an electric field?

An electric field is the region around a charge where another charge would feel a force. You don’t have to touch a charge to feel its push/pull — the field reaches out through space.

🌟 Analogy: just as the Earth has a gravitational field that pulls objects toward it, a charge has an electric field that pushes/pulls other charges around it.

2. Electric field lines (direction)

We draw arrows to show which way a positive test charge would be pushed:

  • Field lines point AWAY from a positive charge (it pushes a + test charge away).
  • Field lines point TOWARD a negative charge (it pulls a + test charge in).
  • Lines start on + and end on −.
  • Field lines NEVER cross (a point can have only one field direction).
  • Closer lines = stronger field.

3. Electric field strength / intensity (E)

This tells you how strong the field is — the force felt per unit charge.

E = F / Q
  • E = electric field strength, unit N/C (newton per coulomb) or V/m
  • F = force on the charge (N)
  • Q = the charge placed in the field (C)

Field strength is a vector — it has direction (same direction a + charge would move).

Worked example: A charge of 5 µC feels a force of 0.1 N in a field. Find E.
E = F ÷ Q = 0.1 ÷ (5×10⁻⁶) = 2×10⁴ N/C

4. Field due to a point charge

The field strength at a distance r from a single charge Q:

E = k Q / r²   (k = 9 × 10⁹)

Same inverse-square idea as Coulomb’s law: move twice as far → field drops to a quarter.

Worked example: Find the field 0.3 m from a +2 µC charge.
E = kQ/r² = 9×10⁹ × 2×10⁻⁶ ÷ (0.3)²
= 9×10⁹ × 2×10⁻⁶ ÷ 0.09 = 18×10³ ÷ 0.09 = 2×10⁵ N/C

5. Electric potential & potential difference (p.d.)

Electric potential (V) at a point = the work done to bring 1 coulomb of positive charge from infinity to that point. Unit = volt (V).

V = W / Q  ⇒  Work W = QV

Potential difference (p.d. or voltage) between two points = the work done to move 1 C of charge between them. A battery provides this “push”.

🌟 Analogy: potential is like height/pressure. Charges flow from high potential to low potential, just as water flows downhill from high pressure to low.
Worked example: 12 J of work moves 4 C between two points. Find the p.d.
V = W ÷ Q = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 V

6. Capacitors (storing charge) — basic idea

A capacitor stores electric charge. Its capacitance (C) measures how much charge it holds per volt.

C = Q / V  (unit: farad, F)
Worked example: A capacitor stores 12 µC at 6 V. Find its capacitance.
C = Q ÷ V = 12×10⁻⁶ ÷ 6 = 2×10⁻⁶ F = 2 µF

💡 PART 3 — Current Electricity

1. Electric current

Electric current is the rate of flow of charge — how much charge passes a point each second. Unit = ampere (A).

I = Q / t  ⇒  Q = I × t
  • I = current (A)   Q = charge (C)   t = time (s)
  • 1 ampere = 1 coulomb per second. So 1 C = 1 A·s.

Conventional current flows from + to − (outside the cell). Electrons actually flow the opposite way (− to +), but we follow the conventional direction.

Worked example: A current of 2 A flows for 30 s. How much charge passed?
Q = I × t = 2 × 30 = 60 C

2. E.m.f. vs potential difference

  • E.m.f. (electromotive force, E): the TOTAL energy a cell/battery gives to each coulomb of charge. Measured across the cell when no current flows.
  • P.d. (voltage, V): the energy used by each coulomb in a particular component of the circuit.

Both are measured in volts. Some energy is lost inside the cell itself (internal resistance), so usually p.d. across the circuit < e.m.f.

3. Ohm’s Law & resistance

Ohm’s Law: for a metal conductor at constant temperature, current is directly proportional to voltage.

V = I × R  ⇒  R = V/I ,  I = V/R
  • V = voltage (V)   I = current (A)   R = resistance (ohms, Ω)
  • Resistance = how much a component opposes current. High R → small current.
🌟 Analogy: voltage = pump pressure, current = water flow, resistance = a narrow pipe that fights the flow.
Worked example: A 12 V battery drives current through a 4 Ω resistor. Find I.
I = V ÷ R = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 A

4. What affects resistance? (Resistivity)

R = ρL / A
  • ρ (rho) = resistivity of the material (Ω·m)
  • L = length of wire — longer wire → more resistance
  • A = cross-sectional area — thicker wire → less resistance

Also: resistance of a metal increases with temperature.

Quick logic: Double the length → double R. Double the thickness (area) → halve R.

5. Resistors in series and parallel

Series (one path, joined end to end)

R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …

Same current everywhere; voltages add up. Resistances simply add.

Example: 2 Ω, 3 Ω, 5 Ω in series → R = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 Ω.

Parallel (branches, side by side)

1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + …

Same voltage across each branch; currents add. Total R is smaller than the smallest resistor.

Example: two 6 Ω resistors in parallel:
1/R = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3 → R = 3 Ω
Shortcut for two resistors: R = (R₁×R₂)/(R₁+R₂) = (6×6)/(6+6) = 36/12 = 3 Ω. (For n EQUAL resistors in parallel: R = one resistor ÷ n.)

6. Electrical energy & power

Power P = V × I = I²R = V²/R  (unit: watt, W)
Energy W = P × t = V I t  (unit: joule, J)
Worked example: A bulb runs on 240 V drawing 5 A. Find its power.
P = V × I = 240 × 5 = 1200 W = 1.2 kW
Cost of electricity (the kilowatt-hour)

Electricity bills use the kWh (1 kW used for 1 hour).

Example: a 2 kW heater for 3 hours uses 2 × 3 = 6 kWh (6 units). Multiply by the price per unit for the cost.

7. Cells & internal resistance

A real cell wastes a little energy inside itself — its internal resistance (r). The full circuit equation:

E = I (R + r)  ⇒  E = V + I r
Worked example: A 1.5 V cell with r = 0.5 Ω connects to a 4 Ω resistor. Find the current.
I = E ÷ (R + r) = 1.5 ÷ (4 + 0.5) = 1.5 ÷ 4.5 = 0.33 A

8. Safety in electricity (quick facts)

  • Fuse: a thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit if the current is too high — protects appliances.
  • Earthing: a wire connecting the metal case to the ground, so stray charge flows safely away instead of shocking you.
  • Switches and fuses are placed in the LIVE wire.
  • Never touch electrical things with wet hands — water + salt on skin conducts electricity.
