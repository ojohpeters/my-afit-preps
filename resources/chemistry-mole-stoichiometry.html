⚖️ PART 1 — The Mole: Counting by Weighing

1. Why chemists invented the mole

Atoms are far too small and too many to count one by one. A single spoon of water holds billions of billions of molecules. So chemists count atoms the same way a shopkeeper counts rice — by weighing a known bundle.

The mole is that bundle. It is simply a fixed NUMBER of particles — just like a "dozen" always means 12 and a "pair" always means 2. A mole always means the same huge number of particles.

🌟 Memory: a dozen eggs = 12 eggs. A mole of atoms = 6.02×10²³ atoms. Same idea, just a much bigger count.

2. Avogadro's number

The number of particles in one mole is called Avogadro's number (or constant):

1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles

"Particles" can mean atoms, molecules, ions or electrons — whatever the substance is made of. So:

  • 1 mole of carbon atoms = 6.02×10²³ carbon atoms
  • 1 mole of water molecules = 6.02×10²³ H₂O molecules
  • 1 mole of sodium ions = 6.02×10²³ Na⁺ ions
Number ↔ moles
moles = number of particles ÷ 6.02×10²³
number of particles = moles × 6.02×10²³
Worked example — particles in 2 mol: How many molecules are in 2 mol of CO₂?
particles = moles × 6.02×10²³
particles = 2 × 6.02×10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules
Answer = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules.
Worked example — moles from particles: How many moles are in 3.01×10²³ atoms of iron?
moles = 3.01×10²³ ÷ 6.02×10²³ = 0.5 mol
Answer = 0.5 mol.

3. Molar mass — the weight of one mole

Here is the beautiful trick: one mole of a substance has a mass, in grams, equal to its relative atomic / molecular / formula mass. That mass is called the molar mass (units: g/mol or g per mole).

  • Relative atomic mass of carbon = 12, so molar mass of C = 12 g/mol.
  • Relative molecular mass of water (H₂O) = 18, so molar mass of H₂O = 18 g/mol.
  • Relative molecular mass of CO₂ = 44, so molar mass of CO₂ = 44 g/mol.
How to work out a molar mass

Add up the relative atomic masses of every atom in the formula. Common values to remember: H=1, C=12, N=14, O=16, Na=23, Mg=24, S=32, Cl=35.5, K=39, Ca=40, Fe=56, Cu=64, Zn=65.

Worked example — molar mass of NaOH:
Na = 23, O = 16, H = 1
Molar mass = 23 + 16 + 1 = 40 g/mol
Worked example — molar mass of CaCO₃:
Ca = 40, C = 12, O₃ = 3 × 16 = 48
Molar mass = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 g/mol
Worked example — molar mass of H₂SO₄:
H₂ = 2 × 1 = 2, S = 32, O₄ = 4 × 16 = 64
Molar mass = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 g/mol

4. The master formula: moles ↔ mass

This is the most-used equation in the whole topic. Learn it three ways:

moles = mass ÷ molar mass
mass = moles × molar mass
molar mass = mass ÷ moles
🌟 Triangle trick: put mass on top, with moles and molar mass below. Cover what you want to find and the triangle tells you to multiply or divide.
Worked example — moles in 44 g of CO₂:
molar mass of CO₂ = 44 g/mol
moles = mass ÷ molar mass = 44 ÷ 44 = 1 mol
Answer = 1 mole. (So 44 g of CO₂ is exactly one mole.)
Worked example — moles in 9 g of water:
molar mass of H₂O = 18 g/mol
moles = 9 ÷ 18 = 0.5 mol
Answer = 0.5 mol.
Worked example — mass of 0.5 mol NaOH:
molar mass of NaOH = 40 g/mol
mass = moles × molar mass = 0.5 × 40 = 20 g
Answer = 20 g.
Worked example — mass of 3 mol CO₂:
mass = 3 × 44 = 132 g
Answer = 132 g.

5. Molar volume of a gas at s.t.p.

For gases there is a shortcut. Avogadro's law says equal volumes of any gas (at the same temperature and pressure) contain equal numbers of molecules. So one mole of ANY gas takes up the same volume:

At s.t.p., 1 mole of any gas = 22.4 dm³ (22 400 cm³)

s.t.p. means standard temperature and pressure (0 °C and 1 atmosphere). The 22.4 dm³ is called the molar volume.

Volume ↔ moles of gas
moles of gas = volume (dm³) ÷ 22.4
volume (dm³) = moles × 22.4
Worked example — volume of 2 mol of gas:
volume = moles × 22.4 = 2 × 22.4 = 44.8 dm³
Answer = 44.8 dm³.
Worked example — moles in 11.2 dm³ of gas:
moles = volume ÷ 22.4 = 11.2 ÷ 22.4 = 0.5 mol
Answer = 0.5 mol.
🌟 Remember: 1 dm³ = 1 litre = 1000 cm³. Always change cm³ to dm³ (÷1000) before using 22.4.

6. The mole map — one picture to rule them all

Every mole problem is just moving between four boxes, and moles sits in the middle:

🧮 PART 2 — Formulae & Percentage Composition

1. Empirical vs molecular formula

  • Empirical formula = the simplest whole-number ratio of the atoms in a compound.
  • Molecular formula = the actual number of each atom in one molecule.

Example: glucose is C₆H₁₂O₆ (molecular formula). Divide every subscript by 6 and you get CH₂O (empirical formula). The molecular formula is always the empirical formula multiplied by some whole number n.

Molecular formula = (Empirical formula) × n
🌟 Empirical = the recipe ratio (1 : 2 : 1). Molecular = the real batch size (6 : 12 : 6).

2. Finding the empirical formula from % composition

The method is always the same four steps. Treat percentages as grams (imagine 100 g of the compound).

The 4 steps
  • Step 1: Write the mass (or %) of each element.
  • Step 2: Divide each by its relative atomic mass → gives moles of each element.
  • Step 3: Divide every answer by the SMALLEST of them → gives the ratio.
  • Step 4: If the ratio is not whole numbers, multiply all by a small whole number to clear it.
Worked example: A compound contains 40% carbon, 6.7% hydrogen and 53.3% oxygen. Find its empirical formula. (C=12, H=1, O=16)
C: 40 ÷ 12 = 3.33  |  H: 6.7 ÷ 1 = 6.7  |  O: 53.3 ÷ 16 = 3.33
Divide by smallest (3.33): C = 1, H = 2, O = 1
Empirical formula = CH₂O.
Worked example — when the ratio isn't whole: An oxide of iron is 70% Fe and 30% O. Find its empirical formula. (Fe=56, O=16)
Fe: 70 ÷ 56 = 1.25  |  O: 30 ÷ 16 = 1.875
Divide by smallest (1.25): Fe = 1, O = 1.5
Multiply both by 2 to clear the 0.5: Fe = 2, O = 3
Empirical formula = Fe₂O₃.

3. Finding the molecular formula

Once you have the empirical formula, you only need the compound's molar mass to find the molecular formula.

n = molar mass ÷ empirical formula mass
Molecular formula = (empirical formula) × n
Worked example: A compound has empirical formula CH₂O and a molar mass of 180 g/mol. Find its molecular formula. (C=12, H=1, O=16)
Empirical mass of CH₂O = 12 + (2×1) + 16 = 30
n = 180 ÷ 30 = 6
Molecular formula = (CH₂O) × 6 = C₆H₁₂O₆
Answer = C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose).
Worked example: Empirical formula NO₂, molar mass 92 g/mol. Find the molecular formula. (N=14, O=16)
Empirical mass of NO₂ = 14 + (2×16) = 46
n = 92 ÷ 46 = 2
Molecular formula = (NO₂) × 2 = N₂O₄
Answer = N₂O₄.

4. Percentage composition of an element

To find what fraction of a compound's mass comes from one element:

% of element = (total mass of that element in the formula ÷ molar mass) × 100
Worked example — % of nitrogen in NH₄NO₃: (N=14, H=1, O=16)
Molar mass = 2 N + 4 H + 3 O = (2×14) + (4×1) + (3×16)
= 28 + 4 + 48 = 80 g/mol
Mass of N = 2 × 14 = 28 (there are TWO nitrogen atoms)
% N = (28 ÷ 80) × 100 = 35%
Answer = 35% nitrogen.
Worked example — % of oxygen in water (H₂O): (H=1, O=16)
Molar mass = 2 + 16 = 18
% O = (16 ÷ 18) × 100 = 88.9%
Answer = 88.9% oxygen (so water is only about 11.1% hydrogen by mass).
Worked example — % of calcium in CaCO₃: (Ca=40, molar mass 100)
% Ca = (40 ÷ 100) × 100 = 40%
Answer = 40% calcium.
🌟 Watch the subscripts: if there are 2 nitrogen atoms, you must use 2 × 14 = 28, not just 14.

🔥 PART 3 — Stoichiometry: Reacting Masses & Gas Volumes

1. Balancing chemical equations

The law of conservation of mass says atoms are never created or destroyed in a reaction — they are only rearranged. So an equation must have the same number of each atom on both sides. We balance by putting big numbers (coefficients) in front of formulae — never by changing a formula's subscripts.

Worked example — burning hydrogen: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
Oxygen: 2 on the left, only 1 on the right — put a 2 before H₂O
H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O   (now 4 H on the right)
Balance hydrogen: put a 2 before H₂
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O ✓ (H: 4 = 4, O: 2 = 2)
Worked example — heating limestone:
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ ✓ (Ca: 1=1, C: 1=1, O: 3 = 1+2 = 3)
Already balanced.
Worked example — zinc + acid:
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ ✓ (Zn: 1=1, H: 2=2, Cl: 2=2)

2. Mole ratios from a balanced equation

The big numbers in a balanced equation give the mole ratio — the recipe of the reaction. This ratio is the bridge from one substance to another.

Read the ratios: In 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
  • Mg : O₂ = 2 : 1 (2 moles of magnesium react with 1 mole of oxygen)
  • Mg : MgO = 2 : 2 = 1 : 1 (2 moles of Mg give 2 moles of MgO)
  • O₂ : MgO = 1 : 2
The universal method for reacting-mass problems
  • Step 1: Write the balanced equation.
  • Step 2: Change the mass you are GIVEN into moles (÷ molar mass).
  • Step 3: Use the mole ratio to find moles of the thing you WANT.
  • Step 4: Change those moles back into mass (× molar mass) or into volume (× 22.4 dm³).
🌟 Mantra: mass → moles → ratio → moles → mass. Never jump straight from mass to mass.

3. Worked reacting-mass calculations

Problem 1 — mass of CaO from heating CaCO₃: What mass of calcium oxide (CaO) is formed when 50 g of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is heated? (CaCO₃ = 100, CaO = 56)
Equation: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
moles of CaCO₃ = 50 ÷ 100 = 0.5 mol
ratio CaCO₃ : CaO = 1 : 1, so moles of CaO = 0.5 mol
mass of CaO = 0.5 × 56 = 28 g
Answer = 28 g of CaO.
Problem 2 — mass of MgO from Mg: What mass of magnesium oxide is formed when 6 g of magnesium burns in excess oxygen? (Mg = 24, MgO = 40)
Equation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
moles of Mg = 6 ÷ 24 = 0.25 mol
ratio Mg : MgO = 2 : 2 = 1 : 1, so moles of MgO = 0.25 mol
mass of MgO = 0.25 × 40 = 10 g
Answer = 10 g of MgO.
Problem 3 — mass of water from hydrogen: What mass of water is produced when 4 g of hydrogen burns in excess oxygen? (H₂ = 2, H₂O = 18)
Equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
moles of H₂ = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 mol
ratio H₂ : H₂O = 2 : 2 = 1 : 1, so moles of H₂O = 2 mol
mass of H₂O = 2 × 18 = 36 g
Answer = 36 g of water.

4. Worked gas-volume calculations

Problem 4 — volume of CO₂ from CaCO₃: What volume of carbon dioxide (measured at s.t.p.) is produced when 25 g of CaCO₃ is heated? (CaCO₃ = 100)
Equation: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
moles of CaCO₃ = 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25 mol
ratio CaCO₃ : CO₂ = 1 : 1, so moles of CO₂ = 0.25 mol
volume of CO₂ = 0.25 × 22.4 = 5.6 dm³
Answer = 5.6 dm³ of CO₂.
Problem 5 — volume of hydrogen from zinc: What volume of hydrogen gas (at s.t.p.) is produced when 13 g of zinc reacts with excess dilute hydrochloric acid? (Zn = 65)
Equation: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
moles of Zn = 13 ÷ 65 = 0.2 mol
ratio Zn : H₂ = 1 : 1, so moles of H₂ = 0.2 mol
volume of H₂ = 0.2 × 22.4 = 4.48 dm³
Answer = 4.48 dm³ of H₂.
Problem 6 — using the 2:1 ratio: What volume of oxygen (at s.t.p.) is needed to burn 8 g of hydrogen completely? (H₂ = 2)
Equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
moles of H₂ = 8 ÷ 2 = 4 mol
ratio H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1, so moles of O₂ = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 mol
volume of O₂ = 2 × 22.4 = 44.8 dm³
Answer = 44.8 dm³ of O₂.

5. The limiting reagent idea

When two reactants are mixed, one usually runs out first. The reactant that runs out is the limiting reagent — it controls (limits) how much product can form. The other is said to be in excess.

Simple example: In 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, suppose you mix 3 mol of H₂ with 3 mol of O₂.
The ratio needs H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1
3 mol H₂ only needs 1.5 mol O₂ — but 3 mol O₂ is present
So H₂ runs out first → H₂ is the limiting reagent; O₂ is in excess
The amount of water made is decided by the H₂.
🌟 Think of making sandwiches: 2 slices of bread + 1 filling. However much filling you have, if you only have bread for 3 sandwiches, bread is the limiting reagent.

💧 PART 4 — Solutions & Concentration

1. What concentration means

Concentration tells you how much solute (the dissolved substance) is packed into a given volume of solution. The more solute per litre, the more concentrated. There are two common ways to measure it.

  • Concentration in g/dm³ — grams of solute per dm³ of solution.
  • Concentration in mol/dm³ — moles of solute per dm³. This is also called molarity (a "1 molar" solution = 1 mol/dm³).
Concentration (g/dm³) = mass (g) ÷ volume (dm³)
Concentration (mol/dm³) = moles ÷ volume (dm³)
🌟 Always change the volume to dm³ first: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. So 500 cm³ = 0.5 dm³, 25 cm³ = 0.025 dm³.

2. Worked concentration calculations

Worked example — both units at once: 4 g of NaOH is dissolved to make 500 cm³ of solution. Find the concentration in g/dm³ and in mol/dm³. (NaOH = 40)
Volume = 500 cm³ = 0.5 dm³
Concentration (g/dm³) = 4 ÷ 0.5 = 8 g/dm³
moles of NaOH = 4 ÷ 40 = 0.1 mol
Concentration (mol/dm³) = 0.1 ÷ 0.5 = 0.2 mol/dm³
Answer = 8 g/dm³ = 0.2 mol/dm³.

3. Converting between g/dm³ and mol/dm³

The molar mass is the bridge between the two units — exactly like mass ↔ moles.

g/dm³ = mol/dm³ × molar mass
mol/dm³ = g/dm³ ÷ molar mass
Worked example: Convert 0.5 mol/dm³ H₂SO₄ into g/dm³. (H₂SO₄ = 98)
g/dm³ = 0.5 × 98 = 49 g/dm³
Answer = 49 g/dm³.
Worked example: A solution of NaOH is 8 g/dm³. What is its molarity? (NaOH = 40)
mol/dm³ = 8 ÷ 40 = 0.2 mol/dm³
Answer = 0.2 mol/dm³.

4. Making up a solution

To prepare a solution of a chosen concentration, first find the moles you need, then the mass.

moles needed = concentration (mol/dm³) × volume (dm³)
mass to weigh out = moles × molar mass
Worked example: What mass of NaOH is needed to make 250 cm³ of a 0.1 mol/dm³ solution? (NaOH = 40)
Volume = 250 cm³ = 0.25 dm³
moles = 0.1 × 0.25 = 0.025 mol
mass = 0.025 × 40 = 1.0 g
Answer = 1.0 g of NaOH, dissolved and made up to 250 cm³.

5. Dilution — adding water

Diluting a solution adds water but does NOT change the number of moles of solute already there. So the moles before = moles after, which gives the dilution formula:

C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

C₁, V₁ = concentration and volume before; C₂, V₂ = after. (Any matching units work, as long as you are consistent.)

Worked example: 25 cm³ of 2 mol/dm³ acid is diluted with water to a final volume of 100 cm³. Find the new concentration.
C₁V₁ = C₂V₂
2 × 25 = C₂ × 100
C₂ = 50 ÷ 100 = 0.5 mol/dm³
Answer = 0.5 mol/dm³.
🌟 Diluting 4× (25 cm³ → 100 cm³) makes the concentration 4× weaker (2 → 0.5). Volume up ⇒ concentration down.

6. A simple titration calculation

In a titration, an acid and a base react exactly. If you measure the volumes and know one concentration, you can find the other. The method is the same mole route as stoichiometry.

Worked example: 25 cm³ of NaOH is exactly neutralised by 20 cm³ of 0.1 mol/dm³ HCl. Find the concentration of the NaOH.
Equation: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O (ratio 1 : 1)
moles of HCl = conc × volume = 0.1 × (20 ÷ 1000) = 0.002 mol
ratio 1 : 1, so moles of NaOH = 0.002 mol
conc of NaOH = moles ÷ volume = 0.002 ÷ (25 ÷ 1000) = 0.002 ÷ 0.025
= 0.08 mol/dm³
Answer = 0.08 mol/dm³.
