🍎 PART 1 — The Classes of Food

1. What is food and nutrition?

Food is any substance which, when taken into the body, provides energy, materials for growth and repair, or substances to protect the body and keep it working properly.

Nutrition is the process by which living things obtain and use food. The useful substances inside food are called nutrients.

The classes (groups) of food
  • Carbohydrates — the main energy foods
  • Proteins — for growth and repair (body-building)
  • Fats and oils (lipids) — a concentrated energy store
  • Vitamins — tiny amounts needed to protect the body
  • Mineral salts — needed for bones, blood, etc.
  • Water — the medium in which everything happens

We also need roughage (dietary fibre). It is not really a nutrient — the body cannot digest it — but it is important for a healthy gut.

🌟 Memory: the energy group (carbohydrates + fats), the body-building group (proteins) and the protective group (vitamins, minerals, water). Learn them in these three families.

2. Carbohydrates — the main energy food

Function: the body's main and cheapest source of energy. They are broken down to glucose, which is burned in respiration to release energy.

Food sources: yam, cassava (garri), rice, maize, bread, potatoes, sugar, honey, fruits.

Elements present: Carbon, Hydrogen and Oxygen only (the H and O are in the ratio 2:1, as in water).

Types: sugars (glucose, sucrose), starch, and cellulose (fibre). Excess carbohydrate is stored as glycogen in the liver and muscles, or turned into fat.

3. Proteins — for growth and repair

Function: building new cells and tissues, growth, and repair of worn-out or damaged parts. Proteins also make enzymes, hormones and antibodies, and can supply energy in emergencies.

Food sources: meat, fish, eggs, milk, beans, groundnut, soya beans, melon (egusi).

Elements present: Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen and Nitrogen (some also contain sulphur and phosphorus). The presence of nitrogen is what makes proteins different from carbohydrates and fats.

Building blocks: proteins are made of small units called amino acids.

4. Fats and oils (lipids) — a concentrated energy store

Function: a store of energy (they give about twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrate); they insulate the body against heat loss and protect delicate organs; they also carry the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E and K.

Food sources: groundnut oil, palm oil, butter, margarine, milk, cheese, meat fat, coconut, egg yolk.

Elements present: Carbon, Hydrogen and Oxygen (like carbohydrates, but with far less oxygen).

Building blocks: fats are made of fatty acids and glycerol. Fats are solid at room temperature; oils are liquid.

5. Vitamins — small amounts, big jobs

Function: needed only in very small quantities to keep the body healthy and working. They do not give energy, but a lack of a vitamin causes a deficiency disease.

6. Mineral salts — for bones, blood and balance

Function: needed in small amounts to build certain tissues and control body processes.

Other useful minerals include sodium and potassium (nerve and fluid balance) and phosphorus (bones, teeth, with calcium).

7. Water and roughage

Water makes up about two-thirds of the body. It is the medium in which reactions happen, it carries dissolved food and waste, and it helps control body temperature (through sweating). We get it from drinks and from food.

Roughage (dietary fibre) is mainly cellulose from plant foods (vegetables, whole grains, fruit skins). It is not digested, but it adds bulk that the gut muscles can grip. This helps peristalsis push food along and prevents constipation.

9. A balanced diet

A balanced diet is a diet that contains all the classes of food in the correct proportions (amounts) needed by the body for health.

It must supply enough energy (carbohydrate and fat), enough body-building food (protein), and the protective foods (vitamins, minerals, water) plus roughage. The exact amounts depend on age, sex, size and how active a person is — a growing child or a labourer needs more than an office worker.

Eating too much (especially fat and sugar) leads to obesity; eating too little protein and energy leads to malnutrition such as kwashiorkor (protein deficiency) and marasmus.

🦷 PART 2 — The Digestive System

1. Why do we digest food?

Most food is made of large, insoluble molecules (starch, protein, fat) that are too big to pass into the blood. Digestion is the breaking down of large, insoluble food into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed and used by the body.

Two kinds of digestion
  • Physical (mechanical) digestion: the breaking up of food into smaller pieces without changing it chemically — chewing by the teeth and churning by the stomach. This increases the surface area for enzymes to work on.
  • Chemical digestion: the breaking down of food molecules by enzymes into simpler substances (e.g. starch → glucose). This does change the food chemically.

2. The alimentary canal (gut) — in order

The alimentary canal is the long tube from the mouth to the anus through which food passes. Food travels through these parts in order:

Mouth → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small intestine → Large intestine → Rectum → Anus
  • Mouth (buccal cavity): teeth chew the food; saliva mixes with it and starts digesting starch. The tongue rolls it into a ball (bolus).
  • Oesophagus (gullet): a muscular tube that carries food from the mouth to the stomach by peristalsis.
  • Stomach: a muscular bag that stores food and churns it with gastric juice (contains HCl and pepsin). Protein digestion begins here.
  • Small intestine: the longest part, in two main regions — the duodenum (first part; receives bile and pancreatic juice) and the ileum (where digestion is completed and food is absorbed).
  • Large intestine (colon): absorbs most of the remaining water and mineral salts from the undigested material.
  • Rectum: stores the solid waste (faeces).
  • Anus: the opening through which faeces are passed out (egestion).
Associated glands (help digestion but food does not pass through them)
  • Salivary glands — in the mouth; make saliva (contains salivary amylase / ptyalin).
  • Liver — makes bile.
  • Pancreas — makes pancreatic juice (and the hormones insulin and glucagon).

3. Teeth — types and functions

Humans have different-shaped teeth for different jobs (this is called being heterodont).

Type of toothShapeFunction

Chewing is physical digestion: it breaks food into small pieces and mixes it with saliva, giving enzymes a larger surface area to work on.

4. Peristalsis — how food moves

Peristalsis is the wave of muscular contraction and relaxation of the gut wall that pushes food along the alimentary canal. The muscles behind the food squeeze (contract) while the muscles in front relax, so the food is pushed forward — like squeezing toothpaste along a tube.

🌟 It is peristalsis (not gravity) that moves food. This is why an astronaut, or a person lying down, can still swallow food normally.

5. The liver and pancreas

The liver — the body's chemical factory

  • Produces bile, which helps digest fats.
  • Regulates blood sugar — stores excess glucose as glycogen and releases it when needed.
  • Deamination — breaks down excess amino acids, forming urea (removed by the kidneys).
  • Detoxification — makes poisons (e.g. alcohol) harmless.
  • Stores iron and some vitamins; produces heat for the body.

The pancreas

  • Makes pancreatic juice, which contains three enzymes: amylase, trypsin and lipase (poured into the duodenum).
  • Also makes the hormones insulin and glucagon, which control the level of glucose in the blood.

🧪 PART 3 — Enzymes & Chemical Digestion

1. What is an enzyme?

An enzyme is a biological catalyst — a substance (made of protein) produced by living cells that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up in it.

Key properties of enzymes
  • They are proteins and are made by living cells.
  • They are specific — each enzyme works on only one type of substrate (a lock-and-key fit). Amylase only digests starch; it cannot digest protein.
  • They are affected by temperature. They work fastest at about 37 °C (body temperature). Too cold and they slow down; too hot and they are denatured (destroyed) — a change that cannot be reversed.
  • They are affected by pH. Each enzyme has its own best pH — pepsin works in acid, but enzymes in the small intestine work in alkaline conditions.
  • They are needed only in small amounts (they are re-used).
🌟 Lock and key: the substrate (key) fits exactly into the enzyme (lock). If the shape is wrong, or heat has bent the lock (denatured it), the key no longer fits.

2. The digestive enzymes — where and what they do

The big picture: carbohydrates end up as glucose, proteins as amino acids, and fats as fatty acids and glycerol — all small and soluble, ready to be absorbed.

3. The role of hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach

Cells in the stomach wall release hydrochloric acid. Its jobs are:

  • To provide the acidic conditions (about pH 2) that the enzyme pepsin needs to work.
  • To kill bacteria (germs) swallowed with the food.
  • To activate pepsin from its inactive form (pepsinogen).
🌟 Exam trap: pepsin works in ACID, not alkali. The stomach is the one acidic part of the gut.

4. Bile — an emulsifier, not an enzyme

Bile is a greenish-yellow fluid made in the liver and stored in the gall bladder. It flows into the duodenum.

  • Bile is NOT an enzyme — it contains no enzymes.
  • It emulsifies fats — it breaks large fat globules into many tiny droplets. This gives a much larger surface area for the enzyme lipase to act on, so fat is digested faster.
  • It is alkaline, so it neutralises the acidic food coming from the stomach, providing the right conditions for the intestinal and pancreatic enzymes.
🌟 Remember: bile emulsifies (physically breaks up) fat — it does not chemically digest it. The enzyme lipase does the actual digestion.

5. Summary — chemical digestion region by region

  • Mouth: salivary amylase starts starch → maltose.
  • Stomach: pepsin (in acid) starts protein → peptides; rennin curdles milk.
  • Duodenum: bile emulsifies fat; pancreatic amylase, trypsin and lipase continue digestion.
  • Ileum: maltase, sucrase, lactase and peptidase complete digestion to glucose, other sugars and amino acids — then absorption takes place.

🌿 PART 4 — Absorption & Modes of Nutrition

1. Absorption in the ileum

Absorption is the passage of the small, soluble products of digestion from the gut into the blood (and lymph). This happens mainly in the ileum (the second part of the small intestine).

The villi — built for absorption

The inner wall of the ileum is folded into millions of tiny finger-like projections called villi (singular: villus). They are perfectly adapted to absorb food quickly:

  • Very many, finger-like — this gives an enormous surface area for absorption.
  • Thin wall (one cell thick) — so food passes through easily and quickly.
  • Rich blood supply (blood capillaries) — glucose and amino acids are absorbed into the blood.
  • Each villus contains a lacteal (a tiny lymph vessel) — this absorbs the digested fats (fatty acids and glycerol).
🌟 Absorption happens in the ileum; the large intestine mainly absorbs water — do not mix them up.

2. Assimilation and egestion

  • Assimilation: the use of absorbed food by the body cells — for example, glucose is used in respiration to release energy, and amino acids are built into new proteins for growth.
  • Egestion (defaecation): the removal of undigested food (faeces) through the anus. Note: this is different from excretion, which removes waste made by the body's own reactions.

3. Modes of nutrition

Living things feed in two broad ways:

A. Autotrophic nutrition (self-feeding)

The organism makes its own food from simple raw materials. Green plants do this by photosynthesis (using light and chlorophyll). Autotrophs are the producers of food.

B. Heterotrophic nutrition (feeding on others)

The organism cannot make its own food and must take in ready-made organic food. There are three types:

  • Holozoic: taking in solid organic food, then digesting and absorbing it — the way animals (including humans) feed.
  • Saprophytic: feeding on dead and decaying organic matter — e.g. many fungi (mushrooms, Rhizopus) and bacteria. They are important decomposers.
  • Parasitic: feeding on or inside a living host, harming it — e.g. tapeworm, roundworm, Plasmodium (malaria).

4. Photosynthesis — how green plants make food

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants use light energy (trapped by the green pigment chlorophyll) to make glucose from carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen.

6CO₂ + 6H₂O  —(light + chlorophyll)→  C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

In words: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen. The raw materials are carbon dioxide (from the air) and water (from the soil); the essential conditions are sunlight and chlorophyll.

🌟 Notice: photosynthesis is the reverse of respiration. Photosynthesis stores energy in glucose and gives out oxygen; respiration releases that energy and gives out carbon dioxide.
