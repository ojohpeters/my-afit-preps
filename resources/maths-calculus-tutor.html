🗺️ The Lesson Plan (≈ 90-minute call)

Before you start

  • Ask: “Have you done indices/laws of powers?” Differentiation is impossible without confidence in xⁿ, x⁻¹ = 1/x, x^½ = √x. If shaky, spend 5 minutes here first.
  • Have both files open: send him the Student Guide so he follows the same examples on his screen.
  • Golden teaching rule: you do one, he does the next. Never do more than two examples before he tries one.

Suggested running order & timing

If he is doing Further Maths, add a second session for substitution, by parts, implicit & parametric (Student Guide → “Further Maths Extras” tab).

The one big idea to anchor everything

Say this early: “Differentiation and integration are opposites — one breaks a quantity down into its rate of change, the other builds it back up. If you can differentiate, you can integrate by running the steps backwards.”

Keep returning to this. When you teach integration, literally show him a derivative and then reverse it on screen so he sees they undo each other.

How to know he actually understands (not just copying)

  • Change ONE number in your example and ask him to redo it.
  • Ask him to explain the step back to you (“why did the 7 disappear?”).
  • Give a deliberately wrong line and ask “is this right?” — see if he catches it.

🎤 Concept-by-Concept Teaching Scripts

1. What differentiation MEANS (don’t skip this)

Open with: “When you drive, the speedometer shows how fast your distance is changing each second — that is differentiation. We’re measuring how steep a graph is at one exact point.”
Analogy to use: steepness of a hill. Flat ground = gradient 0; steep climb = big gradient; going downhill = negative gradient.
Check question: “If a graph is a flat horizontal line, what is its gradient?” (Answer: 0 — this primes him for why constants differentiate to 0.)

2. The power rule — teach it as a 2-step chant

Say: “Two moves: (1) bring the power down to the front, (2) take one off the power. That’s it. Say it with me.”

Do live: x³ → 3x². Then 5x⁴ → 20x³. Then ask HIM: 2x⁶ → ? (expect 12x⁵).

Watch for these errors:
  • Forgetting to multiply the coefficient (writes x² instead of 6x for 3x²).
  • Thinking d/dx of a constant is the constant (it’s 0).
  • Thinking d/dx of x is 0 (it’s 1).
  • With 1/x and √x: not rewriting as powers first. Always rewrite 1/x = x⁻¹ and √x = x^½ before differentiating.
Check questions: differentiate (a) 4x³ (b) 9 (c) x (d) 1/x². Answers: 12x², 0, 1, −2/x³.

3. Term-by-term differentiation

Say: “A long expression is just several small ones added. Differentiate each piece on its own and keep the + and − signs.”

Live example: y = x³ + 2x² − 5x + 7 → 3x² + 4x − 5. Emphasise the 7 vanishing.

His turn: y = 2x³ − 4x² + x − 6 → 6x² − 8x + 1.

4. Chain rule (the “bracket” rule)

Say: “When there’s a bracket raised to a power, do the normal power rule on the bracket, then multiply by the derivative of what’s inside. Outside, then inside.”

Live: (2x+3)⁵ → 5(2x+3)⁴ × 2 = 10(2x+3)⁴. Circle the inside derivative (the 2) so he sees where it comes from.

Top error: forgetting the “× inside derivative.” Students write 5(2x+3)⁴ and stop. Drill the extra multiply.
His turn: (3x−1)⁴ → 4(3x−1)³ × 3 = 12(3x−1)³.

5. Product & quotient rules (only if he’s solid on the above)

Product: “u·v differentiates to u·v′ + v·u′ — ‘this times derivative of that, plus that times derivative of this.’”
Quotient: “(bottom × derivative of top − top × derivative of bottom) ÷ bottom².” Have him memorise the chant.
Watch: sign order in the quotient rule (it’s vu′ − uv′, NOT uv′ − vu′). Getting the order wrong flips the sign.

6. Turning points (max/min) — the exam favourite

Say: “At the top of a hill or bottom of a valley the curve is flat for an instant, so set dy/dx = 0 to FIND the points. Then the second derivative tells you which: positive = valley (min), negative = hill (max).”
Use the smiley/sad-face trick: min = smiley (∪, second derivative +), max = sad (∩, second derivative −).

Live: y = x³ − 3x → dy/dx = 3x² − 3 = 0 → x = ±1; d²y/dx² = 6x → x=1 min, x=−1 max.

Watch: solving 3x² − 3 = 0 — students forget x can be ±1, not just +1. Also forgetting to test with the second derivative.
His turn: y = x² − 4x + 1 → dy/dx = 2x − 4 = 0 → x = 2; second derivative = 2 (positive) → minimum.

7. Integration — sell it as “undo”

Say: “Integration reverses the power rule: instead of ‘down and minus one,’ it’s ‘add one to the power, then divide by the new power.’ And because reversing isn’t unique, we add + C.”

Show the undo live: differentiate x³ → 3x²; now integrate 3x² → x³. Let him watch them cancel.

The #1 lost mark: forgetting + C on indefinite integrals. Make him say “plus C” out loud every time until it’s automatic.
His turn: ∫(4x³ + 2x) dx → x⁴ + x² + C.

8. Definite integrals & area

Say: “With limits, integrate as normal, then plug in the top number, plug in the bottom number, and subtract. No + C — it cancels.”

Live: ∫₀² x² dx = [x³/3]₀² = 8/3 − 0 = 8/3. Stress the square brackets and the subtraction.

Watch: subtracting in the wrong order (must be top − bottom), and area below the x-axis coming out negative — mention they may need to take the modulus.

✍️ Practice Set (with full solutions)

Use these live or assign as homework. Click each “Show solution” to reveal the full worked answer. Sections go from easy → exam-standard.

A. Differentiation basics

1Differentiate y = 4x³ − 2x² + 7x − 9.
Show solution
dy/dx = 12x² − 4x + 7
(power rule each term; the −9 vanishes.)
2Differentiate y = 5/x² (hint: rewrite as 5x⁻²).
Show solution
y = 5x⁻² → dy/dx = −10x⁻³ = −10/x³
3Differentiate y = √x + 3x (hint: √x = x^½).
Show solution
dy/dx = ½x^(−½) + 3 = 1/(2√x) + 3

B. Chain, product & quotient

4Differentiate y = (3x + 2)⁴.
Show solution
dy/dx = 4(3x+2)³ × 3 = 12(3x+2)³
5Differentiate y = x²(2x − 1) using the product rule.
Show solutionu = x², v = 2x−1, u′ = 2x, v′ = 2.
dy/dx = x²(2) + (2x−1)(2x) = 2x² + 4x² − 2x = 6x² − 2x
6Differentiate y = (x + 1)/(x − 1) using the quotient rule.
Show solutionu = x+1, v = x−1, u′ = 1, v′ = 1.
dy/dx = [(x−1)(1) − (x+1)(1)] / (x−1)²
= (x−1−x−1)/(x−1)² = −2/(x−1)²

C. Applications

7Find the gradient of y = 2x² − 5x + 1 at x = 3.
Show solution
dy/dx = 4x − 5 → at x=3: 12 − 5 = 7
8Find the equation of the tangent to y = x² + 1 at (2, 5).
Show solution
dy/dx = 2x → m = 4 at x=2
y − 5 = 4(x − 2) → y = 4x − 3
9Find and classify the turning points of y = x³ − 12x.
Show solution
dy/dx = 3x² − 12 = 0 → x² = 4 → x = ±2
d²y/dx² = 6x → x=2: +12 = MIN; x=−2: −12 = MAX
y-values: at x=2, y = 8−24 = −16 (min); at x=−2, y = −8+24 = 16 (max)
10A particle’s displacement is s = 2t³ − 3t² + 4t. Find its velocity and acceleration at t = 2.
Show solution
v = ds/dt = 6t² − 6t + 4 → at t=2: 24 − 12 + 4 = 16 units/s
a = dv/dt = 12t − 6 → at t=2: 24 − 6 = 18 units/s²

D. Integration

11Evaluate ∫ (6x² − 4x + 5) dx.
Show solution
= 2x³ − 2x² + 5x + C
12Evaluate ∫ (x³ + 1/x²) dx.
Show solutionRewrite 1/x² = x⁻².
= x⁴/4 + x⁻¹/(−1) + C = x⁴/4 − 1/x + C
13Evaluate the definite integral ∫₁² (3x²) dx.
Show solution
∫3x² dx = x³ → [x³]₁² = 8 − 1 = 7
14Find the area under y = x² + 1 between x = 0 and x = 3.
Show solution
∫₀³ (x²+1) dx = [x³/3 + x]₀³ = (9 + 3) − 0 = 12 square units
15Given dy/dx = 3x² − 2 and the curve passes through (1, 4), find y.
Show solution
y = x³ − 2x + C
4 = 1 − 2 + C → C = 5 → y = x³ − 2x + 5

E. Further Maths stretch (optional)

16∫ 2x(x² + 5)³ dx (substitution).
Show solutionLet u = x² + 5, du = 2x dx.
= ∫u³ du = u⁴/4 + C = (x² + 5)⁴/4 + C
17∫ x·eˣ dx (by parts).
Show solutionu = x (du = dx), dv = eˣ dx (v = eˣ).
= x·eˣ − ∫eˣ dx = x·eˣ − eˣ + C = eˣ(x − 1) + C
18Find dy/dx for x² + y² = 25 (implicit).
Show solution
2x + 2y(dy/dx) = 0 → dy/dx = −x/y

Application checklist

  • Gradient at a point: find dy/dx, substitute x.
  • Tangent: m = dy/dx; use y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). Normal: slope = −1/m.
  • Turning points: dy/dx = 0 to find them; d²y/dx² sign to classify (+ min, − max).
  • Motion: v = ds/dt, a = dv/dt. Reverse with integration (s = ∫v dt).
  • Area under curve: ∫ₐᵇ y dx (split if it dips below the x-axis).

Top 6 mistakes to catch on the call

  1. Dropping the coefficient in the power rule.
  2. Saying d/dx(constant) = constant (it’s 0).
  3. Chain rule: forgetting “× inside derivative.”
  4. Quotient rule sign order (vu′ − uv′).
  5. Forgetting + C on indefinite integrals.
  6. Definite integral: subtracting bottom − top instead of top − bottom.
