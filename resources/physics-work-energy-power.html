🏋️ PART 1 — Work

1. What is "work" in physics?

In everyday life "work" means any effort. In physics it is much stricter: work is done only when a force moves its point of application through a distance.

Work done = force × distance moved in the direction of the force. If you push and nothing moves, or you push at right angles to the motion, then in physics you have done no work, no matter how tired you feel.

The formula
W = F × d
  • W = work done, measured in joules (J)
  • F = force, measured in newtons (N)
  • d = distance moved in the direction of the force, in metres (m)

1 joule is the work done when a force of 1 newton moves a body through 1 metre in the direction of the force. So 1 J = 1 N·m.

🌟 Memory: no movement in the direction of the force → no work. Work needs both a force AND a distance moved along that force.

2. Straight-line work — worked examples

Example 1: A force of 20 N pushes a box 5 m along the floor in the direction of the push. Find the work done.
W = F × d = 20 × 5
W = 100 J
Answer = 100 J.
Example 2: A boy drags a bag with a horizontal force of 15 N through a distance of 8 m. Find the work done.
W = F × d = 15 × 8 = 120 J
Answer = 120 J.
Example 3 — find the force. A machine does 600 J of work moving a load 4 m. What force did it use?
F = W ÷ d = 600 ÷ 4 = 150 N
Answer = 150 N. (Rearrange W = F × d to get F = W ÷ d.)

3. Work when the force is at an angle

Sometimes the force is not along the direction of motion — for example, pulling a box with a rope tilted upwards. Only the part of the force along the motion does work. That part is F·cosθ, where θ is the angle between the force and the direction of motion.

W = F × d × cosθ

When θ = 0° (force along the motion), cos0° = 1, so W = F·d — the ordinary formula. As the angle grows, cosθ shrinks, so less work is done.

Worked example: A rope pulls a load 6 m with a force of 40 N at 60° to the ground. (cos60° = 0.5) Find the work done.
W = F × d × cosθ = 40 × 6 × cos60°
W = 40 × 6 × 0.5 = 120 J
Answer = 120 J.
Worked example: A force of 100 N drags a crate 10 m at 0° (straight along the floor). Find the work done.
cos0° = 1, so W = 100 × 10 × 1 = 1000 J
Answer = 1000 J — the angle formula agrees with W = F·d when θ = 0°.

4. When is the work ZERO?

There are three important cases where the work done is zero:

  • No displacement. You push very hard against a wall but it does not move (d = 0), so W = F × 0 = 0. No work is done, even though you get tired.
  • Force perpendicular (90°) to the motion. cos90° = 0, so W = F·d·cos90° = 0. Example: a man carrying a bag walks horizontally — his lifting force is vertical (upward) but his motion is horizontal, so that force does no work.
  • No force at all. A body coasting in space with no force acting has nothing to do work.
🌟 A satellite going round the Earth in a circle does NO work against gravity — gravity pulls towards the centre while motion is sideways (90°), so cos90° = 0.

5. Work done against gravity = mgh

To lift a body upwards you must push against its weight. The weight of a body is W = mg (mass × gravity). To raise it a height h, the work done against gravity is:

Work against gravity = m × g × h
  • m = mass in kg, g = 10 m/s² (acceleration of gravity), h = height raised in m
Worked example: How much work is done lifting a 5 kg block through a height of 3 m? (g = 10 m/s²)
W = mgh = 5 × 10 × 3
W = 150 J
Answer = 150 J.
Worked example: A boy of mass 40 kg climbs stairs of vertical height 5 m. Find the work he does against gravity. (g = 10 m/s²)
W = mgh = 40 × 10 × 5 = 2000 J
Answer = 2000 J. (Only the VERTICAL height counts, not the length of the stairs.)
🌟 Note: this work against gravity is exactly the gravitational potential energy the body gains — you will meet mgh again in the next tab.

⚡ PART 2 — Energy (Kinetic & Potential)

1. What is energy?

Energy is the capacity (ability) to do work. Anything that can do work has energy, and the more energy it has, the more work it can do. Energy is measured in the same unit as work — the joule (J).

Work and energy are two sides of the same coin: when a force does work on a body, it transfers energy to it. When a body does work, it loses energy.

🌟 Think of energy as "stored ability to push things". Petrol has it, a raised hammer has it, a moving car has it, a charged battery has it.

2. The forms of energy

Energy comes in many forms. The main ones you must know are:

  • Kinetic energy — energy of a MOVING body (a car, a bullet, running water).
  • Potential energy — stored energy due to position or shape (a raised load, a stretched spring).
  • Chemical energy — stored in fuel, food and batteries; released in reactions.
  • Electrical energy — energy carried by moving charges (electric current).
  • Heat (thermal) energy — energy of hot bodies due to particle motion.
  • Light energy — carried by light waves.
  • Sound energy — carried by sound waves.
  • Nuclear energy — stored in the nucleus, released by fission or fusion.
🌟 Mechanical energy = kinetic energy + potential energy. It is the "energy of movement and position" and is the type used most in work-energy problems.

3. Kinetic energy — energy of motion

A body of mass m moving with speed v has kinetic energy:

K.E. = ½ m v²

Notice the speed is squared. This is very important: if you double the speed, the K.E. becomes four times as large (2² = 4); if you triple the speed, K.E. is nine times as large.

Worked example 1: Find the kinetic energy of a 2 kg body moving at 4 m/s.
K.E. = ½ × m × v² = ½ × 2 × 4²
= ½ × 2 × 16 = 16 J
Answer = 16 J.
Worked example 2: A 10 kg trolley moves at 6 m/s. Find its kinetic energy.
K.E. = ½ × 10 × 6² = ½ × 10 × 36
= 5 × 36 = 180 J
Answer = 180 J.
Worked example 3 — the speed doubles. A car of mass 800 kg speeds up from 10 m/s to 20 m/s. Compare the kinetic energies.
At 10 m/s: K.E. = ½ × 800 × 10² = 40 000 J
At 20 m/s: K.E. = ½ × 800 × 20² = 160 000 J
Doubling the speed made the K.E. 4 times bigger (40 000 → 160 000 J).

4. Gravitational potential energy — energy of height

A body raised to a height h above the ground has stored energy because gravity can pull it back down and do work. This is gravitational potential energy:

P.E. = m g h
  • m = mass (kg), g = 10 m/s², h = height above the reference level (m)
Worked example 1: Find the potential energy of a 5 kg object 10 m above the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)
P.E. = mgh = 5 × 10 × 10 = 500 J
Answer = 500 J.
Worked example 2: A 2 kg mass is lifted onto a shelf 20 m high. Find its gravitational P.E. (g = 10 m/s²)
P.E. = mgh = 2 × 10 × 20 = 400 J
Answer = 400 J.
Worked example 3 — find the height. A 4 kg object has 800 J of potential energy. How high is it? (g = 10 m/s²)
h = P.E. ÷ (mg) = 800 ÷ (4 × 10) = 800 ÷ 40 = 20 m
Answer = 20 m.

5. Elastic potential energy

Potential energy can also be stored by changing the shape of an elastic body. When you stretch or compress a spring (or bend a bow, or stretch a catapult), you store elastic potential energy in it.

When released, that stored energy is given back — the spring springs back, the catapult fires the stone. A stretched rubber band, a wound-up clockwork spring and a drawn bow all store elastic potential energy.

🌟 Gravitational P.E. depends on HEIGHT; elastic P.E. depends on how far the elastic body is stretched or squashed from its natural shape.

♻️ PART 3 — Conservation & Transformation of Energy

1. The principle of conservation of energy

This is one of the most important laws in physics:

Energy can neither be created nor destroyed —
it can only be changed (transformed) from one form to another.

This means the total amount of energy in a closed system stays the same. When energy seems to "disappear", it has really just changed into another form (often heat). When it seems to "appear", it has come from some other store.

🌟 Energy is never lost or made — it only changes its "costume". Petrol → motion, food → muscle work, sunlight → plant growth: the total is always conserved.

2. Energy transformations — everyday examples

In every device energy simply changes from one form to another:

Device / eventEnergy change
Falling bodyPotential → Kinetic
Battery / dry cellChemical → Electrical
Electric bulbElectrical → Light (+ heat)
Electric motor / fanElectrical → Kinetic (mechanical)
Generator / dynamoKinetic (mechanical) → Electrical
LoudspeakerElectrical → Sound
MicrophoneSound → Electrical
Solar cellLight → Electrical
Car engineChemical (petrol) → Kinetic (+ heat)
Electric heaterElectrical → Heat
🌟 To answer these questions, ask "what goes IN?" and "what comes OUT?" — that gives the energy change, e.g. a torch: chemical (battery) → electrical → light.

3. PE → KE for a falling or sliding body

When a body falls, it loses height so its potential energy falls, but it speeds up so its kinetic energy rises by the same amount. In the absence of friction, the P.E. lost equals the K.E. gained:

P.E. lost = K.E. gained  ⇒  m g h = ½ m v²

The mass m cancels from both sides, giving a neat formula for the speed after falling a height h:

gh = ½v²  ⇒  v² = 2gh  ⇒  v = √(2gh)
Worked example 1: A stone is dropped from a height of 20 m. Find its speed just before it hits the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)
v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 10 × 20)
v = √400 = 20 m/s
Answer = 20 m/s.
Worked example 2: A body falls from rest through 5 m. Find its speed on reaching the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)
v = √(2 × 10 × 5) = √100 = 10 m/s
Answer = 10 m/s.
Worked example 3 — using energy amounts. A 2 kg ball is dropped from 20 m. Show that the K.E. at the bottom equals the P.E. at the top. (g = 10 m/s²)
P.E. at top = mgh = 2 × 10 × 20 = 400 J
Speed at bottom v = √(2×10×20) = 20 m/s
K.E. at bottom = ½ × 2 × 20² = ½ × 2 × 400 = 400 J
The 400 J of P.E. became 400 J of K.E. — energy is conserved.

4. Conservation of mechanical energy

For a body moving freely under gravity only (no friction, no air resistance), the sum of its kinetic and potential energy stays constant:

K.E. + P.E. = constant (total mechanical energy)

As a ball rises, K.E. turns into P.E. (it slows down); as it falls, P.E. turns back into K.E. (it speeds up). At every point the total K.E. + P.E. is the same. At the very top of the flight the speed is zero, so all the energy is potential; at the bottom it is all kinetic.

🌟 A swinging pendulum shows this beautifully: fastest (all K.E.) at the lowest point, momentarily still (all P.E.) at the two highest points — trading energy back and forth.

5. Energy "lost" to friction and heat

In real life, machines and moving bodies meet friction and air resistance. These do not destroy energy — the "lost" energy is turned into heat (and a little sound). That is why brakes, engines and rubbing surfaces get hot.

So the conservation law still holds, but we must write:

Energy input = useful output + energy wasted as heat/sound

Because some energy is always wasted, no real machine is 100% efficient — which leads us straight to the next tab, Power & Efficiency.

🚀 PART 4 — Power & Efficiency

1. What is power?

Two machines may do the same amount of work, but one may do it much faster. Power tells us how FAST work is done (or how fast energy is used):

Power = work done ÷ time  =  energy ÷ time
P = W ÷ t
  • P = power, measured in watts (W)
  • W = work done or energy used, in joules (J)
  • t = time taken, in seconds (s)

1 watt is a rate of working of 1 joule per second (1 W = 1 J/s). A larger unit is the kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W.

🌟 Same job, less time → more power. A strong engine lifts the same load in fewer seconds, so it develops more power.

2. Worked power examples

Example 1: A machine does 600 J of work in 30 s. Find its power.
P = W ÷ t = 600 ÷ 30 = 20 W
Answer = 20 W.
Example 2 — pump lifting water. A pump raises 100 kg of water through a height of 10 m in 20 s. Find its power. (g = 10 m/s²)
Work done = mgh = 100 × 10 × 10 = 10 000 J
P = W ÷ t = 10 000 ÷ 20 = 500 W
Answer = 500 W.
Example 3 — a person climbing. A boy of mass 50 kg runs up stairs of height 4 m in 5 s. Find his power. (g = 10 m/s²)
Work = mgh = 50 × 10 × 4 = 2000 J
P = 2000 ÷ 5 = 400 W
Answer = 400 W.

3. The second power formula: P = F × v

When a steady force F moves a body at a steady speed v, the power can be found directly from force and speed:

P = F × v

This comes from P = W/t = (F × d)/t, and since d/t = v (speed), we get P = F × v.

Worked example: A car engine exerts a driving force of 200 N while moving at a steady 5 m/s. Find the power developed.
P = F × v = 200 × 5 = 1000 W
Answer = 1000 W (= 1 kW).
Worked example — find the force. A motor develops 3000 W while moving a belt at 6 m/s. Find the force.
F = P ÷ v = 3000 ÷ 6 = 500 N
Answer = 500 N.

4. Efficiency of a machine

No real machine turns ALL its input energy into useful output — some is always wasted as heat and sound. Efficiency tells us what fraction of the input actually comes out as useful work:

Efficiency = (useful output energy ÷ total input energy) × 100%

You can also use power instead of energy: Efficiency = (useful output power ÷ input power) × 100%. Efficiency has no unit — it is a percentage, and it is always less than 100% for a real machine.

🌟 Rule: efficiency can never be more than 100%. If a calculation gives more than 100%, you have divided the wrong way round — put the SMALLER (useful output) number on top.

5. Worked efficiency examples

Example 1: A machine is supplied with 500 J and gives out 400 J of useful energy. Find its efficiency.
Efficiency = (useful output ÷ input) × 100%
= (400 ÷ 500) × 100% = 80%
Answer = 80%.
Example 2: An electric motor takes in 2000 J of electrical energy and produces 1500 J of useful mechanical energy. Find its efficiency.
Efficiency = (1500 ÷ 2000) × 100% = 75%
Answer = 75%.
Example 3 — find the wasted energy. A machine that is 60% efficient is given 1000 J. How much useful energy comes out, and how much is wasted?
Useful output = 60% of 1000 = 0.6 × 1000 = 600 J
Wasted = 1000 − 600 = 400 J (as heat/sound)
Answer = 600 J useful, 400 J wasted.
