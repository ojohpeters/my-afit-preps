📐 PART 1 — Trig Ratios: SOHCAHTOA

1. Labelling a right-angled triangle

Trigonometry starts with the right-angled triangle (one angle is exactly 90°). Pick one of the other angles and call it θ (theta). Now name the three sides relative to θ:

  • Hypotenuse — the longest side, always opposite the right angle. It never changes.
  • Opposite — the side facing the angle θ (it does not touch θ).
  • Adjacent — the side next to θ that is NOT the hypotenuse.
🌟 Memory: the hypotenuse is fixed, but "opposite" and "adjacent" swap depending on which angle you call θ. Always mark θ first, then label.

2. The three ratios — SOHCAHTOA

For a given angle θ, the ratio of two sides is always the same, no matter how big the triangle is. There are three key ratios, remembered by the word SOHCAHTOA:

SOH · CAH · TOA
sin θ = Opposite ÷ Hypotenuse
cos θ = Adjacent ÷ Hypotenuse
tan θ = Opposite ÷ Adjacent

SOH = Sine is Opposite over Hypotenuse. CAH = Cosine is Adjacent over Hypotenuse. TOA = Tangent is Opposite over Adjacent.

Worked example: In a right triangle the side opposite θ = 3, the adjacent = 4, and the hypotenuse = 5. Find all three ratios.
sin θ = opp ÷ hyp = 3 ÷ 5 = 0.6
cos θ = adj ÷ hyp = 4 ÷ 5 = 0.8
tan θ = opp ÷ adj = 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75

3. Pythagoras' theorem — finding a side without angles

If you know two sides of a right triangle you can find the third with Pythagoras:

hypotenuse² = a² + b²  (the two shorter sides squared and added)
Worked example: The two short sides are 6 and 8. Find the hypotenuse.
hyp² = 6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100
hyp = √100 = 10
The hypotenuse is 10.
Finding a shorter side: hypotenuse = 13, one side = 5. Find the other side.
other² = 13² − 5² = 169 − 25 = 144
other = √144 = 12
This is the famous 5–12–13 triangle.
🌟 Learn two triangles by heart: 3–4–5 and 5–12–13. Their multiples (6–8–10, 9–12–15…) turn up all the time.

4. Finding a missing SIDE using a ratio

When you know an angle and one side, pick the ratio that links the side you know to the side you want.

Worked example: A right triangle has hypotenuse 10 and an angle of 30°. Find the side opposite the 30° angle.
We know hyp and want opp → use sin (SOH)
opp = hyp × sin30° = 10 × 0.5 = 5
The opposite side is 5.
Ladder example: An 8 m ladder leans against a wall at 60° to the ground. How high up the wall does it reach?
Height reached = 8 × sin60° = 8 × 0.866 ≈ 6.93 m
The ladder reaches about 6.9 m up the wall.

5. Finding a missing ANGLE (inverse ratios)

If you know two sides, you can find the angle by reversing the ratio with the inverse buttons sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹.

Worked example: The opposite side = 3 and the hypotenuse = 6. Find θ.
sin θ = opp ÷ hyp = 3 ÷ 6 = 0.5
θ = sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°
Using tan: opposite = 4, adjacent = 4. Find θ.
tan θ = 4 ÷ 4 = 1
θ = tan⁻¹(1) = 45°
🌟 Rule of thumb: to find a side you multiply by the ratio; to find an angle you use the inverse (sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹).

🔺 PART 2 — Special Angles & Identities

1. The exact-value table (memorise this!)

For 0°, 30°, 45°, 60° and 90° the ratios have exact values — you can write them without a calculator. These come up constantly, so learn the table:

θ0°30°45°60°90°
sin θ01/2√2/2√3/21
cos θ1√3/2√2/21/20
tan θ01/√31√3undefined

Key ones to fix in memory: sin30° = 1/2, cos30° = √3/2, tan45° = 1, sin60° = √3/2, tan60° = √3.

2. The easy way to remember the sine row

Write the numbers 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 for the angles 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°. Take the square root and divide by 2:

sin θ = √0/2, √1/2, √2/2, √3/2, √4/2
= 0,  1/2,  √2/2,  √3/2,  1

The cosine row is the sine row backwards (cos of an angle = sin of its complement), so cos0° = 1, cos30° = √3/2, cos45° = √2/2, cos60° = 1/2, cos90° = 0.

🌟 Notice sin30° = cos60° and sin60° = cos30°. Angles that add to 90° swap their sine and cosine.

3. Two identities you must know

Identity 1 — Pythagorean identity
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1  (true for EVERY angle)
Check at 30°:
sin²30° + cos²30° = (1/2)² + (√3/2)² = 1/4 + 3/4 = 1 ✓
Identity 2 — the tangent identity
tan θ = sin θ ÷ cos θ
Check at 60°:
tan60° = sin60° ÷ cos60° = (√3/2) ÷ (1/2) = √3 ✓

4. Evaluating expressions without a calculator

Example 1: Evaluate sin30° + cos60°.
= 1/2 + 1/2 = 1
Example 2: Evaluate cos²45°.
cos45° = √2/2, so cos²45° = (√2/2)² = 2/4 = 1/2
Example 3: Evaluate sin60° × cos30°.
= (√3/2) × (√3/2) = 3/4
Example 4: Evaluate tan45° + tan60°.
= 1 + √3 ≈ 1 + 1.732 = 2.732
🌟 Exam tip: whenever a question gives a "nice" angle (30°, 45°, 60°), reach for the exact-value table instead of a calculator.

🗼 PART 3 — Angles of Elevation & Depression

1. The two definitions

  • Angle of elevation — the angle you look UP through, measured from the horizontal to an object above you (e.g. looking up to the top of a tower).
  • Angle of depression — the angle you look DOWN through, measured from the horizontal to an object below you (e.g. looking down from a cliff to a boat).
🌟 Both angles are measured from a HORIZONTAL line, never from the vertical. Elevation = eyes go up; depression = eyes go down.

2. A useful fact — they are equal

The horizontal line at your eye and the horizontal line at the object are parallel. So the angle of elevation from A up to B equals the angle of depression from B down to A — they are equal alternate angles.

Elevation (A → B) = Depression (B → A)

3. Setting up the right triangle

Almost every elevation/depression problem is a right triangle:

  • the horizontal distance along the ground is the adjacent side,
  • the height (of tower, tree, cliff) is the opposite side,
  • so the ratio to use is nearly always tan θ = height ÷ distance.
height = distance × tan(angle)  ·  distance = height ÷ tan(angle)

4. Worked height & distance problems

Example 1 — find a height. A man stands 50 m from the foot of a tower. The angle of elevation to the top is 30°. Find the height.
height = distance × tan30° = 50 × 0.5774 ≈ 28.9 m
The tower is about 28.9 m tall.
Example 2 — a 45° special case. From a point 30 m from a mast the elevation to the top is 45°.
height = 30 × tan45° = 30 × 1 = 30 m
At 45° the height equals the horizontal distance.
Example 3 — angle of depression. From the top of a cliff 40 m high, the angle of depression to a boat is 45°. How far is the boat from the base?
distance = height ÷ tan45° = 40 ÷ 1 = 40 m
The boat is 40 m away.
Example 4 — a steeper look. A pole is viewed from 20 m away with an elevation of 60°.
height = 20 × tan60° = 20 × 1.732 ≈ 34.6 m
The pole is about 34.6 m tall.

5. A quick link to bearings

Elevation/depression are angles in a vertical plane; bearings are angles in a horizontal plane, measured clockwise from North as a three-figure number (e.g. 060°, 135°, 245°). Many exam questions combine them: use bearings to draw the ground plan (a top view) and elevation/depression to work out heights. The same SOHCAHTOA and sine/cosine rules solve both.

🌟 Bearing = which way (compass, flat ground). Elevation/depression = how steeply up or down (side view).

📏 PART 4 — Sine Rule, Cosine Rule & Area

1. Naming sides and angles

In any triangle ABC (it does NOT have to be right-angled), each side is named with the small letter of the angle opposite it: side a is opposite angle A, side b opposite B, side c opposite C. These two rules handle triangles with no right angle.

2. The sine rule

a / sinA = b / sinB = c / sinC

Each side divided by the sine of its opposite angle gives the same value. Use it in pairs (two fractions at a time).

When to use the sine rule
  • You know two angles and any one side (and want another side), or
  • You know two sides and an angle opposite one of them (and want the other angle).
Worked example: In triangle ABC, a = 12, A = 30° and B = 90°. Find side b.
b ÷ sinB = a ÷ sinA
b = a × sinB ÷ sinA = 12 × sin90° ÷ sin30°
= 12 × 1 ÷ 0.5 = 24
So b = 24.

3. The cosine rule

a² = b² + c² − 2bc·cosA

This is like Pythagoras but with a correction term −2bc·cosA for the non-right angle A. (If A = 90° then cosA = 0 and it collapses back to a² = b² + c².) To find an angle from three sides, rearrange it:

cosA = (b² + c² − a²) ÷ (2bc)
When to use the cosine rule
  • You know two sides and the angle BETWEEN them (included angle) and want the third side, or
  • You know all three sides and want an angle.
Worked example (find a side): b = 8, c = 5, included angle A = 60°. Find a.
a² = 8² + 5² − 2×8×5×cos60°
= 64 + 25 − 80 × 0.5 = 89 − 40 = 49
a = √49 = 7
So a = 7.
Worked example (find an angle): a = 7, b = 5, c = 8. Find angle A.
cosA = (5² + 8² − 7²) ÷ (2×5×8) = (25 + 64 − 49) ÷ 80 = 40 ÷ 80 = 0.5
A = cos⁻¹(0.5) = 60°

4. Area of a triangle

When you know two sides and the angle between them, the area is:

Area = ½ · a · b · sinC

(Two sides times the sine of the included angle, all halved. You may also write ½·b·c·sinA or ½·a·c·sinB — pick whichever angle sits between the two known sides.)

Worked example: a = 6, b = 8, included angle C = 30°. Find the area.
Area = ½ × 6 × 8 × sin30°
= 24 × 0.5 = 12 square units

5. Choosing the right tool — a summary

What you knowUse
Right-angled triangle, one side + one angleSOHCAHTOA
Right-angled triangle, two sides (no angle)Pythagoras
Two angles + one sideSine rule
Two sides + a non-included (opposite) angleSine rule
Two sides + the included angleCosine rule
All three sides (want an angle)Cosine rule
Two sides + included angle (want area)Area = ½ab·sinC
🌟 Quick decider: a right angle → SOHCAHTOA/Pythagoras. No right angle: if an angle sits BETWEEN two known sides use the cosine rule, otherwise use the sine rule.
