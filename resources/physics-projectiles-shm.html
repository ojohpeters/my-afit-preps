🎯 PART 1 — Projectile Basics & The Three Master Formulas

1. What is a projectile?

A projectile is any object that is thrown, kicked or fired into the air and then moves under the force of gravity alone (we ignore air resistance). Examples: a kicked football, a thrown stone, a bullet leaving a gun, water from a hose.

The one big idea — the two motions are independent

Projectile motion is really two separate motions happening at the same time:

  • Horizontal motion: there is no horizontal force, so the horizontal velocity is constant and the horizontal acceleration is zero.
  • Vertical motion: gravity pulls down, so there is a constant downward acceleration g (= 10 m/s²). The vertical motion is exactly like free fall / a ball thrown straight up.

Because one direction speeds up and the other stays steady, the path traced out is a parabola (a curved arch).

🌟 Memory: sideways = cruise control (constant speed); up-and-down = gravity doing its normal free-fall thing. They never interfere with each other.

2. Splitting the launch velocity into components

An object launched at speed u at an angle θ to the ground has its velocity split into two parts:

Horizontal:  uₓ = u cosθ  (stays constant)
Vertical:  u_y = u sinθ  (changes because of g)
  • Going up, the vertical velocity decreases (gravity opposes it) until it reaches zero at the very top.
  • At the highest point the object still moves horizontally with speed u cosθ — it is not at rest.
  • Coming down, the vertical velocity grows again until it lands.

3. The three master formulas

For a projectile launched at speed u and angle θ over level ground, learn these three by heart:

(a) Time of flight — how long it stays in the air
T = 2u sinθ / g

Time to reach the top is u sinθ / g; the whole flight (up + down) is twice that.

(b) Maximum height — how high it climbs
H = u²sin²θ / 2g

Found from v² = u² − 2gH using vertical launch speed u sinθ and v = 0 at the top.

(c) Range — how far it lands horizontally
R = u²sin2θ / g

Range = horizontal speed × time of flight = (u cosθ)(2u sinθ / g). Because 2 sinθ cosθ = sin2θ, this tidies up to u²sin2θ / g.

4. Maximum range & complementary angles

Maximum range is at 45°

In R = u²sin2θ / g, the only thing that changes with angle is sin2θ. This is largest (= 1) when 2θ = 90°, i.e. θ = 45°. So for a given speed, a 45° launch goes the farthest:

R(max) = u² / g  (at θ = 45°)
Complementary angles give equal range

Two angles that add up to 90° (for example 30° and 60°, or 20° and 70°) are called complementary. They produce the same range, because sin2θ has the same value for both.

Why 30° and 60° give equal range:
30°: sin(2 × 30°) = sin60° = 0.866
60°: sin(2 × 60°) = sin120° = 0.866
Same sin2θ → same range (but the 60° shot goes higher and stays up longer).
🌟 Memory: a low, flat shot and a high, lobbed shot can land in exactly the same spot if their angles add up to 90°.

5. Worked examples

Example 1 — the full set (u = 20 m/s, θ = 30°, g = 10):
Time of flight: T = 2u sinθ / g = 2 × 20 × 0.5 / 10 = 20 / 10 = 2 s
Max height: H = u²sin²θ / 2g = 400 × (0.5)² / 20 = 400 × 0.25 / 20 = 5 m
Range: R = u²sin2θ / g = 400 × sin60° / 10 = 400 × 0.866 / 10 ≈ 34.6 m
So T = 2 s, H = 5 m, R ≈ 34.6 m.
Example 2 — a 45° shot (u = 30 m/s, g = 10):
R = u²sin2θ / g = 30² × sin90° / 10 = 900 × 1 / 10 = 90 m
Since 45° is the best angle, this is also the maximum range for a 30 m/s launch (R(max) = u²/g = 900/10 = 90 m).
Example 3 — vertical velocity at the top: A stone is thrown at 25 m/s at 40°. What is its speed at the highest point?
At the top the vertical velocity is 0, only the horizontal part remains.
Speed at top = u cosθ = 25 × cos40° = 25 × 0.766 ≈ 19.2 m/s
Answer ≈ 19.2 m/s (purely horizontal).

📐 PART 2 — Horizontal Projection & Tower Problems

1. What is horizontal projection?

This is the special case where an object is thrown horizontally (straight sideways) from a height h — like a ball rolling off a table, a stone thrown off a cliff, or a bomb dropped from a plane.

The key starting point

Because it is launched sideways, the object starts with zero vertical velocity (it is not thrown up or down). Gravity then takes over the vertical motion.

  • Vertically: it is exactly like dropping the object from rest — free fall.
  • Horizontally: it keeps its steady launch speed u the whole time.

The path is half a parabola — it curves downward as it moves forward.

2. The two working formulas

(a) Time to reach the ground (from the height only)
h = ½gt²  →  t = √(2h / g)

Notice: the fall time depends only on the height h and g — not on how fast it was thrown sideways.

(b) Horizontal range (how far from the base it lands)
Range, x = u × t

where u is the horizontal launch speed and t is the fall time from formula (a).

🌟 Two-step recipe: Step 1 find the fall time from the height. Step 2 multiply that time by the horizontal speed to get the range.

3. Two objects, same height, land together

Drop one ball straight down and, at the same instant, throw an identical ball horizontally from the same height. They hit the ground at exactly the same time.

Why? The vertical motion is identical for both (both start with zero vertical velocity and fall under the same g). The thrown ball simply travels sideways as well — but sideways motion does not change how fast it falls.

🌟 Big idea: horizontal speed does NOT affect fall time. Sideways and downwards are independent.

4. Worked examples

Example 1 — cliff problem: A stone is projected horizontally at 15 m/s from a cliff 20 m high (g = 10). Find (a) the time to land and (b) how far from the base it lands.
(a) h = ½gt² → 20 = ½ × 10 × t² = 5t² → t² = 4 → t = 2 s
(b) Range x = u × t = 15 × 2 = 30 m
Answers: t = 2 s, range = 30 m.
Example 2 — table top: A ball rolls off a table 5 m high (g = 10). How long is it in the air?
t = √(2h / g) = √(2 × 5 / 10) = √1 = 1 s
Answer = 1 s.
Example 3 — speed on landing (tower type): An object is thrown horizontally at 20 m/s from a 45 m tower (g = 10). Find its speed just before it hits the ground.
Fall time: t = √(2 × 45 / 10) = √9 = 3 s
Vertical velocity gained: v_y = g × t = 10 × 3 = 30 m/s
Horizontal velocity (unchanged): vₓ = 20 m/s
Resultant speed = √(vₓ² + v_y²) = √(20² + 30²) = √(400 + 900) = √1300 ≈ 36 m/s
Answer ≈ 36 m/s.

5. The monkey-and-hunter problem

A classic exam favourite. A hunter aims a gun straight at a monkey hanging from a branch. At the exact instant the gun fires, the monkey lets go and falls. Where should the hunter aim?

Surprising answer: aim directly at the monkey — the bullet will still hit it. During the flight, gravity pulls the bullet down by ½gt², and it pulls the falling monkey down by exactly the same ½gt² in the same time. Both drop by the same amount, so they meet.

🌟 Lesson: because horizontal and vertical motions are independent, the bullet and the monkey fall together — aiming straight at a falling target still scores a hit.

🌀 PART 3 — Simple Harmonic Motion: The Fundamentals

1. What is simple harmonic motion (SHM)?

Simple harmonic motion is a to-and-fro (back-and-forth) motion about a fixed central point, in which:

The acceleration is proportional to the displacement from a fixed point, and is always directed towards that point.

In symbols this defining condition is written:

a = −ω²x
  • x = displacement from the centre (equilibrium) position.
  • a = acceleration.
  • ω (omega) = angular frequency, a constant for the system.
  • The minus sign is the heart of SHM — it means the acceleration always points back towards the centre (a restoring effect), opposite to the displacement.

Everyday examples: a swinging pendulum, a mass bouncing on a spring, a vibrating guitar string, a loaded test-tube bobbing in water.

🌟 Memory: the further you pull it out, the harder it is pulled back — and always back towards the middle. That is SHM.

2. The key quantities

QuantityMeaningSymbol / unit
AmplitudeThe maximum displacement from the centreA (metre, m)
PeriodTime for one complete oscillation (one full cycle)T (second, s)
FrequencyNumber of complete cycles per secondf (hertz, Hz)
Angular frequencyHow fast the phase advances (2π per cycle)ω (rad/s)
The connecting formulas
f = 1 / T  ·  T = 2π / ω  ·  ω = 2πf

So period, frequency and angular frequency are just three ways of describing the same rate. Learn to switch between them.

3. Velocity and acceleration in SHM

Velocity — fastest at the centre
v = ω√(A² − x²)
  • At the centre (x = 0): v is a maximum → v(max) = ωA. The body is moving fastest as it whips through the middle.
  • At the extremes (x = ±A): v = 0. The body is momentarily at rest before turning back.
Acceleration — greatest at the extremes
a = −ω²x  →  a(max) = ω²A (at x = ±A)
  • At the extremes (x = ±A): acceleration is a maximum (biggest restoring pull).
  • At the centre (x = 0): acceleration is zero.
🌟 Opposite ends of the story: speed is greatest where acceleration is zero (the centre), and speed is zero where acceleration is greatest (the extremes).

4. Worked examples

Example 1 — period from angular frequency: A body moves with ω = 4 rad/s. Find its period and frequency.
T = 2π / ω = 2π / 4 = 1.57 s
f = 1 / T = 1 / 1.57 ≈ 0.64 Hz
Answers: T ≈ 1.57 s, f ≈ 0.64 Hz.
Example 2 — maximum speed and acceleration: A particle in SHM has ω = 2 rad/s and amplitude A = 0.5 m. Find its maximum speed and maximum acceleration.
v(max) = ωA = 2 × 0.5 = 1.0 m/s (at the centre)
a(max) = ω²A = 2² × 0.5 = 4 × 0.5 = 2 m/s² (at the extremes)
Answers: v(max) = 1.0 m/s, a(max) = 2 m/s².
Example 3 — velocity at a given point: For the same particle (ω = 2, A = 0.5 m), find its speed when x = 0.3 m.
v = ω√(A² − x²) = 2 × √(0.5² − 0.3²) = 2 × √(0.25 − 0.09)
= 2 × √0.16 = 2 × 0.4 = 0.8 m/s
Answer = 0.8 m/s.

🔩 PART 4 — SHM Systems: Pendulum, Spring & Energy

1. The simple pendulum

A simple pendulum is a small heavy bob on a light string that swings back and forth. For small swings its motion is SHM, with period:

T = 2π√(L / g)
  • L = length of the string (metre)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity
What the period depends on — and what it does NOT

The period depends only on L and g. It does NOT depend on:

  • the mass of the bob (a heavy and a light bob of the same length swing at the same rate),
  • the amplitude (for small swings), or the material of the string.

Because T is proportional to √L: to double the period you must make the string 4 times longer (2² = 4); to halve the period you use a string ¼ as long.

🌟 Longer string = slower swing. Mass makes no difference — that is why a big church bell and a small pendulum of the same length keep the same beat.

2. The mass–spring system

A mass hanging on (or attached to) a spring and set bouncing also performs SHM. Its period is:

T = 2π√(m / k)
  • m = the mass (kilogram)
  • k = the spring (force) constant — the stiffness, in newtons per metre (N/m)

So a heavier mass gives a longer period (slower bounce), while a stiffer spring (larger k) gives a shorter period (faster bounce). Note that, unlike the pendulum, the spring period does depend on the mass.

3. Energy in SHM

As the body oscillates, energy is constantly swapped between kinetic energy (KE) and potential energy (PE), but the total mechanical energy stays constant (no friction).

Total energy E = KE + PE = constant = ½kA²
PositionSpeedKinetic energyPotential energy
Centre (x = 0)Maximum (ωA)MaximumMinimum (zero)
Extreme (x = ±A)ZeroZeroMaximum
  • KE is greatest at the centre, where the body moves fastest.
  • PE is greatest at the extremes, where the body is momentarily at rest.
  • At every point KE + PE adds up to the same total.
🌟 Picture a swing: fastest (all KE) at the bottom, momentarily still and highest (all PE) at the ends — energy just keeps trading places.

4. Worked examples

Example 1 — pendulum period: Find the period of a simple pendulum of length 0.9 m (g = 10, π ≈ 3.14).
T = 2π√(L / g) = 2π√(0.9 / 10) = 2π√0.09
= 2π × 0.3 = 0.6π ≈ 1.9 s
Answer ≈ 1.9 s.
Example 2 — changing the length: A pendulum has a period of 2 s. What length is needed to make the period 4 s?
T ∝ √L, and the period is doubled (2 s → 4 s)
So L must be multiplied by 2² = 4 → the string must be 4 times as long.
Answer: make it 4 times longer.
Example 3 — spring period: A 0.2 kg mass oscillates on a spring of force constant k = 20 N/m (π ≈ 3.14). Find the period.
T = 2π√(m / k) = 2π√(0.2 / 20) = 2π√0.01
= 2π × 0.1 = 0.2π ≈ 0.63 s
Answer ≈ 0.63 s.
Example 4 — finding the spring constant: A 0.5 kg mass on a spring has a period of 0.628 s. Find k (π ≈ 3.14).
T = 2π√(m / k) → 0.628 = 2 × 3.14 × √(0.5 / k)
0.628 / 6.28 = √(0.5 / k) → 0.1 = √(0.5 / k)
0.01 = 0.5 / k → k = 0.5 / 0.01 = 50 N/m
Answer: k = 50 N/m.
