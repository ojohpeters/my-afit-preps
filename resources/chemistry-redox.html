🔁 PART 1 — The Meaning of Oxidation & Reduction

1. What are we talking about?

Oxidation and reduction are two opposite chemical changes. Long ago chemists only watched what oxygen did, but today we describe them in four ways. The most powerful one is about electrons.

The important truth first: oxidation and reduction ALWAYS happen together. If one substance loses electrons (is oxidised), another substance must gain those same electrons (is reduced). You cannot have one without the other — that is why the combined reaction is called a redox reaction (REDuction + OXidation).

🌟 Think of it like a pass in football: electrons cannot vanish. If one atom passes them away, a partner atom must receive them. One loses, one gains — at the same time.

2. The four ways to define oxidation & reduction

Learn this table by heart — every redox question comes from one of these four rows.

Definition based on…OXIDATION is…REDUCTION is…
(a) OxygenGain of oxygenLoss of oxygen
(b) HydrogenLoss of hydrogenGain of hydrogen
(c) Electrons — OIL RIGLoss of electronsGain of electrons
(d) Oxidation numberIncrease in oxidation numberDecrease in oxidation number

Notice each pair is a mirror image: whatever oxidation does, reduction does the opposite.

3. (a) In terms of OXYGEN — the oldest idea

This is where the word "oxidation" came from — reacting with oxygen.

  • Oxidation = gain of oxygen.
  • Reduction = loss of oxygen.
Example: when magnesium burns, 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO, the magnesium gains oxygen, so magnesium is oxidised.
Example: when copper(II) oxide is heated with hydrogen, CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O, the CuO loses its oxygen, so the copper is reduced. (Notice the hydrogen removed the oxygen — a reducing agent.)

4. (b) In terms of HYDROGEN

This is the exact opposite pattern to oxygen:

  • Oxidation = loss of hydrogen.
  • Reduction = gain of hydrogen.
Example: H₂S → S + H₂ (hydrogen sulphide losing its hydrogen). The sulphur has lost hydrogen, so it is oxidised.
🌟 Careful — oxygen and hydrogen give OPPOSITE rules. Gaining oxygen and losing hydrogen both mean the SAME thing: oxidation.

5. (c) In terms of ELECTRONS — OIL RIG (the key one!)

This is the modern, all-purpose definition. It works even when there is no oxygen and no hydrogen in the reaction. Just remember the magic words:

OIL RIG
Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons) · Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)
  • Oxidation = loss of electrons (the atom gives electrons away).
  • Reduction = gain of electrons (the atom takes electrons in).
Example — oxidation: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻. Zinc loses 2 electrons (e⁻), so zinc is oxidised.
Example — reduction: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu. The copper ion gains 2 electrons, so Cu²⁺ is reduced.
🌟 Two mnemonics for the same rule: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain) and LEO the lion says GER (Loss of Electrons = Oxidation, Gain of Electrons = Reduction). Use whichever sticks.

6. (d) In terms of OXIDATION NUMBER

Every atom is given an oxidation number (studied fully in the next tab). Using it:

  • Oxidation = increase in oxidation number (it becomes more positive).
  • Reduction = decrease in oxidation number (it becomes more negative).
Example: in Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻, iron goes from +2 to +3. The number increased, so iron is oxidised (it also lost an electron — same conclusion by OIL RIG).
Example: in Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻, chlorine goes from 0 to −1. The number decreased, so chlorine is reduced.

7. Redox = both together

Put it all in one reaction. Take the displacement: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu.

Zn: 0 → +2 (lost electrons) → OXIDISED
Cu²⁺: +2 → 0 (gained electrons) → REDUCED

One is oxidised while the other is reduced — that is a redox reaction. The electrons Zn loses are exactly the electrons Cu²⁺ gains.

🔢 PART 2 — Assigning Oxidation Numbers

1. What is an oxidation number?

An oxidation number (or oxidation state) is a signed number (+ or −) given to each atom that shows how many electrons it appears to have lost or gained compared with the free element. It is a bookkeeping tool — it lets us spot instantly what was oxidised and what was reduced.

🌟 Think of it as each atom's "electron score". If the score goes UP, the atom lost electrons (oxidised). If it goes DOWN, it gained electrons (reduced).

2. The rules — learn them in order

Apply from the top; the fixed rules win
  • Rule 1 — Free elements = 0. Any atom in an uncombined element has oxidation number 0. So Zn, Cu, O₂, H₂, Cl₂, Na, S₈ are all 0.
  • Rule 2 — Simple (monatomic) ion = its charge. Na⁺ = +1, Mg²⁺ = +2, Al³⁺ = +3, Cl⁻ = −1, O²⁻ = −2.
  • Rule 3 — Group 1 metals = +1, Group 2 metals = +2 in all their compounds (Na, K = +1; Mg, Ca = +2).
  • Rule 4 — Oxygen = −2 in almost every compound. Exception: in peroxides (like H₂O₂) oxygen is −1.
  • Rule 5 — Hydrogen = +1 when joined to non-metals. Exception: in metal hydrides (like NaH, CaH₂) hydrogen is −1.
  • Rule 6 — The sum rule. In a neutral compound all oxidation numbers add up to 0. In an ion they add up to the charge on the ion.
🌟 The whole method: fix the atoms you know from the rules (O, H, group 1 & 2), then let the sum rule give you the unknown one.

3. The 3-step method

Every "find the oxidation number" question:
  • Step 1: Let the unknown element = x.
  • Step 2: Fill in the known ones (usually O = −2, H = +1, group 1 = +1, group 2 = +2), multiplying by how many atoms of each.
  • Step 3: Set the total equal to 0 (neutral compound) or to the ion's charge, then solve for x.

4. Worked example — S in H₂SO₄

Find the oxidation number of sulphur in sulphuric acid, H₂SO₄.
Let S = x. H = +1 (×2), O = −2 (×4). Neutral → sum = 0.
2(+1) + x + 4(−2) = 0
2 + x − 8 = 0 → x = +6
So sulphur is +6 in H₂SO₄.

5. Worked example — Mn in KMnO₄

Find the oxidation number of manganese in potassium permanganate, KMnO₄.
Let Mn = x. K = +1 (group 1), O = −2 (×4). Neutral → sum = 0.
(+1) + x + 4(−2) = 0
1 + x − 8 = 0 → x = +7
So manganese is +7 in KMnO₄ — its highest possible state, which is why KMnO₄ is such a strong oxidising agent.

6. Worked example — Cr in K₂Cr₂O₇

Find the oxidation number of chromium in potassium dichromate, K₂Cr₂O₇.
Let Cr = x. K = +1 (×2), O = −2 (×7). Neutral → sum = 0.
2(+1) + 2x + 7(−2) = 0
2 + 2x − 14 = 0 → 2x = 12 → x = +6
So each chromium is +6 in K₂Cr₂O₇.

7. Worked example — N in HNO₃

Find the oxidation number of nitrogen in nitric acid, HNO₃.
Let N = x. H = +1, O = −2 (×3). Neutral → sum = 0.
(+1) + x + 3(−2) = 0
1 + x − 6 = 0 → x = +5
So nitrogen is +5 in HNO₃.

8. Worked example — Cl in NaClO (an ion inside)

Find the oxidation number of chlorine in sodium chlorate(I), NaClO.
Let Cl = x. Na = +1, O = −2. Neutral → sum = 0.
(+1) + x + (−2) = 0
x − 1 = 0 → x = +1
So chlorine is +1 here — note chlorine is NOT always −1; it depends on the compound.
Bonus — using the ion charge: the sulphate ion SO₄²⁻ has an overall charge of −2.
x + 4(−2) = −2 → x − 8 = −2 → x = +6
Sulphur is +6 in SO₄²⁻ too — the same as in H₂SO₄, which makes sense.

🧪 PART 3 — Oxidising & Reducing Agents

1. The tricky-but-simple definitions

An agent is the substance that causes the change to happen to something else. Here is the twist that confuses everyone — so read slowly:

  • An oxidising agent oxidises another substance. To do that it must take electrons from that substance. So the oxidising agent gains electrons and is itself REDUCED.
  • A reducing agent reduces another substance. To do that it must give electrons to that substance. So the reducing agent loses electrons and is itself OXIDISED.
Oxidising agent → is itself REDUCED (gains e⁻)
Reducing agent → is itself OXIDISED (loses e⁻)
🌟 The agent always does the OPPOSITE to its name. An oxidising agent gets reduced; a reducing agent gets oxidised. (Like a fire "lighter" that gets used up itself.)

2. Common oxidising and reducing agents

Common OXIDISING agents (they gain e⁻)Common REDUCING agents (they lose e⁻)
Acidified KMnO₄ (potassium permanganate)Hydrogen, H₂
Acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate)Carbon, C
Chlorine, Cl₂ (and other halogens)Carbon monoxide, CO
Oxygen, O₂Reactive metals (Na, Mg, Zn, Fe)
Concentrated H₂SO₄Hydrogen sulphide, H₂S
Concentrated / dilute HNO₃Sulphur dioxide, SO₂
Hydrogen peroxide, H₂O₂*Potassium iodide, KI (iodide ions, I⁻)

*H₂O₂ usually acts as an oxidising agent, but can act as a reducing agent towards very strong oxidisers — a common exam trap.

3. Colour-change tests (very popular in exams)

The two purple/orange oxidising agents give famous colour changes when they get reduced by a reducing agent. Memorise them.

Oxidising agentColour change when it actsWhat happens to the metal
Acidified KMnO₄Purple → colourlessMn: +7 → +2 (MnO₄⁻ becomes Mn²⁺)
Acidified K₂Cr₂O₇Orange → greenCr: +6 → +3 (Cr₂O₇²⁻ becomes Cr³⁺)
How to read it: if you add a reducing agent (say SO₂ or Fe²⁺) to purple KMnO₄ and it turns colourless, the KMnO₄ has been reduced — which proves the other substance is a reducing agent.
Another test: chlorine water (an oxidising agent) added to colourless potassium iodide turns it brown/yellow as iodine is set free: Cl₂ + 2KI → 2KCl + I₂. Here Cl₂ is reduced and I⁻ is oxidised.

4. How to name the agent in a reaction

3-step trick:
  • Step 1: Find what was oxidised (lost electrons / went up in oxidation number). That substance is the reducing agent.
  • Step 2: Find what was reduced (gained electrons / went down in oxidation number). That substance is the oxidising agent.
  • Step 3: Remember: the agent is named the OPPOSITE of what happens to it.
Example: in H₂ + CuO → Cu + H₂O:
Cu: +2 → 0 (reduced) → so CuO is the OXIDISING agent
H: 0 → +1 (oxidised) → so H₂ is the REDUCING agent

⚗️ PART 4 — Half-Equations, Displacement & Uses

1. What is a half-equation?

A half-equation shows only ONE half of the redox story — either the oxidation OR the reduction — including the electrons (e⁻). Because electrons are shown, you can see at a glance which half is which.

  • Oxidation half-equation: electrons appear on the RIGHT (they are lost).
  • Reduction half-equation: electrons appear on the LEFT (they are gained).
Oxidation examples (e⁻ on the right):
Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + e⁻
Reduction examples (e⁻ on the left):
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂

2. Writing a half-equation (method)

Simple method for a metal / ion:
  • Step 1: Write the atom on the left, its ion on the right (or the reverse for reduction).
  • Step 2: Balance the atoms.
  • Step 3: Add electrons (e⁻) to whichever side is needed so the charges balance on both sides.
Example: zinc atom to zinc ion. Left charge = 0, right (Zn²⁺) = +2. Add 2e⁻ to the right to balance charge:
Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (charge: 0 = +2 − 2 ✓)

3. Identifying oxidised / reduced & the agents — worked

Worked Example 1: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu
Zn: 0 → +2 (increase, lost 2e⁻) → OXIDISED → Zn is the reducing agent
Cu²⁺: +2 → 0 (decrease, gained 2e⁻) → REDUCED → Cu²⁺ is the oxidising agent
Half-equations: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation) and Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (reduction).
Worked Example 2: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Mg: 0 → +2 (oxidised) → Mg is the reducing agent
O: 0 → −2 (reduced) → O₂ is the oxidising agent
Worked Example 3: H₂ + CuO → Cu + H₂O
H: 0 → +1 (oxidised) → H₂ is the reducing agent
Cu: +2 → 0 (reduced) → CuO is the oxidising agent

4. Displacement reactions are redox

When a more reactive metal pushes a less reactive metal out of its salt solution, electrons move — so displacement is a redox reaction.

Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (ionically: Fe + Cu²⁺ → Fe²⁺ + Cu)
Fe: 0 → +2 (oxidised, reducing agent)
Cu²⁺: +2 → 0 (reduced, oxidising agent)
The blue Cu²⁺ solution fades and reddish-brown copper is deposited on the iron. (SO₄²⁻ is just a spectator ion — it does not change.)
🌟 In every displacement: the metal added is oxidised (reducing agent); the metal ion in solution is reduced (oxidising agent).

5. Everyday applications of redox

  • Corrosion / rusting of iron: iron is oxidised by oxygen and water: iron loses electrons (Fe → Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺) while oxygen is reduced. Rust is hydrated iron(III) oxide. Painting, oiling, galvanising and sacrificial protection all fight this oxidation.
  • Extraction of metals: a metal in its ore is a positive ion, so extracting the metal means reducing it. Iron is reduced in the blast furnace by carbon monoxide: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. Very reactive metals (Na, Al) are reduced by electrolysis.
  • Electrochemical cells (batteries): a cell turns a redox reaction into electricity. Oxidation happens at one electrode (the negative one in a simple cell) and reduction at the other; the electrons flow through the wire as current. In electrolysis the reverse happens — electricity drives redox.
  • Respiration & combustion: both are oxidation reactions that release energy (glucose or fuel is oxidised by oxygen).
🌟 Big picture: rusting = unwanted oxidation, extraction = reduction of the ore, batteries = controlled redox turned into electricity. Redox is everywhere.
