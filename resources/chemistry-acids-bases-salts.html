🧪 PART 1 — Acids & Bases from Zero

1. Three ways to define an acid and a base

Chemists have three definitions. They get wider and wider — each one includes more substances than the one before.

(a) Arrhenius definition (the water definition)

  • An acid is a substance that produces hydrogen ions, H⁺ (really H₃O⁺, the hydroxonium ion), in water.
  • A base is a substance that produces hydroxide ions, OH⁻, in water.
HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻  ·  NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻

Strictly, the H⁺ never floats alone — it joins a water molecule: H⁺ + H₂O → H₃O⁺ (hydroxonium ion). We usually just write H⁺ for short.

(b) Brønsted–Lowry definition (the proton definition)

  • An acid is a proton (H⁺) donor — it gives away an H⁺.
  • A base is a proton (H⁺) acceptor — it takes an H⁺.

When an acid loses its proton it becomes a conjugate base; when a base gains a proton it becomes a conjugate acid. They come in conjugate pairs that differ by exactly one H⁺.

HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
Reading the conjugate pairs:
  • HCl gives away an H⁺ → so HCl is the acid; its conjugate base is Cl⁻.
  • H₂O takes the H⁺ → so H₂O is the base; its conjugate acid is H₃O⁺.
Pairs: (HCl / Cl⁻) and (H₃O⁺ / H₂O). Each pair differs by one proton.
🌟 Memory: Brønsted Acid = Away with the proton (donor). Base = Brings it in (acceptor).

(c) Lewis definition (the electron-pair definition — the widest)

  • A Lewis acid is an electron-pair acceptor.
  • A Lewis base is an electron-pair donor.

This is the broadest idea — it covers substances with no hydrogen at all (like BF₃ or AlCl₃, which accept a lone pair). NH₃, which has a lone pair to give, is a Lewis base.

2. Properties of acids

  • They have a sour taste (e.g. lemon, vinegar) — never actually taste lab acids.
  • They turn blue litmus red and have a pH below 7.
  • They are electrolytes — they conduct electricity in solution because they form ions.
  • Acid + reactive metal → salt + hydrogen gas.
    Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
    Only metals above hydrogen in the activity series react this way. The H₂ gives a "pop" with a lighted splint.
  • Acid + carbonate (or hydrogencarbonate) → salt + water + carbon dioxide.
    Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑
    The CO₂ turns limewater milky — the standard test.
  • Acid + base → salt + water (neutralisation).
    HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

3. Properties of bases and alkalis

A base is any substance that neutralises an acid (metal oxides and hydroxides). An alkali is simply a base that dissolves in water (e.g. NaOH, KOH, aqueous NH₃). Every alkali is a base, but not every base is an alkali (CuO is a base but is insoluble, so it is not an alkali).

  • Alkalis have a bitter taste and feel soapy / slippery.
  • They turn red litmus blue and have a pH above 7.
  • Base + acid → salt + water (neutralisation).
  • Alkali + ammonium salt → salt + water + ammonia gas.
    NaOH + NH₄Cl → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃↑
    Ammonia turns damp red litmus blue — a test for ammonium salts.

4. Strong vs weak — it is about ionisation, not concentration

"Strong/weak" tells you how completely the substance splits into ions (its degree of ionisation). "Concentrated/dilute" is a different idea — that is just how much is dissolved in the water.

StrongWeak
AcidsFully ionised — HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃Partly ionised — CH₃COOH (ethanoic), H₂CO₃, H₂S
BasesFully ionised — NaOH, KOHPartly ionised — aqueous NH₃ (NH₄OH)
🌟 A strong acid ionises 100%. A weak acid may ionise only a few % — most of it stays as whole molecules. You can have a dilute strong acid and a concentrated weak acid; the two words are unrelated.

5. Basicity of an acid

The basicity of an acid is the number of replaceable hydrogen (H⁺) ions in one molecule — i.e. the number of H⁺ it can donate.

AcidFormulaReplaceable H⁺Basicity
Hydrochloric acidHCl1Monobasic (1)
Ethanoic acidCH₃COOH1 (only the –COOH hydrogen)Monobasic (1)
Tetraoxosulphate(VI) acidH₂SO₄2Dibasic (2)
Trioxocarbonate(IV) acidH₂CO₃2Dibasic (2)
Trioxophosphate(V)/phosphoric acidH₃PO₄3Tribasic (3)
🌟 Careful: in CH₃COOH there are 4 hydrogens written, but only the ONE on –COOH is replaceable, so its basicity is 1, not 4.

📊 PART 2 — The pH Scale & Indicators

1. The pH scale (0 to 14)

pH is a number that tells how acidic or alkaline a solution is. It runs from 0 to 14:

  • pH below 7 → acidic (the lower the number, the stronger the acid). pH 0–1 is a strong acid.
  • pH exactly 7 → neutral (pure water at 25 °C).
  • pH above 7 → alkaline/basic (the higher the number, the stronger the alkali). pH 13–14 is a strong alkali.
🌟 Small pH = "small friendly acid number." Big pH = "big basic number." 7 sits neutral in the middle.

2. The pH and pOH formulas

pH = −log[H⁺]  ·  pOH = −log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14

Here [H⁺] means "the concentration of hydrogen ions in mol/dm³". The square brackets always mean concentration. Because pH + pOH = 14, once you know one you can always get the other.

A neat trick: if a concentration is a clean power of ten (like 10⁻² or 10⁻³), the log is easy — the pH is just that power made positive.

3. Worked pH calculations

Example 1 — a strong acid. Find the pH of 0.01 M hydrochloric acid (HCl).
HCl is strong → fully ionised, so [H⁺] = 0.01 = 10⁻² mol/dm³
pH = −log(10⁻²) = 2
Answer: pH = 2 (acidic, as expected).
Example 2 — a stronger acid. Find the pH of 0.1 M HCl.
[H⁺] = 0.1 = 10⁻¹
pH = −log(10⁻¹) = 1
Answer: pH = 1. (More concentrated → lower pH.)
Example 3 — a strong alkali (go through pOH). Find the pH of 0.001 M sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
NaOH is strong → fully ionised, so [OH⁻] = 0.001 = 10⁻³
pOH = −log(10⁻³) = 3
pH = 14 − pOH = 14 − 3 = 11
Answer: pH = 11 (alkaline, as expected).
Example 4 — another alkali. Find the pH of 0.01 M NaOH.
[OH⁻] = 0.01 = 10⁻² → pOH = 2
pH = 14 − 2 = 12
Answer: pH = 12.
🌟 Golden rule: for an acid, work out [H⁺] then use pH = −log[H⁺]. For an alkali, work out [OH⁻] → pOH → then pH = 14 − pOH.

4. Indicators and their colours

An indicator is a dye that changes colour depending on whether it is in acid or alkali. The three you must know:

IndicatorColour in ACIDColour in ALKALI/BASENeutral
LitmusRedBluePurple
Methyl orangeRed / pinkYellowOrange
PhenolphthaleinColourlessPinkColourless
🌟 Memory hooks: "phenolphthalein is pink in alkaline, and colourless in acid." Methyl orange goes red when things get rough (acidic).

5. Choosing the right indicator for a titration

At the end point the pH jumps sharply. You pick an indicator whose colour change happens inside that jump — it depends on which acid and base you are using.

Titration typeBest indicator
Strong acid + strong baseMethyl orange OR phenolphthalein (both work)
Strong acid + weak baseMethyl orange
Weak acid + strong basePhenolphthalein
Weak acid + weak baseNo suitable indicator (change is too gradual)
🌟 Quick rule: match the indicator to the STRONG partner — strong base ⇒ phenolphthalein (works in alkali region); strong acid (with weak base) ⇒ methyl orange.

⚗️ PART 3 — Salts & How to Prepare Them

1. What is a salt?

A salt is the compound formed when the replaceable hydrogen ions of an acid are replaced by a metal (or ammonium, NH₄⁺) ion. Salts are the product of neutralisation.

Acid + Base → Salt + Water
Example: in HCl the H⁺ is replaced by Na⁺ to give the salt NaCl; in H₂SO₄ both H⁺ are replaced to give Na₂SO₄.

2. Types of salt (by structure)

  • Normal salt: ALL the replaceable H⁺ of the acid have been replaced by a metal. Examples: NaCl, Na₂SO₄, KNO₃, ZnCl₂.
  • Acid salt: only PART of the replaceable H⁺ has been replaced — some H is still there. Only dibasic/tribasic acids can form these. Examples: NaHSO₄ (sodium hydrogensulphate), NaHCO₃ (sodium hydrogencarbonate), Ca(HCO₃)₂.
  • Basic salt: contains OH⁻ or O²⁻ in addition to the normal salt part (from incomplete neutralisation of a base). Example: Pb(OH)Cl, basic lead carbonate.
  • Double salt: two simple salts crystallised together in one crystal; it splits back into the two salts when dissolved. Examples: potash alum KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O; Mohr's salt FeSO₄·(NH₄)₂SO₄·6H₂O.

3. Acidic, basic or neutral salts (by hydrolysis in water)

Even a "normal" salt can give an acidic or alkaline solution, because of the acid and base it came from:

  • Salt of strong acid + strong base → NEUTRAL (pH 7). Example: NaCl, KNO₃.
  • Salt of strong acid + weak base → ACIDIC (pH < 7). Example: NH₄Cl, (NH₄)₂SO₄.
  • Salt of weak acid + strong base → ALKALINE (pH > 7). Example: Na₂CO₃, CH₃COONa.
🌟 The stronger parent wins: strong acid parent pulls the solution acidic; strong base parent pulls it alkaline; when both are strong they cancel to neutral.

4. Four methods of preparing salts

Which method to use depends on whether the salt (and its starting metal/base) is soluble.

  • (a) Neutralisation / titration — for soluble salts of sodium, potassium and ammonium. Titrate the acid against the alkali using an indicator, then repeat without the indicator and evaporate.
    HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
  • (b) Metal + acid — for a soluble salt of a fairly reactive metal (above H, but not the violent ones like Na/K). Add excess metal to the acid, filter off the leftover metal, evaporate.
    Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
  • (c) Insoluble base / carbonate + acid — for a soluble salt of an unreactive metal (Cu, Pb). Add the insoluble base/carbonate in excess to the warm acid, filter off the excess solid, evaporate.
    CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
  • (d) Precipitation (double decomposition) — for an INSOLUBLE salt. Mix two solutions of soluble salts; the insoluble salt drops out as a precipitate, which you filter, wash and dry.
    AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
    BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl

5. Solubility rules (learn these — they decide the method)

  • All sodium, potassium and ammonium salts are soluble.
  • All nitrates (NO₃⁻) are soluble.
  • All chlorides are soluble EXCEPT silver chloride (AgCl) and lead(II) chloride (PbCl₂).
  • All sulphates are soluble EXCEPT barium sulphate (BaSO₄) and lead(II) sulphate (PbSO₄) (calcium sulphate is only slightly soluble).
  • All carbonates are INSOLUBLE EXCEPT those of sodium, potassium and ammonium.
  • Most hydroxides are insoluble EXCEPT NaOH, KOH and aqueous NH₃ (Ca(OH)₂ slightly soluble).
🌟 Chant: "Nitrates always; sodium/potassium/ammonium always; chlorides except silver & lead; sulphates except barium & lead; carbonates only Na, K, NH₄."

6. Water of crystallisation, deliquescence & efflorescence

  • Water of crystallisation: a fixed number of water molecules chemically built into a crystal. Such a salt is hydrated. Examples: CuSO₄·5H₂O (blue), Na₂CO₃·10H₂O. Heating drives off the water to leave the white anhydrous salt (anhydrous CuSO₄ is white; it turns blue with water — a test for water).
  • Deliquescence: a solid absorbs so much water vapour from the air that it dissolves into a solution. Examples: NaOH, CaCl₂.
  • Efflorescence: a hydrated crystal LOSES its water of crystallisation to the air and turns powdery. Example: Na₂CO₃·10H₂O.
  • Hygroscopic: absorbs water vapour but does NOT dissolve (used as drying agents). Examples: concentrated H₂SO₄, CaO.

🔬 PART 4 — Titration Calculations & Salt Hydrolysis

1. What is a titration?

A titration is a method of finding an unknown concentration by reacting it exactly with a solution of known concentration, using an indicator to spot the end point (the moment neutralisation is complete). You measure the volumes and calculate.

Equipment: the known solution goes in the burette; a fixed volume of the unknown is measured with a pipette into a conical flask with a few drops of indicator; you run the burette in until the colour just changes.

2. The titration formula

CₐVₐ / CᵦVᵦ = nₐ / nᵦ
  • Cₐ, Vₐ = concentration and volume of the ACID
  • Cᵦ, Vᵦ = concentration and volume of the BASE
  • nₐ : nᵦ = the mole ratio of acid to base taken straight from the balanced equation

The volumes can be in cm³ or dm³ as long as both use the same unit (they cancel). Concentration is in mol/dm³.

Get nₐ : nᵦ from the equation. For HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O the ratio is 1 : 1. For H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O the ratio is 1 : 2 (one acid to two base).

3. Worked titration calculations

Example 1 — find an unknown concentration (1 : 1). 25.0 cm³ of sodium hydroxide is exactly neutralised by 20.0 cm³ of 0.10 M HCl. Find the concentration of the NaOH.
Equation: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O, so nₐ : nᵦ = 1 : 1
CₐVₐ / CᵦVᵦ = 1/1 → Cᵦ = CₐVₐ / Vᵦ
Cᵦ = (0.10 × 20.0) / 25.0 = 2.0 / 25.0 = 0.08 M
Answer: [NaOH] = 0.08 mol/dm³.
Example 2 — a 1 : 2 ratio (dibasic acid). What volume of 0.10 M NaOH is needed to neutralise 25.0 cm³ of 0.10 M H₂SO₄?
Equation: H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O, so nₐ : nᵦ = 1 : 2
CₐVₐ / CᵦVᵦ = 1/2 → CᵦVᵦ = 2 × CₐVₐ
Vᵦ = (2 × 0.10 × 25.0) / 0.10 = 5.0 / 0.10 = 50.0 cm³
Answer: 50.0 cm³ (it needs twice as much base, which makes sense — each H₂SO₄ gives 2 H⁺).
Example 3 — find the acid concentration. 20.0 cm³ of HCl is neutralised by 25.0 cm³ of 0.20 M NaOH (1 : 1). Find [HCl].
Cₐ = CᵦVᵦ / Vₐ = (0.20 × 25.0) / 20.0
Cₐ = 5.0 / 20.0 = 0.25 M
Answer: [HCl] = 0.25 mol/dm³.

4. Salt hydrolysis (why a salt solution can be acidic or alkaline)

When a salt dissolves, its ions can react with water (hydrolysis). Whether the solution ends up acidic, basic or neutral depends on the acid and base that made the salt:

Salt made from…Solution is…Example
Strong acid + strong baseNeutral (pH 7) — no hydrolysisNaCl, KNO₃
Strong acid + weak baseAcidic (pH < 7)NH₄Cl, (NH₄)₂SO₄
Weak acid + strong baseAlkaline (pH > 7)Na₂CO₃, CH₃COONa
🌟 The stronger parent decides: strong-acid parent ⇒ acidic salt; strong-base parent ⇒ alkaline salt; both strong ⇒ neutral. So common salt (NaCl) is neutral, ammonium chloride is acidic, and washing soda (Na₂CO₃) is alkaline.
