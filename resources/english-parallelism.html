📖 What is Parallelism?

1. The simple definition

Parallelism (also called parallel structure or parallel construction) is a grammar rule that says: when ideas are joined together in a list, a pair, or a comparison, each item must be in the SAME grammatical form.

In short: matching ideas need matching forms. If one item in a series is a noun, they should all be nouns. If one is an -ing word, they should all be -ing words. If one is a to + verb (infinitive), they should all be infinitives.

🌟 Think of a market display where eggs are lined up in a tray. If every slot holds an egg, the tray looks neat and balanced. Put one orange among the eggs and the whole row looks wrong. Parallelism is keeping every “slot” in a sentence the same kind of word — so the row of ideas looks and reads smoothly.

2. Why it matters

  • Clarity: the reader follows your list easily because the pattern is steady.
  • Balance & rhythm: parallel sentences sound smooth and professional (think “I came, I saw, I conquered”).
  • Exam marks: WAEC, JAMB and Post-UTME love to test this. A sentence that breaks parallelism is marked wrong, and you must pick the option that keeps it.

3. Faulty vs correct — the classic example

❌ Wrong: She likes reading, dancing and to sing.
✅ Right: She likes reading, dancing and singing.
The first two items are -ing words (gerunds), so the third must also be -ing. “to sing” breaks the pattern.
Rule: Find the joining word (usually and, or, but) and make sure the items on each side are the same form.

4. Parallelism of NOUNS

When you list things (nouns), keep them all as nouns of the same kind.

❌ Wrong: The team needs strength, speed and to be intelligent.
✅ Right: The team needs strength, speed and intelligence.
strength, speed, intelligence — all abstract nouns.

5. Parallelism of VERBS

When verbs are joined, keep them in the same tense and form.

❌ Wrong: He came in, sat down and is reading a book.
✅ Right: He came in, sat down and read a book.
came, sat, read — all simple past tense. Mixing in “is reading” breaks the chain.

6. Parallelism of GERUNDS (-ing words)

If a sentence starts a list with an -ing word, finish it with -ing words.

❌ Wrong: My hobbies are swimming, cooking and to paint.
✅ Right: My hobbies are swimming, cooking and painting.
All three must be gerunds: swimming, cooking, painting.

7. Parallelism of INFINITIVES (to + verb)

If the list begins with a to + verb, keep all items as infinitives. (You may write “to” once and let it cover the rest.)

❌ Wrong: The aim is to learn, to grow and becoming better.
✅ Right: The aim is to learn, to grow and to become better.
(also correct: to learn, grow and become better)
Keep them all infinitives, or repeat “to” consistently.

8. Parallelism of ADJECTIVES

Describing words joined together should all be adjectives.

❌ Wrong: The child is bright, polite and works hard.
✅ Right: The child is bright, polite and hardworking.
bright, polite, hardworking — all adjectives.

9. Parallelism of PHRASES and CLAUSES

Whole phrases or clauses joined together should follow the same pattern too.

❌ Wrong (phrases): She searched in the kitchen, under the table and was looking on the shelf.
✅ Right: She searched in the kitchen, under the table and on the shelf.
All three are prepositional phrases of place.
❌ Wrong (clauses): He said that he was tired and wanting to rest.
✅ Right: He said that he was tired and that he wanted to rest.
Match “that he was...” with “that he wanted...” — same clause pattern.

🔗 Parallelism with Correlative Conjunctions & Comparisons

1. What are correlative conjunctions?

Correlative conjunctions are word-pairs that work together to join two equal parts of a sentence. Because they come in pairs, the words that follow each part of the pair must be the same grammatical form. This is one of the most tested parallelism rules in WAEC and JAMB.

Correlative pairMeaningRight example
either ... orone of two choicesYou may either stay or leave.
neither ... nornot one and not the otherHe is neither rude nor lazy.
not only ... but alsotwo things both trueShe is not only clever but also kind.
both ... andtwo togetherBoth the teacher and the pupil agreed.
whether ... ortwo possibilitiesDecide whether to go or to stay.
Golden rule: Whatever form comes right after the FIRST word of the pair must also come right after the SECOND word. Verb matches verb, noun matches noun, phrase matches phrase.

2. either ... or

❌ Wrong: You can either pay now or you wait till tomorrow.
✅ Right: You can either pay now or wait till tomorrow.
After “either” comes a verb (pay), so after “or” you also need a verb (wait), not a new clause.

3. neither ... nor

❌ Wrong: He neither speaks French nor German.
✅ Right: He speaks neither French nor German.
Move “neither” so both words after the pair are nouns (French, German). In the wrong version “speaks French” (verb + noun) does not match “German” (noun).

4. not only ... but also

❌ Wrong: She not only sings but also is dancing.
✅ Right: She not only sings but also dances.
“sings” (simple present verb) must be balanced by “dances” (simple present verb), not “is dancing”.

5. both ... and

❌ Wrong: He is both intelligent and works hard.
✅ Right: He is both intelligent and hardworking.
After “both” comes an adjective (intelligent), so after “and” you also need an adjective (hardworking).

6. whether ... or

❌ Wrong: I cannot tell whether to laugh or crying.
✅ Right: I cannot tell whether to laugh or to cry.
Match infinitive with infinitive: to laugh, to cry.

7. Parallelism in COMPARISONS (than, as ... as)

When you compare two things, the items being compared must be in the same form.

❌ Wrong: Reading is easier than to write.
✅ Right: Reading is easier than writing.
Compare a gerund with a gerund: reading vs writing.
❌ Wrong: Walking to school is as healthy as to ride a bicycle.
✅ Right: Walking to school is as healthy as riding a bicycle.
With “as ... as”, balance the two sides: walking vs riding.

8. Parallelism after coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or)

The coordinating conjunctions and, but, or join equal grammatical units. Whatever sits on one side must match the other side.

❌ Wrong: The job is tiring but it pays well and exciting.
✅ Right: The job is tiring but well-paid and exciting.
Keep all three as adjectives describing the job.
❌ Wrong: We went to the market and buying some yams.
✅ Right: We went to the market and bought some yams.
went and bought — both simple past verbs.

🛠️ Spotting & Fixing Faulty Parallelism

The 4-step method to catch the error

Step 1 — Find the joiner. Look for and, or, but, a comma list, or a correlative pair (either...or, not only...but also, etc.). The error always hides around the joiner.
Step 2 — List the items. Pull out each item that the joiner connects and stack them in your mind.
Step 3 — Name the form of each. Ask: is it a noun? a gerund (-ing)? an infinitive (to + verb)? an adjective? a clause? Most items will share one form.
Step 4 — Fix the odd one out. Find the single item whose form is different and rewrite it to match the rest.

Worked example 1

Sentence: Tunde enjoys football, music and to dance.
Step 1 (joiner): the comma list ending in “and”.
Step 2 (items): football / music / to dance.
Step 3 (forms): noun / noun / infinitive — the last one is odd.
Step 4 (fix): change “to dance” to the noun “dancing”.
✅ Correct: Tunde enjoys football, music and dancing.

Worked example 2

Sentence: The manager asked us to be punctual, polite and that we should work hard.
Forms: adjective (punctual) / adjective (polite) / clause (that we should work hard) — the clause is odd.
✅ Correct: The manager asked us to be punctual, polite and hardworking.

Worked example 3 (correlative trap)

Sentence: She is not only a writer but also teaches.
Joiner: not only ... but also.
After “not only”: “a writer” (noun phrase). After “but also”: “teaches” (verb) — they do not match.
✅ Correct: She is not only a writer but also a teacher. (noun ↔ noun)

Common error patterns examiners use

Trap they setWhat to watch for
Mixing gerund with infinitive...reading, writing and to talk → should be “talking”
Mixing a verb into a noun liststrength, speed and to run fast → should be a noun
Mixing an adjective with a clausebrave, honest and who works hard → should be “hardworking”
Unbalanced correlativeseither + verb but or + clause, etc.
Unbalanced comparison“easier than to write” → “easier than writing”
Tense shift in a verb listcame, sat and is leaving → “left”

Identify-the-error practice

Find the fault: The lecture was long, boring and it confused everyone.
Fault: “it confused everyone” is a clause among adjectives (long, boring).
✅ Fix: The lecture was long, boring and confusing.
Find the fault: He promised to study harder, to sleep early and waking up on time.
Fault: “waking up” is a gerund among infinitives (to study, to sleep).
✅ Fix: He promised to study harder, to sleep early and to wake up on time.
Find the fault: Our duty is to obey the law, respect elders and helping the poor.
Fault: “helping” breaks the infinitive pattern (to obey, [to] respect).
✅ Fix: Our duty is to obey the law, respect elders and help the poor.

⭐ Golden checklist for the exam

  • Spot the joiner (and / or / but / a comma list / a correlative pair).
  • Make every item in the series the same word class.
  • Choose the option in which all items match — that is the answer.
  • If asked for the WRONG/odd item, pick the one whose form does not fit the others.
