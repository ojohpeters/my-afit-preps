📖 The Basics — Why and How We Separate Mixtures

1. Pure substances vs mixtures

  • A pure substance is made of only one kind of particle (e.g. pure water, pure salt).
  • A mixture contains two or more substances physically mixed — NOT chemically joined. Their parts keep their own properties and can be separated again.

Examples of mixtures: salt + water, sand + iron filings, crude oil, air, ink, blood.

🌟 Think of it like a bowl of rice and beans: they are mixed but not "joined" — you can still pick them apart. A chemical compound (like water, H₂O) is different — the hydrogen and oxygen are truly bonded and need a chemical reaction to separate.

2. The golden rule: separate using a DIFFERENCE in physical property

Every separation method works by exploiting a property in which the components differ. Ask: "How are these two things different?" Then pick the method that uses that difference.

Difference between componentsMethod to use
One is magnetic, the other is notMagnetic separation
Different particle sizesSieving
One sublimes (solid → gas), the other doesn’tSublimation
One is soluble, the other insoluble (in same liquid)Dissolve, then filter
Insoluble solid in a liquidFiltration / decantation / centrifugation
Soluble solid dissolved in a liquidEvaporation / crystallisation / distillation
Two liquids that don’t mix (immiscible)Separating funnel
Two liquids that mix, different boiling pointsFractional distillation
Coloured substances with different solubilitiesChromatography

This table is the master key — if you understand it, every exam question becomes "spot the difference, then name the method."

3. Words you MUST know (examiners love these)

  • Filtrate — the liquid that passes through the filter paper.
  • Residue — the solid left behind on the filter paper.
  • Distillate — the pure liquid collected after distillation (it boiled then condensed).
  • Soluble — can dissolve; Insoluble — cannot dissolve.
  • Miscible — two liquids that mix fully (e.g. alcohol + water); Immiscible — don’t mix (e.g. oil + water).
  • Saturated solution — a solution holding the maximum solute it can at that temperature.
  • Sublimation — a solid turning straight into vapour (skipping liquid).

🧱 Separating Solid–Solid Mixtures

1. Magnetic separation

Principle: one solid is attracted to a magnet, the other is not.

How: run a magnet over (or through) the mixture; the magnetic part sticks to the magnet while the rest stays behind.

Examples: separating iron filings from sulphur, or iron/steel scrap from rubbish at a recycling plant.

2. Sublimation

Principle: one solid sublimes (turns directly to vapour when heated) while the other does not.

How: heat the mixture. The subliming solid becomes vapour, rises, then cools on a cold surface and re-forms as pure solid; the other solid stays at the bottom.

Solids that sublime: ammonium chloride, iodine, camphor, naphthalene, solid CO₂ (dry ice).

Examples: separating common salt from iodine, ammonium chloride from sand, or camphor from sand.

3. Dissolving + filtering (when one is soluble, one is not)

Principle: in a chosen liquid, one solid dissolves and the other does not.

How (3 steps):

  • ① Add water — the soluble solid dissolves, the insoluble one doesn’t.
  • ② Filter — insoluble solid stays as residue; the solution passes through as filtrate.
  • ③ Evaporate the filtrate to get the dissolved solid back.
Example: separating a mixture of salt and sand → salt dissolves, sand is filtered off, then evaporate to recover the salt.

4. Sieving

Principle: the solids have different particle sizes.

How: shake the mixture on a sieve/mesh; small particles fall through, large ones stay on top.

Examples: separating garri from lumps/chaff, fine sand from gravel, flour from bran.

5. Floatation & winnowing (bonus methods)

  • Floatation: add to water — the lighter/less dense solid floats while the denser one sinks (e.g. good seeds from bad ones; sawdust from sand).
  • Winnowing: use wind/air to blow away the lighter part (e.g. separating chaff from grain by tossing in the air).

💧 Separating Solid–Liquid Mixtures

1. Decantation

Principle: an insoluble solid is denser than the liquid, so it settles at the bottom.

How: let the mixture stand so the solid sinks (sediment), then gently pour off the clear liquid on top.

Examples: pouring clear water off settled sand; pouring oil off water. Quick but not very pure.

2. Filtration

Principle: the solid is insoluble and too big to pass through the tiny holes of filter paper; the liquid passes through.

How: pour the mixture through filter paper in a funnel. Residue (solid) stays on the paper; filtrate (liquid) collects below.

Examples: separating chalk from water, sand from water, or precipitate from a solution. (Like a sieve for very fine particles.)

3. Centrifugation

Principle: spinning very fast forces the denser solid/particles to the bottom faster than they would settle naturally.

How: place the mixture in a centrifuge and spin it; the denser part collects at the bottom of the tube, the lighter liquid stays on top, then decant.

Examples: separating blood cells from plasma, getting cream from milk, separating very fine suspensions that won’t filter easily.

4. Evaporation (to recover a soluble solid)

Principle: the liquid (solvent) turns to vapour on heating, leaving the dissolved solid behind.

How: heat the solution in an evaporating dish until all the liquid evaporates, leaving the solid.

Note: use this when you want the SOLID and don’t care about keeping the liquid, and when heat won’t damage the solid.

Example: getting salt from salt solution (how table salt is made from sea water in salt pans).

5. Crystallisation

Principle: as a hot saturated solution cools, the solid can hold less in solution, so it comes out as pure crystals.

How: heat the solution to make it concentrated (saturated), then let it cool slowly. Clean crystals form and are filtered out and dried.

Why better than plain evaporation sometimes: it gives pure, well-shaped crystals and is gentle — good for substances that decompose with strong heat, like sugar or copper(II) sulphate, and for those with water of crystallisation.

Examples: getting copper(II) sulphate crystals or sugar crystals from their solutions.

🌡️ Separating Liquids & Special Mixtures

1. Simple distillation (get the pure liquid from a solution)

Principle: the liquid boils off and is condensed back, while the dissolved solid stays behind in the flask.

How: heat the solution → the liquid boils to vapour → the vapour passes through a condenser (cooled by water) → it condenses back to pure liquid, collected as the distillate.

Use it when you want to keep BOTH the pure liquid AND the solid (unlike evaporation, which throws the liquid away).

Example: getting pure water from sea water / salt solution. Output = pure water (distillate); salt remains in the flask.

2. Fractional distillation (separate two MISCIBLE liquids)

Principle: miscible liquids have different boiling points, so they boil off at different temperatures.

How: heat the mixture. The liquid with the lower boiling point boils off first and is collected; the higher-boiling one comes later. A tall fractionating column (packed with glass beads) gives a big surface area so the liquids separate more cleanly — vapours of the higher-boiling liquid condense and drip back, only the lowest-boiling vapour reaches the top.

Examples: separating alcohol (ethanol) from water (ethanol boils at 78°C, water at 100°C); separating crude oil into petrol, kerosene, diesel, etc.; getting nitrogen and oxygen from liquid air.

3. Separating funnel (two IMMISCIBLE liquids)

Principle: immiscible liquids don’t mix and have different densities, so they form two layers — denser at the bottom.

How: pour the mixture into a separating funnel, let the layers settle, then open the tap to run off the bottom layer first; close the tap when it’s gone, then collect the top layer.

Examples: separating oil and water, or kerosene and water, or palm oil and water.

4. Chromatography (separate dissolved coloured substances)

Principle: different substances dissolve in and travel with the solvent at different speeds (different solubility / how strongly they cling to the paper).

How: put a spot of the mixture (e.g. ink) near the bottom of chromatography paper, dip the edge in solvent. As the solvent creeps up, it carries the components different distances → they separate into bands of colour. The more soluble component travels farthest.

Examples: separating the dyes in ink or food colouring; identifying substances; testing for drugs/pigments in plants.

5. Precipitation (bonus)

Principle: add a reagent that turns a dissolved substance into an insoluble solid (precipitate), which can then be filtered out.
Example: removing certain dissolved ions from water by precipitating them, then filtering.
