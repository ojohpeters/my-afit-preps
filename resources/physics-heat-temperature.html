🌡️ PART 1 — Temperature, Scales & Thermometers

1. Heat vs Temperature — the key difference

These two words feel the same in everyday life, but in physics they mean very different things. Getting this right unlocks the whole topic.

  • Heat is a form of energy — the energy that flows from a hotter body to a colder body because of the temperature difference between them. Its unit is the joule (J).
  • Temperature is the degree of hotness or coldness of a body — it tells you which way heat will flow, not how much energy is stored. Its SI unit is the kelvin (K).

Heat always flows from high temperature to low temperature, never the other way on its own — just as water flows downhill.

🌟 Idea: a full bucket of warm water can hold far more heat energy than a tiny red-hot spark, even though the spark is at a much higher temperature. Temperature is the "level"; heat is the "amount".

2. The three temperature scales

We measure temperature on three common scales. You must be able to move between them.

Celsius (°C)

Built on water: ice melts at 0 °C and water boils at 100 °C (at normal pressure).

Kelvin (K) — the SI / absolute scale

Starts at absolute zero (0 K = −273 °C), the coldest possible temperature. A change of 1 K equals a change of 1 °C — the divisions are the same size, only the zero is shifted.

K = °C + 273  ·  °C = K − 273
Fahrenheit (°F)

Ice melts at 32 °F and water boils at 212 °F. It is converted from Celsius by:

°F = (9/5) × °C + 32  ·  °C = (5/9) × (°F − 32)

3. Worked conversions

Example 1 — Celsius to Kelvin. Convert 27 °C to kelvin.
K = °C + 273 = 27 + 273 = 300 K
Answer = 300 K.
Example 2 — Kelvin to Celsius. Convert 373 K to degrees Celsius.
°C = K − 273 = 373 − 273 = 100 °C
Answer = 100 °C (this is the boiling point of water).
Example 3 — Celsius to Fahrenheit. Convert 20 °C to °F.
°F = (9/5) × 20 + 32 = 36 + 32 = 68 °F
Answer = 68 °F.
Example 4 — body temperature both ways. Normal body temperature is 37 °C. Find it in K and in °F.
K = 37 + 273 = 310 K
°F = (9/5) × 37 + 32 = 66.6 + 32 = 98.6 °F
So 37 °C = 310 K = 98.6 °F.

4. Fixed points — how a scale is defined

To mark a scale on a thermometer we need two known, repeatable temperatures called fixed points.

Fixed pointWhat it isCelsiusKelvin
Lower (ice) pointTemperature of pure melting ice0 °C273 K
Upper (steam) pointTemperature of steam from water boiling at normal pressure100 °C373 K

The gap between the two fixed points is called the fundamental interval — on the Celsius scale it is divided into 100 equal degrees.

5. Thermometers & thermometric properties

A thermometer works by using a physical property that changes steadily and measurably with temperature. Such a property is called a thermometric property.

ThermometerThermometric property used
Liquid-in-glass (mercury or alcohol)Expansion of a liquid — length of the liquid column
Constant-volume gas thermometerPressure of a fixed mass of gas
Resistance thermometer (platinum)Electrical resistance of a metal wire
ThermocoupleEMF (voltage) generated at the junction of two different metals
Bimetallic stripUnequal expansion of two bonded metals (strip bends)

Mercury vs alcohol: mercury is used for high temperatures and reads quickly; alcohol (dyed) is used for very low temperatures because it freezes at a much lower point than mercury.

🌟 Memory: a good thermometric property must be single-valued (one reading = one temperature), change by a fair amount, and be easy to measure.

🔥 PART 2 — Heat Capacity, Specific Heat & Mixtures

1. The heat equation Q = mcΔT

When you give heat to a body without changing its state, its temperature rises. The heat energy needed is:

Q = m c ΔT
  • Q = heat energy supplied, in joules (J)
  • m = mass of the body, in kilograms (kg) — or grams (g) if c is per gram
  • c = specific heat capacity, in J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • ΔT = change (rise) in temperature = final − initial, in K or °C (the size of a degree is the same on both scales)

Specific heat capacity (c) is defined as the heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1 °C). For water c = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ (that is 4.2 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹) — a large value, which is why water is a great coolant.

2. Heat capacity C = mc

Heat capacity (C) is the heat needed to raise the temperature of a whole body by 1 K — it depends on how much stuff there is, not just the material.

C = m c  ·  so  Q = C ΔT

Its unit is J K⁻¹ (joules per kelvin). Note the difference: specific heat capacity is "per kilogram" of the material; heat capacity is for the object as a whole.

3. Worked heat calculations

Example 1 — heat to warm water. How much heat raises 2 kg of water from 20 °C to 70 °C? (c = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)
ΔT = 70 − 20 = 50 °C
Q = m c ΔT = 2 × 4200 × 50 = 420000 J = 420 kJ
Answer = 420 kJ.
Example 2 — heat capacity of a block. A metal block of mass 0.5 kg has c = 900 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Find its heat capacity.
C = m c = 0.5 × 900 = 450 J/K
Answer = 450 J K⁻¹.
Example 3 — find the temperature rise. 6000 J is given to 0.5 kg of a liquid with c = 400 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Find ΔT.
ΔT = Q ÷ (m c) = 6000 ÷ (0.5 × 400) = 6000 ÷ 200 = 30 °C
Answer = temperature rises by 30 °C.

4. The method of mixtures

If a hot object is placed in a cold liquid in a well-insulated container, energy is conserved — no heat escapes to the surroundings. So:

Heat LOST by hot body = Heat GAINED by cold body

This single principle lets us find an unknown mass, specific heat capacity or final temperature.

Worked example. A copper block of mass 100 g at 100 °C is dropped into 200 g of water at 20 °C. (ccopper = 400 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, cwater = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.) Find the final temperature θ.
Heat lost by copper = 0.1 × 400 × (100 − θ) = 40(100 − θ)
Heat gained by water = 0.2 × 4200 × (θ − 20) = 840(θ − 20)
40(100 − θ) = 840(θ − 20)
4000 − 40θ = 840θ − 16800
4000 + 16800 = 880θ → 20800 = 880θ
θ = 20800 ÷ 880 = 23.6 °C
Final temperature ≈ 23.6 °C. (Note: the water, being heavier and having a huge c, barely warms up.)

💧 PART 3 — Latent Heat & Changes of State

1. What is latent heat?

When a substance changes state (solid → liquid, or liquid → gas) it absorbs heat but its temperature does NOT change. This "hidden" heat is called latent heat ("latent" means hidden).

Specific latent heat (L) is the heat needed to change the state of 1 kg of a substance at constant temperature.

  • Latent heat of fusion — for melting (solid ⇄ liquid). For ice, Lf = 3.36 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹ (about 336 J per gram).
  • Latent heat of vaporisation — for boiling/evaporating (liquid ⇄ gas). For water, Lv = 2.26 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹.

2. The formula Q = mL

Q = m L
  • Q = heat absorbed or released (J)
  • m = mass changing state (kg)
  • L = specific latent heat (J kg⁻¹) — use Lf for melting/freezing, Lv for boiling/condensing

Notice there is no ΔT here — the temperature is constant during the change, so a temperature term would be meaningless.

3. Why temperature stays constant

During melting or boiling the heat you supply is not raising the temperature. Instead it is doing work to break the bonds holding the particles together — pulling them apart into the more free liquid or gas state.

Because the energy goes into the potential energy of the molecules (separating them) rather than their kinetic energy (speed), the average kinetic energy — and so the temperature — stays the same until all the substance has changed state.

🌟 Idea: on a heating graph, the temperature climbs, then flattens out at the melting point and again at the boiling point. Those flat portions are where the latent heat is being absorbed.

4. Worked latent-heat calculations

Example 1 — melting ice. How much heat melts 0.5 kg of ice at 0 °C? (Lf = 3.36 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹)
Q = m Lf = 0.5 × 3.36 × 10⁵ = 1.68 × 10⁵ J = 168 kJ
Answer = 168 kJ.
Example 2 — boiling water to steam. How much heat turns 2 kg of water at 100 °C completely into steam? (Lv = 2.26 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹)
Q = m Lv = 2 × 2.26 × 10⁶ = 4.52 × 10⁶ J = 4.52 MJ
Answer = 4.52 MJ. (Vaporisation needs far more heat than melting — the molecules must be pulled fully apart.)
Example 3 — heat + change of state together. Find the heat to change 0.2 kg of ice at 0 °C into water at 20 °C. (Lf = 3.36 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹, cwater = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)
Step 1 melt the ice: Q₁ = m Lf = 0.2 × 3.36 × 10⁵ = 67200 J
Step 2 warm the water 0→20 °C: Q₂ = m c ΔT = 0.2 × 4200 × 20 = 16800 J
Total Q = 67200 + 16800 = 84000 J = 84 kJ
Answer = 84 kJ.

📐 PART 4 — Expansion, Heat Transfer & Gas Laws

1. Thermal expansion of solids

Most solids expand when heated and contract when cooled, because the particles vibrate more and push each other slightly further apart. We describe this with three expansivities.

TypeSymbolApplies toRelation
Linear expansivityα (alpha)Length—
Area (superficial) expansivityβ (beta)Areaβ = 2α
Cubic (volume) expansivityγ (gamma)Volumeγ = 3α
β = 2α  ·  γ = 3α  ·  so α : β : γ = 1 : 2 : 3

The increase in length when a rod is heated is found from:

ΔL = L₀ × α × ΔT
Worked example. A steel rod 10 m long is heated through 50 °C. If α = 1.2 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹, find its increase in length.
ΔL = L₀ α ΔT = 10 × 1.2 × 10⁻⁵ × 50
ΔL = 6 × 10⁻³ m = 0.006 m = 6 mm
Answer = 6 mm.
🌟 Real life: gaps are left in railway lines and bridges so expansion in the hot sun does not buckle them.

2. The three modes of heat transfer

Heat can move from one place to another in exactly three ways:

Conduction
  • Heat passes through a material without the material itself moving as a whole — energy is handed on from particle to particle (and by free electrons in metals).
  • Best in solids, especially metals (good conductors). Liquids and gases are poor conductors.
  • Everyday examples: a metal spoon in hot tea soon feels hot; pot handles are made of wood/plastic (insulators) so they stay cool.
Convection
  • Heat moves by the actual movement of the fluid — hot fluid becomes less dense and rises, cooler fluid sinks to take its place, setting up a convection current.
  • Happens only in fluids (liquids and gases), never in solids.
  • Everyday examples: boiling water in a pot, sea/land breezes, a room warmed by a radiator, hot-air balloons.
Radiation
  • Heat travels as electromagnetic (infrared) waves and needs no medium — it can cross a vacuum.
  • Everyday examples: the Sun's heat reaching Earth through empty space; the warmth felt in front of a fire without touching it.
  • Dull black surfaces are the best absorbers and emitters; shiny white/silver surfaces are poor absorbers and good reflectors (why teapots and roofs are often shiny).

3. The gas laws (fixed mass of gas)

For a fixed mass of gas, pressure P, volume V and (absolute) temperature T in kelvin are linked by three laws:

Boyle's law — constant temperature
P V = constant  ·  P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

At constant T, pressure is inversely proportional to volume: squeeze a gas into half the space and its pressure doubles.

Charles' law — constant pressure
V / T = constant  ·  V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Pressure (Gay-Lussac's) law — constant volume
P / T = constant  ·  P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
Combined (general) gas law
P V / T = constant  ·  P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
Worked example (Boyle's law). A gas of volume 200 cm³ at a pressure of 100 kPa is compressed at constant temperature to 50 cm³. Find the new pressure.
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
100 × 200 = P₂ × 50
P₂ = (100 × 200) ÷ 50 = 400 kPa
Answer = 400 kPa (volume quartered ÷4 → pressure ×4).
🌟 Golden rule: in every gas-law calculation the temperature MUST be in kelvin (K = °C + 273).
