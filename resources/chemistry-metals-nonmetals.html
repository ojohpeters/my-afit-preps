🔩 Metals vs Non-metals — the Big Comparison

1. First, the big picture

Every element is either a metal, a non-metal, or a rare in-between type called a metalloid. The whole topic is about spotting the differences between metals and non-metals — in how they look and behave (physical properties) and in how they react (chemical properties).

  • Metals — e.g. iron (Fe), copper (Cu), aluminium (Al), sodium (Na), zinc (Zn), gold (Au), calcium (Ca).
  • Non-metals — e.g. oxygen (O), carbon (C), sulphur (S), nitrogen (N), chlorine (Cl), phosphorus (P), hydrogen (H).
🌟 Simple picture: metals are the shiny, strong, bendable, electricity-carrying team (think spoons, wires, coins). Non-metals are the dull, brittle-or-gassy team (think charcoal, sulphur powder, the air you breathe).

2. PHYSICAL properties — side by side

This is the table examiners love. Learn it row by row.

Memory hook for the good side of metals: shiny, strong, bendy, ringing, heavy, conducting. Non-metals are basically the opposite.

3. CHEMICAL character — the real difference

Metals are ELECTROPOSITIVE

Metals have few electrons in their outer shell and lose them easily. When a metal loses electrons it becomes a positive ion (cation). Because they give away electrons, metals are called electropositive and act as reducing agents (electron donors).

Metal → positive ion + electron(s)
Ion examples:
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻  (sodium loses 1)
Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻  (magnesium loses 2)
Al → Al³⁺ + 3e⁻  (aluminium loses 3)

Non-metals are ELECTRONEGATIVE

Non-metals have nearly-full outer shells and gain electrons to complete them. When a non-metal gains electrons it becomes a negative ion (anion). Because they pull in electrons, non-metals are called electronegative and act as oxidising agents (electron acceptors).

Ion examples:
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻  (chlorine gains 1)
O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻  (oxygen gains 2)
🌟 Think of a chemical reaction as a game of pass-the-ball with electrons: metals throw electrons away (become +), non-metals catch them (become −). Opposite charges then attract → an ionic bond.

4. Where they sit in the Periodic Table

  • Metals occupy the left side and the whole centre of the table (the biggest region).
  • Non-metals are packed into the top-right corner (plus hydrogen, which sits top-left but behaves as a non-metal).
  • A zig-zag "staircase" line divides the two regions. Right along that dividing line sit the metalloids.
Metalloids (semi-metals)

A metalloid has a mix of metallic and non-metallic properties. The main ones to remember: boron (B), silicon (Si), germanium (Ge), arsenic (As) (also antimony, tellurium). Silicon and germanium are semiconductors — they conduct electricity a little, which is why silicon is used to make computer chips.

🔥 How Metals React

1. The Reactivity Series (learn this order!)

Metals differ in how eagerly they react. We line them up from most reactive (top) to least reactive (bottom). Hydrogen (a non-metal) is slotted in as a reference marker.

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > (H) > Cu > Ag > Au

Mnemonic (first letters — Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Zinc, Iron, Lead, Hydrogen, Copper, Silver, Gold):

"Poor Silly Children Must All Zip Into Lorries, Hoping Cars Stop Gently."
P-Poor · S-Silly · C-Children · M-Must · A-All · Z-Zip · I-Into · L-Lorries · H-Hoping · C-Cars · S-Stop · G-Gently

Higher up = more reactive = loses electrons more easily = harder to extract from its ore. Lower down (Cu, Ag, Au) = unreactive = found almost pure in nature and used for coins/jewellery.

2. Metal + Oxygen → a BASIC oxide

metal + oxygen → metal oxide (basic)

Reactive metals burn brightly; less reactive ones oxidise slowly; gold and silver hardly react at all.

Worked equations:
4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO  (bright white flame)
4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃
2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO  (slow, forms black coating)

These metal oxides are basic — they neutralise acids. (We build on this in the Compounds tab.)

3. Metal + Water / Steam

Very reactive metals (K, Na, Ca) + COLD water

metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen
Examples:
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑

Moderately reactive metals (Mg, Zn, Fe) + STEAM

metal + steam → metal oxide + hydrogen
Examples:
Mg + H₂O → MgO + H₂↑
Zn + H₂O → ZnO + H₂↑
3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑

Unreactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au)

No reaction with water or steam. That is why copper is safe for water pipes.

4. Metal + Dilute Acid

metal + dilute acid → salt + hydrogen

The golden rule: only metals ABOVE hydrogen in the reactivity series react with dilute acids to push out hydrogen gas. Metals below hydrogen (Cu, Ag, Au) give no reaction.

Worked equations:
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑
Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂↑
Cu + HCl → no reaction (Cu is below H)

The gas pops with a lighted splint — that is the classic test for hydrogen. (Note: K, Na, Ca react with acids far too violently to do safely, so lab demos use Mg down to Fe.)

5. Displacement reactions

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Put a piece of a reactive metal into the salt solution of a less reactive metal — the reactive one takes the place of the less reactive one, which comes out as the free metal.

Examples:
Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu  (iron is more reactive, so it displaces copper — blue solution fades, brown copper coats the iron)
Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag
Cu + ZnSO₄ → no reaction (copper is LESS reactive than zinc)

6. Why reactivity decides the extraction method

ReactivityMetalsHow they are extracted
Very reactiveK, Na, Ca, Mg, AlElectrolysis of the molten ore (strong pull needed)
Moderately reactiveZn, Fe, PbReduction with carbon / carbon monoxide (e.g. iron in a blast furnace)
UnreactiveCu, Ag, AuFound native (pure) or freed by simple heating

⚗️ Their Compounds — Oxides & More

1. The four types of OXIDES

An oxide is a compound of an element with oxygen. How the oxide behaves with acids and bases lets us sort it into four groups. This is the heart of the topic.

Type of oxideMade fromReacts with…Examples
BASICMetalsAcids only (→ salt + water)Na₂O, K₂O, CaO, MgO, CuO, FeO
ACIDICNon-metalsBases / alkalis only (→ salt + water)CO₂, SO₂, SO₃, P₄O₁₀, NO₂, SiO₂
AMPHOTERICCertain metalsBOTH acids AND basesAl₂O₃, ZnO, PbO
NEUTRALNon-metalsNeither acids nor basesCO, NO, N₂O, H₂O
🌟 Memory trick: Metal oxide = basic, Non-metal oxide = acidic. The two odd groups to memorise are the amphoteric few (Al₂O₃, ZnO, PbO — react both ways) and the neutral few (CO, NO, N₂O, H₂O — react neither way).

2. Basic oxides (metal oxides)

They neutralise acids to give a salt and water:

Examples:
CaO + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O
MgO + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂O
Soluble basic oxides + water → ALKALIS (bases)
Examples:
Na₂O + H₂O → 2NaOH  (sodium hydroxide, an alkali)
CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂  (slaked lime, an alkali)

3. Acidic oxides (non-metal oxides)

They react with bases/alkalis to give a salt and water:

Example:
CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O
Acidic oxides + water → ACIDS
Examples:
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃  (carbonic acid)
SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃  (sulphurous acid)
SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄  (sulphuric acid)

This is exactly why factory fumes of SO₂ and SO₃ dissolve in rain to make acid rain.

4. Amphoteric oxides (the two-faced ones)

Al₂O₃, ZnO and PbO react with both acids (like a base) and alkalis (like an acid).

Zinc oxide, both ways:
ZnO + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂O  (acts as a base)
ZnO + 2NaOH → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂O  (acts as an acid)

Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) behaves the same way. Remember the trio: Al₂O₃, ZnO, PbO.

5. Neutral oxides

CO, NO, N₂O and H₂O are non-metal oxides that are neither acidic nor basic — they do not react with acids or bases to form salts.

🌟 Don't be tricked: carbon has TWO oxides — CO₂ is acidic, but CO is neutral. Nitrogen too: NO₂ is acidic, but NO and N₂O are neutral.

6. Other compounds: ionic vs covalent

  • Metals form ionic (electrovalent) compounds — a metal gives electrons to a non-metal. Their chlorides and salts are ionic solids with high melting points that conduct electricity when molten or dissolved. e.g. NaCl, MgCl₂, CaCl₂.
  • Non-metals form covalent (molecular) compounds — non-metals share electrons. These are often gases or liquids with low melting points and do not conduct. e.g. CO₂, H₂O, CCl₄, HCl.

Bond typeIonic / electrovalentCovalent
