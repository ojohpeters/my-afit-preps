⚛️ PART 1 — Inside the Atom

1. What is an atom?

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction. Everything around you — water, air, your body — is built from atoms.

An atom is not solid all the way through. It has two regions:

  • A tiny central nucleus that holds the protons and neutrons. It is very small but carries almost all the mass.
  • A cloud of electrons that move around the nucleus in shells (energy levels). This region is mostly empty space.
🌟 Memory: an atom is like a stadium — the nucleus is a marble at the centre, and the electrons buzz around the far seats. Almost all of it is empty space.

2. The three subatomic particles

Every atom is built from just three particles. Learn their charge, relative mass and location — almost every exam question comes from this table.

ParticleChargeRelative massLocation
Proton (p)+1 (positive)1In the nucleus
Neutron (n)0 (neutral)1In the nucleus
Electron (e)−1 (negative)≈ 1/1840 (almost nothing)In shells around the nucleus
  • Protons and neutrons sit in the nucleus and together are called nucleons. Each has a mass of 1 unit.
  • Electrons are almost massless (about 1/1840 of a proton), so the mass of the atom is basically the mass of the nucleus.
  • Proton (+1) and electron (−1) carry equal and opposite charges; the neutron is neutral.
🌟 Memory: Proton = Positive. Neutron = neutral (no charge). Electron is the light, negative one on the outside.

3. Atomic number (Z) — the identity of the element

The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus. It is also called the proton number.

Atomic number Z = number of protons

Z decides which element it is. Every carbon atom has Z = 6; every oxygen atom has Z = 8. Change the number of protons and you change the element itself.

In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons, so Z also tells you the number of electrons.

In a neutral atom:  number of electrons = number of protons = Z

4. Mass number (A) — counting the nucleus

The mass number (A), also called the nucleon number, is the total number of protons + neutrons.

Mass number A = protons + neutrons

Rearrange this to find the neutrons — a very common exam step:

Number of neutrons = A − Z  (mass number − atomic number)

We write an atom as ZAX, for example ²³₁₁Na: the top number 23 is A, the bottom number 11 is Z.

Worked example — count everything in sodium ²³₁₁Na:
Protons = Z = 11
Electrons = Z = 11 (neutral atom)
Neutrons = A − Z = 23 − 11 = 12
So sodium-23 has 11 protons, 11 electrons and 12 neutrons.

5. Isotopes — same element, different mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons (and so different mass numbers).

Because they have the same Z, isotopes have the same chemical properties (chemistry depends on electrons). They differ only in mass.

Worked neutron counts for common isotopes
Chlorine has two isotopes, Cl-35 and Cl-37 (Z = 17):
Cl-35: neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18
Cl-37: neutrons = 37 − 17 = 20
Both are chlorine (17 protons) — they simply carry 18 or 20 neutrons.
Carbon has isotopes C-12 and C-14 (Z = 6):
C-12: neutrons = 12 − 6 = 6
C-14: neutrons = 14 − 6 = 8
🌟 Memory: isotopes are like the same car model with different-weight passengers — same engine (protons), different load (neutrons).

6. Relative atomic mass (Aᵣ)

Because an element can be a mixture of isotopes, we use an average mass called the relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) — the average mass of its atoms, weighting each isotope by how common it is (its abundance).

Aᵣ = (mass × %) added up for all isotopes, ÷ 100
Worked example — chlorine: Cl is 75% Cl-35 and 25% Cl-37.
Aᵣ = [(35 × 75) + (37 × 25)] ÷ 100
Aᵣ = (2625 + 925) ÷ 100 = 3550 ÷ 100 = 35.5
This is why chlorine has Aᵣ = 35.5 — a non-whole number, because it is an average of two isotopes.

7. Ions — atoms that gained or lost electrons

An ion is a charged atom — an atom that has lost or gained electrons so that the number of electrons no longer equals the number of protons. (The nucleus never changes; only electrons move.)

  • Lose electrons → positive ion (cation). Metals lose electrons, e.g. Na → Na⁺ (11 protons, 10 electrons, so charge +1).
  • Gain electrons → negative ion (anion). Non-metals gain electrons, e.g. Cl → Cl⁻ (17 protons, 18 electrons, so charge −1).
Charge on an ion = number of protons − number of electrons
Worked example — the magnesium ion Mg²⁺ (Z = 12):
Protons = 12 (unchanged)
Electrons = 12 − 2 = 10 (lost 2)
Charge = 12 − 10 = +2
The nucleus is untouched; magnesium simply lost 2 electrons to become Mg²⁺.

🔋 PART 2 — Electron Configuration & Atomic Models

1. Electron shells (energy levels)

Electrons do not sit anywhere they like — they occupy shells (also called energy levels) at fixed distances from the nucleus. The shells are named K, L, M, N … starting from the one nearest the nucleus.

Maximum electrons each shell can hold
ShellNameMaximum electrons
1stK2
2ndL8
3rdM8 (in simple filling; up to 18 in full)
4thNup to 18
Simple filling rule (first 20 elements): 2, 8, 8, then 2

For the first 20 elements you fill the shells in the pattern 2, then 8, then 8, and only then start the 4th shell. (The M shell can eventually hold up to 18, but it first takes 8 before the next shell begins.)

2. Writing electronic configurations

To write a configuration, take the number of electrons (= Z for a neutral atom) and fill the shells in order: 2 first, then 8, then 8.

Worked examples:
Hydrogen (Z=1): 1
Carbon (Z=6): 2, 4
Oxygen (Z=8): 2, 6
Sodium (Z=11): 2, 8, 1
Chlorine (Z=17): 2, 8, 7
Calcium (Z=20): 2, 8, 8, 2

Check by adding the numbers back up: sodium 2 + 8 + 1 = 11 ✓. If they add to Z, you filled it correctly.

3. Valence electrons — the outer shell

The valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost shell. They are the ones that take part in bonding, so they control an element's chemistry.

  • Sodium (2, 8, 1) has 1 valence electron.
  • Oxygen (2, 6) has 6 valence electrons.
  • Chlorine (2, 8, 7) has 7 valence electrons.
  • Neon (2, 8) has 8 valence electrons — a full outer shell, which is why noble gases are so stable and unreactive.
🌟 Memory: atoms want a full outer shell (usually 8, the octet). They lose, gain or share valence electrons to reach it — that is the whole reason chemical bonds form.

4. The story of the atomic models

Our picture of the atom was built up by several scientists, each improving on the one before. This is a favourite exam topic — learn who did what.

ScientistModel / discoveryKey idea
John Dalton (1803)Solid sphere (billiard ball)All matter is made of tiny, indivisible atoms; atoms of one element are identical.
J. J. Thomson (1897)‘Plum pudding’ modelDiscovered the electron. The atom is a ball of positive charge with negative electrons dotted through it, like plums in a pudding.
Ernest Rutherford (1911)Nuclear modelHis gold-foil (alpha scattering) experiment showed the atom has a tiny, dense, positive nucleus with electrons around it and mostly empty space.
Niels Bohr (1913)Shell (planetary) modelElectrons orbit the nucleus in fixed shells / energy levels, explaining why atoms are stable and give line spectra.
🌟 Memory order: Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr. Each one added a piece: solid ball → electrons in a pudding → a nucleus → electrons in shells.

5. Rutherford's gold-foil experiment

Rutherford fired positive alpha particles at a very thin sheet of gold foil. He expected them all to pass straight through the ‘pudding’. Instead:

  • Most passed straight through → the atom is mostly empty space.
  • A few were deflected at large angles, and a very few bounced straight back → there is a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus that repels them.

This overturned the plum-pudding model and gave us the nuclear atom we still use today.

🧪 PART 3 — The Periodic Table

1. How the table is arranged

The modern periodic table arranges all the elements in order of increasing atomic number (Z) — that is, by the number of protons, one after another (H=1, He=2, Li=3 …).

(An older version, by Mendeleev, used atomic mass; the modern rule of increasing atomic number fixed the odd cases and is what you use today.)

Laying them out this way makes elements with similar properties fall into the same vertical columns — that repeating pattern is why it is called ‘periodic’.

2. Groups and periods

GroupPeriod
DirectionVertical column (up/down)Horizontal row (left/right)
What is sharedSame number of valence (outer) electronsSame number of occupied electron shells
ConsequenceSimilar chemical properties (react the same way)Properties change gradually from metal to non-metal across the row
NumberingGroups 1–8 (or 1–18)Periods 1–7
Group number = number of valence electrons · Period number = number of shells
Worked example — locate sodium (2, 8, 1):
Valence electrons = 1 → Group 1
Number of shells = 3 → Period 3
So sodium sits in Group 1, Period 3.
🌟 Memory: Group = Going down (columns share valence electrons). Periods go across (rows share shells).

3. The s, p and d blocks (briefly)

The table is split into blocks by which type of sub-shell the outer electrons are filling:

  • s-block: Groups 1 and 2 (the reactive metals on the left).
  • p-block: Groups 3 to 8/0 on the right (includes most non-metals and the noble gases).
  • d-block: the transition metals in the centre (iron, copper, zinc …).

4. The key groups you must know

GroupNameValence e⁻Key facts
Group 1Alkali metals1Li, Na, K — soft, very reactive metals; react with water to give alkalis + hydrogen. Reactivity increases down the group.
Group 2Alkaline earth metals2Be, Mg, Ca — reactive metals, but less so than Group 1.
Group 7Halogens7F, Cl, Br, I — reactive non-metals; exist as diatomic molecules (Cl₂). Reactivity decreases down the group.
Group 8/0Noble gases8 (He has 2)He, Ne, Ar — full outer shell, so almost totally unreactive (inert).
Centre blockTransition metalsvariesFe, Cu, Zn — hard, dense metals; form coloured compounds; act as catalysts; show more than one valency.
🌟 Memory: alkali metals (Group 1) are the wild reactive ones; noble gases (Group 8/0) are the calm, full-shelled loners that refuse to react.

5. Metals, non-metals and the staircase

  • Metals are on the left and centre — they lose electrons, conduct electricity and heat, are shiny and malleable.
  • Non-metals are on the top right — they gain or share electrons, are poor conductors and often gases or brittle solids.
  • A zig-zag ‘staircase’ separates the two; elements along it (e.g. silicon) are metalloids with in-between properties.

📈 PART 4 — Periodic Trends (and Why)

1. Why trends exist — two competing pulls

Almost every trend comes from a tug-of-war between two things:

  • Nuclear charge (number of protons): more protons pull the electrons in more strongly.
  • Number of shells / shielding: more inner shells push the outer electrons further out and shield them from the nucleus.

Ask which one wins as you move across a period or down a group, and every trend below follows.

2. Atomic radius (size of the atom)

Across a period → DECREASES · Down a group → INCREASES
  • Across a period (left → right): the number of protons rises but electrons are added to the same shell. The stronger nuclear pull draws the shell inwards, so the atom gets smaller.
  • Down a group (top → bottom): a whole new shell is added each time, so the outer electrons are further out. The atom gets bigger.
🌟 Memory: across a row the atom shrinks (same shell, tighter grip); down a column it grows (extra shells stacked on).

3. Ionisation energy

Ionisation energy is the energy needed to remove an electron from an atom. A tightly held electron needs more energy to pull off.

Across a period → INCREASES · Down a group → DECREASES
  • Across a period: higher nuclear charge + smaller atom means electrons are held more tightly, so more energy is needed → ionisation energy increases.
  • Down a group: the outer electron is further from the nucleus and shielded by inner shells, so it is held loosely and comes off easily → ionisation energy decreases.

4. Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the power of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons towards itself.

Across a period → INCREASES · Down a group → DECREASES
  • Across a period: smaller atoms with higher nuclear charge pull shared electrons harder → electronegativity increases (fluorine, top-right, is the most electronegative).
  • Down a group: bigger atoms with more shielding pull less strongly → electronegativity decreases.
🌟 Memory: ionisation energy and electronegativity move together — both go up across a period and down a group, because both depend on how tightly the nucleus grips electrons.

5. Metallic and non-metallic character

Metallic character: across a period → DECREASES · down a group → INCREASES
  • Across a period: atoms hold their electrons more tightly, so they stop losing electrons easily → less metallic, more non-metallic. (Left side metals → right side non-metals.)
  • Down a group: outer electrons are held loosely and lost easily → more metallic.

Non-metallic character does the opposite: it increases across a period and decreases down a group.

6. Valency across a period

Valency is the combining power of an element (how many bonds it forms). Across Period 3 it rises then falls:

ElementNaMgAlSiPSClAr
Valency12343210

It goes 1, 2, 3, 4, 3, 2, 1, 0 — rising to 4 in the middle (carbon/silicon family) then falling back to 0 at the noble gas, whose full shell needs no bonds.
