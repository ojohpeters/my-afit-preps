🌸 The Basics — What Pollination Is and How It Happens

1. What is pollination?

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther (the male part) to the stigma (the female part) of a flower. Pollen carries the male sex cells; for a plant to make seeds, that pollen must reach the stigma.

Definition to memorise: Pollination = transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma.
🌟 Think of pollen like a letter that must be "posted" from the anther (post office) to the stigma (the address). Pollination is simply the delivery — by an insect, the wind, or another agent. Without delivery, no seed is formed.

Note: pollination is NOT the same as fertilisation. Pollination just delivers the pollen; fertilisation happens after, when the male cell joins the egg inside the ovule.

2. Parts of a flower (quick tour)

The male part — the STAMEN

  • Anther — the small sac at the top that produces and holds pollen grains.
  • Filament — the stalk that holds up the anther.
  • Stamen = anther + filament (the whole male part).

The female part — the CARPEL / PISTIL

  • Stigma — the top part that receives pollen.
  • Style — the stalk connecting the stigma to the ovary.
  • Ovary — the base that contains the ovules (which become seeds).
  • Carpel / Pistil = stigma + style + ovary (the whole female part).

Other parts

  • Petals — the often colourful parts that attract pollinators.
  • Sepals — the small green leaf-like parts that protect the flower bud.

3. Self-pollination vs cross-pollination

  • Self-pollination: pollen lands on the stigma of the same flower (or another flower on the same plant). No second plant needed.
  • Cross-pollination: pollen is carried to the stigma of a different flower on a different plant of the same species. This mixes characteristics and gives healthier, more varied offspring.
🌟 Self-pollination is like marrying within your own household; cross-pollination is like marrying someone from another family — it brings in fresh variety.

4. Agents of pollination

An agent is the thing that carries the pollen from anther to stigma. The main agents are:

  • Insects — bees, butterflies, moths, beetles (the most common agent).
  • Wind — air currents blow the pollen around.
  • Water — used by some water plants.
  • Birds — e.g. sunbirds (this is called ornithophily).
  • Bats — pollinate some night-opening flowers.
Two key words to define:
Entomophily = pollination by insects (entomophilous flowers).
Anemophily = pollination by wind (anemophilous flowers).

🐝 Insect-Pollinated (Entomophilous) Flowers

The big idea

Goal: the flower must attract insects and make the pollen stick to their bodies, so the insect carries it to the next flower.

Every feature below exists to either (a) attract an insect or (b) stick pollen onto it / pick pollen off it. If you understand that, you can reason out every adaptation.

1. Large, brightly coloured, conspicuous petals

Why: big bright petals are easy for insects to see and find from a distance, like a signboard advertising "food here!"
Example: the large red/yellow petals of Hibiscus and the bright orange-red flowers of the flamboyant tree are very showy.

2. Scented and produce nectar (have a nectary)

Why: sweet scent guides insects in, and the sugary nectar (made in a gland called the nectary) is the food reward that makes insects visit. As the insect feeds, it brushes against the anthers and stigma.
Example: bees are drawn to the scent of sunflower and feed on its nectar, picking up pollen in the process.

3. Pollen grains are sticky and spiky (rough)

Why: sticky, spiky/rough pollen clings firmly to the hairy body of the insect so it doesn't fall off before reaching the next flower's stigma.
🌟 The spiky surface acts like tiny Velcro hooks that grab onto an insect's hairs.

4. Pollen produced in small or moderate amounts

Why: because an insect delivers the pollen directly and reliably to another flower, the plant doesn't need to waste energy making huge quantities. A small/moderate amount is enough.

5. Stigma is small, sticky and INSIDE the flower

Why: it is sticky so the pollen brushed off the insect's body holds onto it; it sits inside the flower exactly where the visiting insect will rub against it.

6. Anthers are firm and held INSIDE the flower

Why: the anthers are firmly fixed inside so that an insect crawling in for nectar must brush against them and get dusted with sticky pollen.
More examples of insect-pollinated plants: Hibiscus, flamboyant, sunflower, and Crotalaria — all showy, scented, nectar-producing flowers.

🌬️ Wind-Pollinated (Anemophilous) Flowers

The big idea

Goal: the wind is a random, wasteful carrier — it doesn't aim. So these flowers don't bother attracting anything; instead they make huge amounts of light pollen and hang their parts outside in the breeze to catch and release it.

Notice how every feature is the opposite of an insect-pollinated flower — because there is no insect to please, only the wind to use.

1. Small, dull/green, inconspicuous petals (or none at all)

Why: there is no insect to attract, so the plant wastes no energy on big colourful petals. They are tiny, green or even absent.

2. No scent and no nectar

Why: scent and nectar exist only to lure insects. With wind doing the job, they are not needed, so the flower produces neither.

3. Large amounts of light, smooth, dry pollen

Why: the wind scatters pollen randomly and most is lost, so the plant makes huge quantities to improve the chance some lands on a stigma. The grains are light, smooth and dry so they are easily lifted and carried far by the wind.
🌟 It's like firing thousands of tiny seeds into the wind hoping a few hit the target — quantity makes up for poor aim.

4. Large, feathery stigmas hanging OUTSIDE the flower

Why: a big feathery stigma has a large surface area, like a net, and it hangs outside in the open air to catch the floating pollen as it drifts past.

5. Long filaments with anthers hanging OUTSIDE (versatile anthers)

Why: long filaments dangle the anthers outside the flower, in the wind. The anthers are versatile (loosely attached so they swing freely), so the slightest breeze shakes the pollen out into the air.
Examples of wind-pollinated plants: maize (its tassels and silky stigmas), grasses, sugarcane, and Guinea corn (sorghum).

How to answer an exam question fast

If a question describes a flower, just check: Is it showy, scented, with sticky spiky pollen and parts inside? → insect. Or is it dull, scentless, with masses of light pollen and feathery stigmas/anthers hanging outside? → wind.

🌟 One word each to remember the whole idea: insect flowers = "advertise" (be seen, smelled, rewarded). Wind flowers = "broadcast" (make lots, hang out, let the breeze do the rest).
