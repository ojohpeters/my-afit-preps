🧭 Vector Basics

1. Scalars vs vectors

  • A scalar has magnitude (size) only. Examples: mass, time, distance, speed, energy, work, temperature, electric charge.
  • A vector has magnitude AND direction. Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, weight, momentum, electric field.
🌟 "5 kg" fully describes a mass (scalar). But "5 N" is not enough for a force — you must say which way it pushes (e.g. 5 N to the right). That "which way" is what makes it a vector.
ScalarsVectors
Distance, speedDisplacement, velocity
Mass, timeForce, weight
Energy, work, powerAcceleration, momentum
Temperature, chargeElectric field, torque

2. How we draw and write a vector

  • A vector is drawn as an arrow: the length shows the magnitude (to scale) and the arrowhead shows the direction.
  • Written in bold (F) or with an arrow (F⃗). Its magnitude is written |F| or just F.
Useful vector words
  • Equal vectors — same magnitude AND same direction.
  • Negative vector — same magnitude but opposite direction (−F).
  • Unit vector — a vector of magnitude 1, used just to show direction.
  • Resultant — the single vector that has the same effect as two or more vectors added together.
  • Equilibrant — the single force that balances the others (equal in size but opposite to the resultant).

3. The idea of a resultant

If two people push a box — one with 3 N east and another with 4 N north — the box moves as if a single force acted on it. That single equivalent force is the resultant. Finding resultants is what most of this topic is about.

💡 Two forces in the same direction simply add (3 N + 4 N = 7 N). In opposite directions they subtract (4 N − 3 N = 1 N). At an angle, we need the parallelogram law or resolution (next tab).

➕ Adding & Resolving Vectors

1. Two vectors in a line

  • Same direction: add them. Max resultant of P and Q = P + Q.
  • Opposite directions: subtract. Min resultant of P and Q = |P − Q|.

2. Two vectors at 90° (perpendicular)

Use Pythagoras for the size, and tan for the direction:

R = √(P² + Q²)   |   tan θ = opposite ÷ adjacent
Worked example: Forces 3 N (east) and 4 N (north) act at a point. Find the resultant.
R = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5 N
Direction: tan θ = 4 ÷ 3 = 1.333 → θ = 53° north of east
So the resultant is 5 N at 53° to the 3 N force. (The famous 3-4-5 triangle.)

3. Two vectors at any angle — the parallelogram law

If two vectors P and Q act at a point with angle θ between them, the resultant is the diagonal of the parallelogram they form:

R = √(P² + Q² + 2PQ·cos θ)
Direction: tan α = Q·sin θ ÷ (P + Q·cos θ)
Worked example: Two forces 5 N and 8 N act at a point with 60° between them. Find the resultant.
R = √(5² + 8² + 2·5·8·cos60°)
= √(25 + 64 + 80 × 0.5) = √(89 + 40) = √129 ≈ 11.4 N
🧠 Note: when θ = 0° (same direction) this gives R = P + Q; when θ = 180° (opposite) it gives R = |P − Q|; when θ = 90° it reduces to √(P²+Q²). One formula covers all cases.

4. Resolving a vector into components (the master skill)

Any single vector F at angle θ to the horizontal can be split into two perpendicular parts — a horizontal part and a vertical part:

Horizontal: Fₓ = F·cos θ   |   Vertical: F_y = F·sin θ
💡 Golden rule: the component along the angle you measured from uses cos; the component across it uses sin. (Angle from horizontal → horizontal part is cos.)
Worked example: Resolve a 100 N force acting at 30° to the horizontal.
Fₓ = 100·cos30° = 100 × 0.866 = 86.6 N
F_y = 100·sin30° = 100 × 0.5 = 50 N

5. Many vectors at once — the component method (always works)

For 3 or more vectors, resolve each into x and y parts, add them up, then recombine:

  • ① Find every Fₓ and add them → ΣFₓ
  • ② Find every F_y and add them → ΣF_y
  • ③ Resultant R = √(ΣFₓ² + ΣF_y²)
  • ④ Direction tan θ = ΣF_y ÷ ΣFₓ

Watch the signs: forces pointing left or down are negative. This method is your safest tool for any messy problem.

⚖️ Equilibrium & Lami's Theorem

1. What "equilibrium" means

A body is in equilibrium when the forces on it are balanced — there is no resultant force, so it stays at rest (or moves at constant velocity).

For equilibrium: ΣFₓ = 0  AND  ΣF_y = 0  (the vector sum of all forces = 0)

If three forces act on a body in equilibrium, drawn head-to-tail they form a closed triangle (the "triangle of forces").

2. Lami's Theorem (the shortcut for THREE forces)

Lami's theorem is a neat way to solve problems with exactly three concurrent forces (all acting at one point) that are in equilibrium.

P / sin α = Q / sin β = R / sin γ

where each force is divided by the sine of the angle between the OTHER two forces:

  • α = angle between Q and R (the angle "opposite" force P)
  • β = angle between R and P (opposite Q)
  • γ = angle between P and Q (opposite R)
  • and the three angles add up: α + β + γ = 360°
🧠 Memory: "each force ÷ sine of the angle FACING it." Use Lami only when you have three forces meeting at a point, in equilibrium, with the angles between them known.

3. Lami worked example — a hanging load on two strings

A load of weight 30 N hangs from two strings. String A makes 30° with the ceiling on the left; string B makes 60° with the ceiling on the right. Find the tensions T_A and T_B.

Step 1 — find the angles between the three forces at the load.

Weight pulls straight down. String A pulls up-left (30° above horizontal); string B pulls up-right (60° above horizontal).

Angle between the two strings (T_A & T_B) = 180° − 30° − 60° = 90°
Angle between T_B and the weight W = 90° + 60° = 150°
Angle between W and T_A = 90° + 30° = 120°
Check: 90° + 150° + 120° = 360° ✓
Step 2 — apply Lami's theorem. Each force ÷ sine of the angle opposite it:
T_A / sin150° = T_B / sin120° = W / sin90°
W / sin90° = 30 / 1 = 30
T_A = 30 × sin150° = 30 × 0.5 = 15 N
T_B = 30 × sin120° = 30 × 0.866 = 26 N (approx)
Answer: T_A = 15 N, T_B ≈ 26 N.
✅ Cross-check by resolution: ΣFₓ = 0 gives T_B = √3·T_A, and ΣF_y = 0 gives 0.5T_A + 0.866T_B = 30 → T_A = 15 N, T_B ≈ 26 N. Same answer — proof both methods agree.

4. When to use which method

SituationBest method
Two vectors, find resultantParallelogram law (or Pythagoras if 90°)
Three forces in equilibrium, angles knownLami's theorem (fastest)
Any number of forces / messy anglesComponent method (ΣFₓ = 0, ΣF_y = 0)

Lami and the component method give the same answers — Lami is just quicker for the classic "three-string / hanging-mass" questions.

🧩 Solving Complex Vector Problems

A universal 4-step strategy

  • ① Draw a clear diagram; mark every force and every angle.
  • ② Pick a method: parallelogram (2 forces), Lami (3 forces in equilibrium), or components (anything else).
  • ③ For equilibrium, write ΣFₓ = 0 and ΣF_y = 0.
  • ④ Solve the equations for the unknowns.

Problem 1 — Object on an inclined plane

A block of weight 50 N rests on a smooth (frictionless) plane inclined at 30°. Find (a) the component of weight along the plane and (b) the normal reaction.

Key idea: resolve the weight into two parts — one down the slope and one into the slope.
Component down the plane = W·sin θ = 50·sin30° = 50 × 0.5 = 25 N
Component into the plane = W·cos θ = 50·cos30° = 50 × 0.866 = 43.3 N
Normal reaction N = W·cos θ = 43.3 N
To hold the block still (no friction), a force of 25 N up the plane is needed.
⚠️ Common trap: students swap them. On an incline the down-slope pull is W·sinθ and the normal reaction is W·cosθ. Steeper slope (bigger θ) → bigger sin → block slides more easily. That sanity-check helps you remember.

Problem 2 — Weight held by two symmetric strings

A weight W = 100 N hangs from a point supported by two strings, each making 30° with the vertical. Find the tension in each string.

By symmetry both tensions are equal (= T). Their vertical parts together hold up the weight:
2·T·cos θ = W (each vertical component is T·cos30°)
2·T·cos30° = 100 → 2 × 0.866 × T = 100 → 1.732·T = 100
T = 100 ÷ 1.732 = 57.7 N
Each string carries ≈ 57.7 N.
💡 Notice: the wider apart the strings (angle to vertical grows), the larger the tension — which is why a tightly stretched clothesline can snap under a small weight.

Problem 3 — Resultant of three forces (component method)

Three forces act at a point: 6 N along +x (east), 8 N along +y (north), and 4 N along −x (west). Find the resultant.

ΣFₓ = 6 − 4 = 2 N    ΣF_y = 8 N
R = √(ΣFₓ² + ΣF_y²) = √(2² + 8²) = √(4 + 64) = √68 ≈ 8.25 N
Direction: tan θ = 8 ÷ 2 = 4 → θ ≈ 76° north of east
Resultant ≈ 8.25 N at 76° to the east direction.

Problem 4 — Force pulling a hanging body sideways (three forces, Lami or components)

A body of weight 20 N hangs from a string. A horizontal force P pulls it aside until the string makes 30° with the vertical. Find the tension T in the string and the force P.

Three forces: weight W (down), tension T (along string), horizontal pull P. Resolve about the body:
Vertical: T·cos30° = W → T = 20 ÷ cos30° = 20 ÷ 0.866 = 23.1 N
Horizontal: P = T·sin30° = 23.1 × 0.5 = 11.55 N
Answer: T ≈ 23.1 N, P ≈ 11.6 N.
🧠 Same problem by Lami: angle between T and P = 120°, between P and W = 90°, between W and T = 150°. Then W/sin(angle between T&P) etc. — gives the identical T and P. Choose whichever is faster for you.

⭐ Exam traps to avoid

  • Mixing up cos and sin — the component along the measured angle uses cos.
  • Forgetting direction/signs — leftward and downward forces are negative in the component method.
  • Using Lami when the forces are not three, or not in equilibrium, or not concurrent.
  • On inclines: down-slope = W sin θ, normal = W cos θ (not the other way round).
  • Reading the angle from the wrong reference line — always note whether it's measured from the horizontal or the vertical.
