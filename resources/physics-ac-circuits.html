📖 Understanding Alternating Current

1. What is A.C.? (and how it differs from D.C.)

  • Direct Current (D.C.) flows in one direction only — like from a battery. Its graph is a flat straight line.
  • Alternating Current (A.C.) keeps reversing direction, back and forth, many times a second. Its graph is a smooth sine wave.
🌟 Real life: the electricity in your wall socket is A.C. In Nigeria/UK it reverses 50 times a second (50 Hz); in the USA, 60 times a second (60 Hz). A battery in a torch is D.C.

2. The shape of A.C. — key words

A.C. voltage/current follows a sine curve: V = V₀ sin(2πft) or I = I₀ sin(ωt). The words you must know:

TermMeaning
Peak value (V₀, I₀)The maximum height of the wave (amplitude).
CycleOne complete up-and-down of the wave.
Period (T)Time for one cycle, in seconds.
Frequency (f)Number of cycles per second, in hertz (Hz). f = 1/T.
Angular frequency (ω)ω = 2πf (radians per second).
Worked example: A.C. mains has f = 50 Hz. Find its period and angular frequency.
T = 1/f = 1/50 = 0.02 s
ω = 2πf = 2 × 3.142 × 50 = 314 rad/s

3. R.M.S. and peak values (very important!)

Because A.C. is always changing, we use an “effective” value called the root-mean-square (r.m.s.) value. The r.m.s. value of A.C. is the D.C. value that would give the same heating effect.

Vrms = V₀ / √2 = 0.707 V₀   |   Irms = I₀ / √2
V₀ = √2 × Vrms  (peak = 1.414 × rms)

⚠️ When we say “the mains is 220 V,” that 220 V is the r.m.s. value. Its actual peak is much higher!

Worked example: Mains r.m.s. voltage is 220 V. Find the peak voltage.
V₀ = √2 × 220 = 1.414 × 220 = 311 V
Worked example 2: An A.C. has peak current 4 A. Find the r.m.s. current.
Irms = I₀/√2 = 4/1.414 = 2.83 A

4. Why is A.C. used for mains supply?

  • A.C. voltage can be easily stepped up or down using a transformer (D.C. cannot).
  • High voltage = low current for transmission → less energy lost as heat in the cables.
  • It is cheaper to generate with rotating generators (alternators).

⚙️ R, C and L in A.C. Circuits

1. The big idea: opposition to A.C. = "impedance"

In D.C. the only opposition is resistance (R). In A.C., capacitors and inductors also oppose the current — this A.C. opposition is called reactance (X). The total combined opposition is called impedance (Z), all measured in ohms (Ω).

💡 Think of impedance as the "A.C. version of resistance" — it controls the current using Ohm’s law for A.C.: V = I × Z.

2. Pure RESISTOR in A.C.

Opposition = R (just normal resistance). Voltage and current are IN PHASE (rise and fall together).

A resistor behaves the same in A.C. as in D.C. — no phase shift. Ohm’s law applies directly: Vrms = Irms × R.

3. Pure CAPACITOR in A.C. — capacitive reactance

XC = 1 / (2πfC) = 1/(ωC)
  • A capacitor’s opposition is called capacitive reactance (XC), in ohms.
  • Higher frequency → smaller XC (capacitor passes high frequencies more easily).
  • Phase: in a capacitor, the current LEADS the voltage by 90° (current peaks a quarter-cycle before voltage). Memory: “ICE” → in a Capacitor (C), I leads E(voltage).
Worked example: A 50 µF capacitor in a 50 Hz circuit. Find XC.
XC = 1/(2πfC) = 1/(2 × 3.142 × 50 × 50×10⁻⁶)
= 1/(0.0157) = 63.7 Ω

4. Pure INDUCTOR in A.C. — inductive reactance

XL = 2πfL = ωL
  • An inductor (coil) opposes A.C. with inductive reactance (XL), in ohms.
  • Higher frequency → larger XL (inductor blocks high frequencies).
  • Phase: in an inductor, the voltage LEADS the current by 90° (current lags). Memory: “ELI” → in an inductor (L), E(voltage) leads I.
🧠 Memory sentence: “ELI the ICE man.” ELI = in inductor(L), voltage(E) before current(I). ICE = in capacitor(C), current(I) before voltage(E).
Worked example: A 0.2 H inductor at 50 Hz. Find XL.
XL = 2πfL = 2 × 3.142 × 50 × 0.2 = 62.8 Ω

🔗 Series RLC Circuits & Resonance

1. Impedance of a series RLC circuit

When R, L and C are in series, they don’t simply add — because L and C are 90° out of phase with R (and opposite to each other). We combine them with a right-angled (Pythagoras) formula:

Z = √[ R² + (XL − XC)² ]

Then the current comes from A.C. Ohm’s law:

Irms = Vrms / Z
Worked example: R = 30 Ω, XL = 80 Ω, XC = 40 Ω. Find Z.
XL − XC = 80 − 40 = 40 Ω
Z = √(30² + 40²) = √(900 + 1600) = √2500 = 50 Ω

2. Phase angle & power factor

tan φ = (XL − XC) / R   |   power factor = cos φ = R / Z
  • φ (phi) is the phase angle between voltage and current.
  • If XL > XC → circuit is inductive (current lags).
  • If XC > XL → circuit is capacitive (current leads).
From the example above (R=30, Z=50): power factor = R/Z = 30/50 = 0.6.

3. Resonance (the star topic!)

Resonance happens when the inductor and capacitor exactly cancel out: XL = XC. At that special frequency:

  • The (XL − XC) term becomes 0, so Z = R (impedance is at its MINIMUM).
  • Since Z is smallest, the current is at its MAXIMUM.
  • The circuit behaves as if purely resistive → voltage and current are in phase (power factor = 1).
At resonance: XL = XC  ⇒  f₀ = 1 / (2π√(LC))
🌟 Real life: this is how a radio is TUNED — you adjust C so the circuit resonates at one station’s frequency, giving maximum current for just that station.
Worked example: Find the resonant frequency for L = 2 H and C = 8 µF.
f₀ = 1/(2π√(LC)) = 1/(2π√(2 × 8×10⁻⁶))
√(16×10⁻⁶) = 4×10⁻³ → f₀ = 1/(2π × 0.004) = 1/0.02513 ≈ 39.8 Hz

⚡ Power in A.C. & Real-Life Uses

1. Real power in an A.C. circuit

In D.C., power = VI. In A.C., because voltage and current can be out of phase, the true (average) power is:

P = Vrms × Irms × cos φ

The term cos φ is the power factor. Only the resistor uses up real power; a pure capacitor or pure inductor uses zero average power (they store and return energy each cycle).

Worked example: Vrms = 200 V, Irms = 5 A, power factor = 0.8. Find the real power.
P = 200 × 5 × 0.8 = 800 W

2. Power in a purely resistive A.C. circuit

If the circuit is only a resistor, φ = 0 and cos φ = 1, so:

P = Vrms × Irms = Irms² R
Example: Irms = 3 A through R = 20 Ω → P = 3² × 20 = 180 W.

3. Wattless (reactive) current

The current component that is 90° out of phase with the voltage (through pure L or C) delivers no real power — it is called wattless current. That is why an ideal capacitor/inductor consumes no energy on average.

4. Transformers (a key A.C. device)

A transformer changes A.C. voltage using two coils. For an ideal transformer:

Vs/Vp = Ns/Np   and   VpIp = VsIs
  • Step-up: more turns on secondary → higher voltage (used to send power over long distances).
  • Step-down: fewer turns on secondary → lower voltage (used to power home appliances).
  • Transformers work on A.C. ONLY (they need a changing magnetic field).
Example: Primary 240 V, 1000 turns; secondary 100 turns. Secondary voltage?
Vs = Vp × (Ns/Np) = 240 × (100/1000) = 24 V (step-down)

5. Measuring A.C.

  • A.C. is measured with instruments that respond to r.m.s. values (e.g. a hot-wire ammeter or an a.c. moving-iron meter).
  • A moving-coil galvanometer alone reads zero for pure A.C. (the needle can’t follow the fast reversals) — it needs a rectifier first.
  • A C.R.O. (oscilloscope) shows the actual wave shape and lets you read the peak value and period.
