🧬 PART 1 — The Foundations of Organic Chemistry

1. What is organic chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon compounds — the study of the structure, properties and reactions of compounds that contain carbon (almost always joined to hydrogen).

A few simple carbon compounds — carbon dioxide (CO₂), carbon monoxide (CO), the carbonates (CO₃²⁻) and hydrogencarbonates — are counted as inorganic by tradition. Everything else built on carbon chains and rings (fuels, plastics, alcohols, fats, sugars, proteins) is organic.

Why is carbon so special?
  • There are millions of known organic compounds but only a few hundred thousand inorganic ones — carbon dominates chemistry.
  • It is the element of life: your food, fuel, medicine and body are all carbon compounds.
🌟 Memory: Organic = carbon. If a compound has carbon chains with hydrogen, think organic.

2. The unique properties of carbon

Two special properties make carbon able to form millions of compounds:

(a) Tetravalency

Carbon has 4 electrons in its outermost shell, so it needs 4 more to fill the shell. It does this by sharing — forming 4 covalent bonds. We say carbon is tetravalent (valency = 4). This lets one carbon bond to up to four other atoms at once.

Carbon always forms 4 covalent bonds
(b) Catenation

Catenation is the ability of carbon atoms to join to one another to form long chains, branched chains and rings. Carbon–carbon bonds are strong and stable, so these chains can be very long.

🌟 Picture it: carbon atoms are like LEGO bricks with 4 studs each — they clip to each other endlessly (catenation) and to hydrogen (tetravalency), building millions of shapes.

3. Hydrocarbons — the simplest organic compounds

A hydrocarbon is a compound made of only carbon and hydrogen. They are grouped into families:

  • Alkanes — only single bonds (saturated), general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂.
  • Alkenes — contain a C=C double bond (unsaturated), general formula CₙH₂ₙ.
  • Alkynes — contain a C≡C triple bond (unsaturated), general formula CₙH₂ₙ₋₂.

4. Saturated vs unsaturated

SaturatedUnsaturated

🌟 Saturated = “full up” on hydrogen (all single bonds). Unsaturated = has room for more (double/triple bonds that can open up and add atoms).

5. The homologous series

A homologous series is a family of organic compounds with the same general formula and the same functional group, in which successive members differ by −CH₂−.

Features of a homologous series
  • All members can be represented by one general formula (e.g. alkanes = CₙH₂ₙ₊₂).
  • Successive members differ by a −CH₂− group (a mass difference of 14).
  • They have similar chemical properties (same functional group).
  • Physical properties (boiling point, density) change gradually down the series as chain length grows.
  • They can be made by similar methods (general preparations).
🌟 Think of a homologous series as one family: each brother looks a bit taller than the last (adds a −CH₂−) but they all behave alike.

7. Structural isomerism

Structural isomers are compounds that have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements of their atoms. Same atoms, different way of joining them — so they can have different properties.

Classic example — C₄H₁₀: the formula C₄H₁₀ can be arranged two ways:
n-butane: CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ (a straight chain)
2-methylpropane: a branched chain (same C₄H₁₀)
Both are C₄H₁₀, yet they are different compounds with different boiling points.
🌟 Same LEGO bricks, different build — same molecular formula, different structure = isomers.

8. IUPAC naming basics

IUPAC names are built from three parts: prefix + stem + suffix.

  • Stem = number of carbon atoms in the longest chain:
Carbons123456
Stemmethethpropbutpenthex
  • Suffix = the family (functional group): -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -yne (alkyne), -anol (alcohol), -anoic acid (carboxylic acid).
  • Prefix / numbers tell you where branches or double bonds are (e.g. but-1-ene, 2-methylbutane).
Reading a name: “prop-ene” = prop (3 carbons) + ene (has a C=C) → propene, C₃H₆.

⛽ PART 2 — The Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)

1. What are alkanes?

The alkanes are the saturated hydrocarbons — they contain only carbon–carbon single bonds. Each carbon is bonded to the maximum number of hydrogen atoms, so they are “full up”.

General formula of alkanes:  CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

Put in a value of n to get any member: for n = 1, C₁H₄ = CH₄ (methane); for n = 3, C₃H₈ = propane.

2. The first members — name table

n (carbons)NameMolecular formula
1MethaneCH₄
2EthaneC₂H₆
3PropaneC₃H₈
4ButaneC₄H₁₀
5PentaneC₅H₁₂
6HexaneC₆H₁₄
🌟 Learn the stems in order: Meth, Eth, Prop, But, Pent, Hex — “Monkeys Eat Peanut Butter Pretty Happily”. These same stems are reused for every family.

3. Sources of alkanes

The alkanes are obtained mainly from petroleum (crude oil) and natural gas.

  • Natural gas is mostly methane (CH₄).
  • Petroleum is a mixture of many alkanes separated by fractional distillation (see Part 4).

4. Physical properties

  • The first four (methane → butane) are gases; the middle members are liquids; long-chain alkanes are solids (waxes).
  • They are non-polar, so they are insoluble in water but dissolve in organic solvents.
  • Boiling point rises as the chain gets longer (bigger molecules, stronger forces).

5. Chemical reactions of alkanes

Alkanes are saturated and therefore fairly unreactive. Their two important reactions are combustion and substitution.

(a) Combustion (burning)

Alkanes burn in air — this is why they are used as fuels.

Complete combustion:  alkane + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat
Worked example — methane burning:
CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
In plenty of air you get carbon dioxide and water. In limited air (incomplete combustion) you get poisonous carbon monoxide (CO) or soot (C) instead.
(b) Substitution (halogenation)

In the presence of sunlight / UV light, a halogen (e.g. chlorine) replaces hydrogen atoms one at a time. This is a substitution reaction (a hydrogen is swapped for a chlorine).

Worked example — methane + chlorine:
CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl
The organic product is chloromethane (CH₃Cl); the reaction only happens in UV light.
🌟 Substitution = swap (one atom takes another's place). Addition (alkenes) = join on. Alkanes do substitution because they have no double bond to open.

🔗 PART 3 — Alkenes & Alkynes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

1. The alkenes

The alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon–carbon double bond (C=C).

General formula of alkenes:  CₙH₂ₙ
n (carbons)NameMolecular formula
2EtheneC₂H₄
3PropeneC₃H₆
4ButeneC₄H₈

The simplest alkene is ethene, C₂H₄ — written CH₂=CH₂. (There is no such thing as “methene” because you need at least 2 carbons for a C=C bond.)

2. Addition reactions of alkenes

Because the C=C double bond can open up, alkenes are reactive and undergo addition reactions — a small molecule adds across the double bond, turning it into a single bond.

(a) Hydrogenation (add H₂)
ethene + hydrogen → ethane  (C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆)

With a nickel catalyst, hydrogen adds on and the alkene becomes an alkane. (This is how liquid oils are hardened into margarine.)

(b) Halogenation (add a halogen)
ethene + bromine → 1,2-dibromoethane  (C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂)
(c) Hydration (add H₂O / steam)
ethene + steam → ethanol  (C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH)

With steam and a catalyst (phosphoric acid), water adds across the C=C to make ethanol — the industrial route to alcohol.

3. Test for unsaturation — bromine water

This is a very common exam point. Bromine water is reddish-brown/orange.

Unsaturated compound + bromine water → decolourised (turns colourless)
  • An alkene (or alkyne) reacts by addition and decolourises bromine water quickly — the orange colour disappears.
  • An alkane has no double bond, so it does NOT decolourise bromine water (stays orange).
🌟 Rule: “Decolourises bromine water = unsaturated”. It is the standard test to tell an alkene from an alkane.

4. Polymerisation

Polymerisation is the joining together of many small molecules (monomers) to form one very large molecule (polymer).

n(CH₂=CH₂) → −(CH₂−CH₂)ₙ−  (many ethene → poly(ethene))

Many ethene molecules add together to form poly(ethene), commonly called polythene — the plastic used for bags and bottles. This is addition polymerisation.

🌟 Monomer = one bead; polymer = the whole necklace of thousands of beads joined up.

5. The alkynes

The alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing a carbon–carbon triple bond (C≡C).

General formula of alkynes:  CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
n (carbons)NameMolecular formula
2EthyneC₂H₂
3PropyneC₃H₄
4ButyneC₄H₆

The most important alkyne is ethyne, C₂H₂ (old name acetylene), written H–C≡C–H. It burns with a very hot flame and is used in oxy-acetylene welding. Like alkenes, alkynes are unsaturated and also decolourise bromine water.

🍷 PART 4 — Alkanols, Alkanoic Acids & Petroleum

1. The alkanols (alcohols)

The alkanols are the family with the −OH (hydroxyl) functional group.

General formula of alkanols:  CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH
NameFormula
MethanolCH₃OH
EthanolC₂H₅OH
PropanolC₃H₇OH

The best-known is ethanol, C₂H₅OH — the alcohol in drinks, used as a fuel, a solvent and an antiseptic.

2. Making ethanol by fermentation

Fermentation is the breakdown of sugar (glucose) into ethanol and carbon dioxide by the enzyme zymase in yeast, in the absence of air.

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂  (glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide)
  • Catalyst: the enzyme zymase (from yeast).
  • Best at about 37 °C, without air (anaerobic).

Industrially, ethanol is also made by the hydration of ethene (C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH), as seen in Part 3.

3. Oxidation of alkanols

When ethanol is oxidised (by air/bacteria, or by an oxidising agent like acidified KMnO₄ or K₂Cr₂O₇) it is converted step by step:

ethanol → ethanal → ethanoic acid
Everyday example: when wine or palm-wine is left open, bacteria oxidise the ethanol in it to ethanoic acid — that is why it turns sour and tastes like vinegar.

4. The alkanoic acids (carboxylic acids)

The alkanoic acids have the −COOH (carboxyl) functional group. They are weak acids that turn blue litmus red and react with bases and carbonates.

General formula:  CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH
NameFormula
Methanoic acidHCOOH
Ethanoic acidCH₃COOH
Propanoic acidC₂H₅COOH

The common one is ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH — the acid in vinegar.

5. Esterification

Esterification is the reaction of an alkanoic acid with an alkanol (with concentrated H₂SO₄ as catalyst) to form an ester and water. It is a reversible reaction.

alkanoic acid + alkanol ⇌ ester + water
Worked example:
CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
ethanoic acid + ethanol → ethyl ethanoate (an ester) + water. Esters have sweet, fruity smells and are used in perfumes and flavourings.
🌟 Remember: acid + alcohol → ester + water (the −OH of the acid and the −H of the alcohol come off together as H₂O).

6. Petroleum — fractional distillation

Petroleum (crude oil) is a mixture of many hydrocarbons (mostly alkanes). It is separated into useful fractions by fractional distillation in a tall fractionating column.

  • Crude oil is heated; the vapours rise up the column, which is hot at the bottom, cooler at the top.
  • Fractions with low boiling points (small molecules) rise high before condensing; fractions with high boiling points (large molecules) condense low down.
Fraction (top → bottom)Use
Refinery gasesBottled gas (LPG) / cooking
Petrol (gasoline)Fuel for cars
KeroseneJet fuel, lamps, stoves
Diesel oilFuel for lorries and buses
Lubricating oil / bitumenLubricants, waxes, road tar

7. Cracking

Cracking is the breaking down of large, less useful alkane molecules into smaller, more useful ones (including alkenes) using heat and a catalyst.

large alkane → smaller alkane + alkene
Example:
C₁₀H₂₂ → C₈H₁₈ + C₂H₄
A big decane molecule cracks into octane (petrol) + ethene. Cracking is important because it: (1) makes more petrol from surplus heavy oil, and (2) produces alkenes like ethene for making plastics.
