🧩 Phrases — Grammatical Name & Function

1. What this topic really asks

In this WAEC/JAMB question, a group of words in a sentence is underlined and you must give two things:

  • The grammatical NAME — what kind of group it is (e.g. noun phrase, adverbial clause).
  • The FUNCTION — the job it does in the sentence (e.g. subject, object, qualifies a noun, modifies a verb).
🌟 Think of it like this: the name is the group’s job title, the function is what it actually does in that particular sentence. You must give both.

2. What is a phrase?

A phrase is a group of words that makes sense together but has NO finite (complete) verb and no subject-verb pair. It cannot stand alone as a sentence.

Grammatical nameWhat it does (function)Example (phrase in bold)
Noun phrasesubject, object or complementThe tall dark man smiled.
Adjectival phrasequalifies / describes a nounThe girl in the blue dress is my sister.
Adverbial phrasemodifies a verb, adjective or adverb (shows time/place/manner)She sang in a sweet voice.
Prepositional phrasebegins with a preposition; acts as an adjective or adverbThe book on the table is mine.
Verb phrasethe verb group (main verb + auxiliaries)She has been working hard.

Note: a prepositional phrase and an adjectival/adverbial phrase can be the same group of words — say prepositional if it starts with a preposition, and add whether it works as an adjective (qualifies a noun) or adverb (modifies a verb).

3. Worked phrase examples

Example 1: ‘The house with the red roof was sold.’

Name: adjectival phrase (a prepositional phrase). Function: it qualifies the noun ‘house’.

Example 2: ‘He drove with great care.’

Name: adverbial phrase (of manner). Function: it modifies the verb ‘drove’.

Example 3: ‘A loaf of bread costs a lot now.’

Name: noun phrase. Function: it is the subject of the verb ‘costs’.

🔗 Clauses — Grammatical Name & Function

1. What is a clause?

A clause is a group of words that DOES contain a subject and a finite verb. That single test — is there a finite verb? — is how you tell a clause from a phrase.

  • Main (independent) clause: makes complete sense on its own.
  • Subordinate (dependent) clause: does not make complete sense alone; it does a job inside the sentence.

2. The three subordinate clauses

NameJob (function)Common startersExample (clause in bold)
Noun clauseacts as a noun → subject, object or complementthat, what, whether, why, how, whoI know that he is honest.
Adjectival (relative) clausequalifies / describes a nounwho, whom, whose, which, thatThe boy who won the prize is my friend.
Adverbial clausemodifies the verb (shows time, place, reason…)when, where, because, if, although, so thatShe cried because she failed.

3. Types of adverbial clause (know them all)

Adverbial clause of…StartersExample
Timewhen, while, after, before, as, untilWe will start when he arrives.
Placewhere, whereverHe hid it where no one could see.
Reason / causebecause, since, asShe left because she was tired.
Conditionif, unless, providedIf you study hard, you will pass.
Concessionalthough, though, even thoughAlthough he is rich, he is stingy.
Purposeso that, in order thatHe came early so that he might help.
Resultso … that, such … thatHe ran so fast that he fell.
Manneras, as if, as thoughShe spoke as if she was angry.

🎯 How to Answer ‘Name & Function’ Questions

The 2-step method examiners want

STEP 1: Give the grammatical NAME  →  STEP 2: State its FUNCTION
  • Step 1 — Name it. Is it a phrase or a clause (look for a finite verb)? Then which type — noun / adjectival / adverbial (and the adverbial sub-type)?
  • Step 2 — Function. Say the exact job using phrases like: ‘it is the subject of the sentence’, ‘it is the object of the verb ___’, ‘it qualifies the noun ___’, ‘it modifies the verb ___’, ‘it is the complement of ___’, or ‘it is in apposition to ___’.
💡 Quick test: does the group answer which/what kind? → it is adjectival (qualifies a noun). Does it answer when/where/why/how? → it is adverbial (modifies a verb). Can it be replaced by ‘it/something’ and act as subject/object? → it is a noun group.

Six fully worked examples

1. ‘The girl in the red dress is my cousin.’

Name: adjectival phrase (prepositional phrase). Function: qualifies the noun ‘girl’.

2. ‘He spoke as if he knew everything.’

Name: adverbial clause of manner. Function: modifies the verb ‘spoke’.

3. ‘That she passed the exam delighted her parents.’

Name: noun clause. Function: subject of the verb ‘delighted’.

4. ‘I met the man whom you described.’

Name: adjectival (relative) clause. Function: qualifies the noun ‘man’.

5. ‘They arrived before the gate was closed.’

Name: adverbial clause of time. Function: modifies the verb ‘arrived’.

6. ‘My father, a strict disciplinarian, punished us.’

Name: noun phrase. Function: in apposition to ‘my father’.

🛠️ Functions in the Sentence & Common Traps

The functions a group can perform

FunctionMeaningExample (group in bold)
Subjectwho/what the sentence is about (does the action)The old man slept.
Direct objectreceives the action of the verbShe bought a new car.
Indirect objectto/for whom the action is doneShe gave the boy a book.
Complementcompletes the meaning after ‘be/seem’ etc.He is a brilliant doctor.
Qualifier of a noundescribes a noun (adjectival)the house on the hill
Adjunct / modifier of a verbtells how/when/where an action happens (adverbial)He ran very quickly.
Appositiona noun group renaming another right beside itMusa, our captain, scored.

Common traps (where students lose marks)

  • Phrase vs clause: the one and only test is a finite verb. No verb → phrase; has subject + finite verb → clause.
  • Adjectival vs adverbial clause: if it describes a noun (answers which/what kind) it is adjectival; if it tells you about the verb (when/where/why/how) it is adverbial.
  • Noun clause as object vs adverbial clause: ‘I know that he is honest’ = noun clause (object). ‘He left so that he could rest’ = adverbial clause of purpose.
  • Give BOTH parts: a name without a function (or vice-versa) only earns half the marks.
  • Be specific with the function: say ‘object of the verb gave’, not just ‘object’; say ‘qualifies the noun man’, not just ‘qualifies a noun’.
