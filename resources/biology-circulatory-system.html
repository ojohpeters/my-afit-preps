❤️ PART 1 — The Mammalian Heart

1. Why do we need a circulatory system?

Every cell in your body needs a constant supply of oxygen and digested food, and must get rid of wastes such as carbon dioxide and urea. In a large animal, simple diffusion is far too slow to reach every cell. So we have a transport (circulatory) system — a pump (the heart) plus a network of tubes (blood vessels) carrying a transport fluid (blood).

The three parts of the transport system
  • The heart — a muscular pump that pushes blood around the body.
  • Blood vessels — arteries, veins and capillaries that carry the blood.
  • Blood — the liquid that actually carries oxygen, food, wastes, heat and defence cells.
🌟 Picture a city: the heart is the pumping station, the vessels are the pipes, and the blood is the water delivering supplies and washing away waste.

2. The four chambers

The mammalian heart is a hollow bag of muscle divided into four chambers. The two upper chambers are the atria (singular: atrium); the two lower chambers are the ventricles. A wall called the septum separates the left side from the right side, so oxygenated and deoxygenated blood never mix.

  • Right atrium — receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
  • Right ventricle — pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs.
  • Left atrium — receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
  • Left ventricle — pumps oxygenated blood to the whole body.

3. Double circulation

In mammals the blood passes through the heart twice for each complete trip around the body. This is called double circulation, and it is made of two loops:

  • Pulmonary circulation — heart ↔ lungs. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs to pick up oxygen, and it returns to the left side. (Pulmo = lung.)
  • Systemic circulation — heart ↔ body. The left side pumps oxygenated blood to all the body tissues, and it returns to the right side.
Body → RIGHT heart → Lungs → LEFT heart → Body

Why double circulation is better: blood is re-pressurised by the heart after it leaves the lungs, so it can be pushed to the whole body at high pressure and high speed. This keeps oxygen delivery fast — perfect for active, warm-blooded animals.

🌟 Two loops, one shared pump: the RIGHT side always deals with the LUNGS, the LEFT side always deals with the BODY.

4. Why the left ventricle has the thickest wall

All four chambers are muscular, but the left ventricle has by far the thickest, most muscular wall. Here is why:

  • The left ventricle must pump oxygenated blood through the aorta to the ENTIRE body — a very long journey against high resistance.
  • This needs a high pressure, so it needs a thick, powerful muscle wall to squeeze hard.
  • The right ventricle only pumps to the nearby lungs (a short trip at lower pressure), so its wall is thinner.
  • The atria have the thinnest walls — they only push blood a short way down into the ventricles.
🌟 The left ventricle is the body's long-distance pump; the right ventricle is the short-hop pump. Longer journey = thicker muscle = higher pressure.

5. Valves — preventing backflow

Blood must flow in one direction only. The heart has valves that snap shut to stop blood flowing backwards.

  • Bicuspid (mitral) valve — between the left atrium and left ventricle. (Bi = two flaps; think Left = mitraL.)
  • Tricuspid valve — between the right atrium and right ventricle. (Tri = three flaps.)
  • Semilunar valves — half-moon shaped valves at the exits, in the pulmonary artery and the aorta. They stop blood falling back into the ventricles.

The valves make the familiar "lub-dub" heart sounds as they close. If a valve leaks, blood flows backwards and the heart works less efficiently.

🌟 Tricuspid is on the Right (TRIcuspid, think "TRy the Right"); the bicuspid/mitral is on the left.

6. The cardiac cycle & heartbeat (simply)

One complete heartbeat is called the cardiac cycle. It has two main phases:

  • Systole = contraction. The muscle squeezes and pushes blood out. (When the ventricles undergo systole, blood is forced into the arteries.)
  • Diastole = relaxation. The muscle relaxes and the chambers fill up with blood again.

The beat is triggered by the heart's own natural pacemaker (the SA node in the right atrium), which is why the heart can beat on its own rhythm. A resting human heart beats about 70 times a minute. The pulse you feel in an artery is this pressure wave from each ventricular systole.

🌟 Systole = Squeeze (both start with S). Diastole = Dilate/rest. Squeeze then rest, over and over.

🩸 PART 2 — Blood Vessels & Blood

1. The three kinds of blood vessel

There are three types of blood vessel, each built for its job:

  • Arteries carry blood away from the heart, at high pressure.
  • Veins carry blood back to the heart, at low pressure.
  • Capillaries are tiny vessels that link arteries to veins and allow exchange of materials with the cells.

2. What is blood made of?

Blood is a tissue made of a liquid part and cells floating in it. About 55% is plasma and 45% is cells.

The components of blood
ComponentDescriptionMain job
PlasmaPale yellow liquid, mostly water (about 90%)Carries dissolved food, CO₂, urea, hormones, salts, heat

3. Red blood cells & haemoglobin

Red blood cells are perfectly designed to carry oxygen:

  • They contain the red pigment haemoglobin, which combines with oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Mature mammalian red cells have no nucleus — this leaves more room for haemoglobin (more oxygen carried).
  • They are a biconcave disc (dented on both sides) — this gives a large surface area for fast gas exchange and lets them squeeze through narrow capillaries.
🌟 Think of a red blood cell as a tiny oxygen delivery van — it throws out its "office desk" (the nucleus) to carry more cargo (haemoglobin).

4. White blood cells & platelets

  • White blood cells protect the body against disease. Some (phagocytes) engulf and digest germs; others (lymphocytes) make antibodies that destroy germs and give immunity.
  • Platelets are cell fragments that gather at a wound and help the blood to clot, sealing the cut and stopping bleeding and germ entry.

5. The functions of blood

Blood does three big jobs — remember T-D-R:

  • Transport: oxygen, carbon dioxide, digested food, hormones, urea, and heat around the body.
  • Defence: white blood cells and antibodies fight infection; platelets clot to seal wounds.
  • Regulation: helps keep body temperature, pH and water balance steady (homeostasis).

🔄 PART 3 — Circulation & Transport

1. The full path of blood

Follow one drop of blood around the whole double circulation. Learn this order — it is a favourite exam question:

Body → vena cava → RIGHT ATRIUM → RIGHT VENTRICLE → pulmonary artery → LUNGS → pulmonary vein → LEFT ATRIUM → LEFT VENTRICLE → aorta → Body
  • Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the right atrium through the vena cava.
  • It drops into the right ventricle, which pumps it through the pulmonary artery to the lungs.
  • In the lungs it picks up oxygen and drops off CO₂, then returns through the pulmonary vein to the left atrium.
  • It drops into the left ventricle, the strongest chamber, which pumps it through the aorta to the whole body.
🌟 Rule to never forget the order: Right side = to the lungs, Left side = to the body. Blood always visits the lungs between the two sides.

3. Blood clotting — the simple steps

When a blood vessel is cut, clotting seals the wound:

  • Step 1: Platelets stick to the wound and release chemicals.
  • Step 2: These chemicals (with vitamin K and calcium) turn the soluble protein fibrinogen into insoluble threads of fibrin.
  • Step 3: The fibrin threads form a mesh that traps red blood cells, making a clot.
  • Step 4: The clot dries into a scab, which stops bleeding and blocks germs while the skin heals.
🌟 Fibrinogen (liquid) becomes fibrin (net). The net catches blood cells and plugs the leak.

4. Tissue fluid & lymph

At the capillaries, some plasma leaks out through the thin walls to bathe the cells — this is tissue fluid. It delivers oxygen and food to cells and collects their wastes.

Most tissue fluid returns to the blood, but some is drained away by separate tubes called lymph vessels. Inside these vessels the fluid is called lymph. Lymph is eventually returned to the bloodstream, and lymph nodes along the way help filter out germs.

🌟 Insects = open (blood sloshes around inside); vertebrates like us = closed (blood stays in pipes). A closed system delivers oxygen faster, which active animals need.

🧬 PART 4 — Blood Groups & Health

1. Antigens & antibodies — the basic idea

Blood groups depend on tiny marker molecules called antigens on the surface of red blood cells, and matching antibodies floating in the plasma.

  • Antigen — a marker on the red cell. In the ABO system these are antigen A and antigen B.
  • Antibody — a protein in the plasma that attacks a foreign antigen and makes red cells clump together (agglutinate).
  • Golden rule: your plasma never contains the antibody against your own antigen (or your own blood would clump!). So group A has anti-B, group B has anti-A.

2. The ABO blood groups

GroupAntigen on red cellsAntibody in plasmaCan DONATE toCan RECEIVE from
AAanti-BA, ABA, O
BBanti-AB, ABB, O
ABA and BnoneAB onlyAll — universal recipient
Ononeanti-A and anti-BAll — universal donorO only
  • Group O = universal donor. Its red cells carry no A or B antigen, so no one's antibodies attack them — O blood can be given to anyone.
  • Group AB = universal recipient. Its plasma has no antibodies, so it will not attack any donated cells — AB can receive from anyone.
🌟 O = zerO antigens = gives to all (universal donor). AB = zero antibodies = takes from all (universal recipient).

3. The Rhesus (Rh) factor

Besides ABO, there is another antigen called the Rhesus (Rh or D) factor.

  • If your red cells carry the Rh antigen, you are Rhesus positive (Rh+). If not, you are Rhesus negative (Rh−).
  • So a full blood group is written like "A positive" or "O negative" — the letter is the ABO group and the sign is the Rh factor.
  • The Rh factor matters in pregnancy: an Rh− mother carrying an Rh+ baby can make antibodies that harm a later Rh+ baby, so doctors watch this carefully.

4. Safe blood transfusion

In a transfusion a patient is given donated blood. The donor's red cells must NOT be attacked by the patient's antibodies, or the cells clump and block vessels — which can be fatal. That is why groups are always matched (and cross-checked) first.

O gives to everyone · AB receives from everyone

5. Common circulatory issues & immunity

  • Anaemia — too few red blood cells or too little haemoglobin (often from lack of iron). The blood carries less oxygen, so the person feels tired, weak and pale.
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure) — blood pushes too hard on artery walls. Over time it strains the heart and can lead to stroke or heart disease. It is linked to too much salt, obesity, stress and lack of exercise.
  • Immunity & vaccination — white blood cells make antibodies against germs. A vaccine gives a harmless dose of a germ (or its markers) so the body makes antibodies and "memory" cells in advance — giving protection (immunity) before the real disease ever strikes.
🌟 A vaccine is like a fire drill for your immune system: it practises on a safe copy so it can react instantly to the real thing.
