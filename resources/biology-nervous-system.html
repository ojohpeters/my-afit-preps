🔗 PART 1 — The Neuron & The Nerve Impulse

1. Why living things need coordination

Coordination is the way an organism links up what its receptors detect (a stimulus) with the correct response from its effectors, so that all the parts of the body work together smoothly.

Your body is made of millions of cells. Without a control system they could not act as one. If your hand touches a hot pan, your eyes, brain, spinal cord and arm muscles must all cooperate instantly. Two systems do this coordination:

  • The nervous system — uses fast electrical impulses carried along nerve cells. Fast, short-lived, precise.
  • The endocrine (hormonal) system — uses chemical hormones carried in the blood. Slower, but longer-lasting.
The basic pathway of any response
Stimulus → Receptor → Coordinator (CNS) → Effector → Response
🌟 Memory: a receptor RECEIVES the message (a sense organ), an effector has an EFFECT / does the action (a muscle or gland).

2. The neuron — the unit of the nervous system

The nervous system is built from special cells called neurons (nerve cells). A neuron is the basic structural and functional unit of the nervous system, adapted to carry electrical messages very quickly over long distances.

Parts of a neuron (learn each one)
  • Dendrites — short, branched fibres that receive impulses and carry them towards the cell body.
  • Cell body (cyton) — contains the nucleus and cytoplasm; it keeps the cell alive and controls its activities.
  • Axon — one long fibre that carries the impulse away from the cell body towards the next cell.
  • Myelin sheath — a fatty layer wrapped around many axons. It insulates the axon and makes the impulse travel much faster.
  • Nodes of Ranvier — small gaps in the myelin sheath. The impulse jumps from node to node, which speeds it up further.
  • Synaptic knobs (axon terminals) — swellings at the end of the axon that pass the impulse on to the next neuron by releasing chemicals.
🌟 Picture a neuron as a wire: dendrites are the frayed input end, the axon is the long insulated cable (myelin = the plastic coating), and the synaptic knobs are the plug at the far end.

3. The three types of neuron

Neurons are classified by the direction in which they carry impulses. Learn the alternative names — examiners use both.

TypeOther nameWhat it doesWhere found
Sensory neuronAfferent neuronCarries impulses from receptors (sense organs) TO the CNSFrom skin, eye, ear etc. into the CNS
Relay neuronIntermediate / interneuron / connectorLinks sensory to motor neurons inside the CNSBrain and spinal cord
Motor neuronEfferent neuronCarries impulses from the CNS TO effectors (muscles/glands)From the CNS out to muscles and glands
🌟 Memory: afferent arrives at the brain (sensory in); efferent exits the brain (motor out). SAME order as the alphabet: Afferent before Efferent.

4. The nerve impulse and the synapse

A nerve impulse is an electrochemical signal — a wave of electrical change that sweeps along the axon. It is NOT a flow of blood and NOT a hormone; it is an electrical message travelling along a nerve fibre.

The synapse

Neurons do not actually touch. The tiny junction between the synaptic knob of one neuron and the next neuron is called a synapse. There is a microscopic gap here.

When the impulse reaches the synaptic knob, it cannot jump the gap as electricity. Instead the knob releases chemicals called neurotransmitters (for example acetylcholine). These diffuse across the gap and start a new impulse in the next neuron.

Two key jobs of the synapse
  • It passes the message on using neurotransmitters.
  • It acts as a one-way valve — chemicals are only released on one side, so impulses can only travel in one direction.
🌟 A synapse is like a ferry crossing a river: the electrical impulse (a car) cannot swim across, so it becomes a chemical (boards a ferry = neurotransmitter) to reach the other bank, then drives on again as electricity.

🧠 PART 2 — The Central Nervous System

1. How the whole system is divided

In mammals the nervous system has two main divisions:

  • Central nervous system (CNS) = the brain + spinal cord. This is the control and coordinating centre.
  • Peripheral nervous system (PNS) = all the nerves that connect the CNS to the rest of the body (covered in Part 4).

The CNS is where information is received, sorted and acted upon. It is protected by bone (the skull guards the brain; the vertebral column / backbone guards the spinal cord) and by membranes called the meninges.

2. The brain and its main parts

The brain is the master organ of coordination. Learn the four regions and the ONE job you must remember for each — this is the most common exam question.

Part of the brainMain function (remember this!)
Cerebrum (largest part, in two hemispheres)Thought, memory, intelligence, reasoning, and the seat of voluntary actions; also interprets the senses
Cerebellum (under the back of the cerebrum)Balance, posture and the coordination of muscular movement
Medulla oblongata (joins the spinal cord)Controls involuntary actions — heartbeat, breathing, swallowing, coughing, blood pressure
Hypothalamus (below the cerebrum)Regulates body temperature, water balance, thirst, hunger and sleep; links the nervous and endocrine systems
🌟 Memory: Cerebrum = your CONSCIOUS mind (thinking). Cerebellum = the little one that keeps you BALANCED. Medulla = the auto-pilot that keeps you alive (heart & lungs).

3. The spinal cord

The spinal cord is a thick cord of nerve tissue running down the back inside the vertebral column. It has two jobs:

  • A relay highway — it carries impulses to and from the brain, connecting the brain with the rest of the body.
  • A reflex centre — it is the coordinating centre for most reflex actions (like the knee-jerk), so a fast response can happen without waiting for the brain. Its relay neurons sit here.

4. The meninges — protective covering

The brain and spinal cord are wrapped in three protective membranes called the meninges. Between the membranes is a watery cerebrospinal fluid that cushions the delicate tissue against shock and supplies it with food and oxygen.

🌟 Don't confuse the coverings: meninges cover the brain/spinal cord, the pleura covers the lungs, and the pericardium covers the heart.

⚡ PART 3 — Reflex Action & The Reflex Arc

1. Voluntary vs involuntary actions

Actions of the body fall into two groups depending on whether the conscious brain (cerebrum) is in charge.

Voluntary actionInvoluntary action
Under conscious control?Yes — you decide to do itNo — happens automatically
Controlled byThe cerebrumSpinal cord, medulla, autonomic nerves
ExamplesWriting, talking, walking, throwing a ballHeartbeat, breathing, digestion, blinking, knee-jerk

A reflex action is a special kind of involuntary action: it is a fast, automatic response to a stimulus that does not need conscious thought. Its job is usually protection — pulling away from danger before you even feel the pain.

2. The reflex arc — the pathway of a reflex

The route an impulse follows during a reflex is called the reflex arc. In a spinal reflex the impulse does NOT go up to the brain first; it is dealt with in the spinal cord, which is why it is so fast.

The five steps — memorise this order
Receptor → Sensory neuron → Relay neuron (in spinal cord) → Motor neuron → Effector
  • Receptor — detects the stimulus (e.g. pain/heat receptors in the skin).
  • Sensory (afferent) neuron — carries the impulse to the spinal cord.
  • Relay neuron — sits inside the spinal cord and passes the impulse across to the motor neuron.
  • Motor (efferent) neuron — carries the impulse out to the effector.
  • Effector — a muscle or gland that carries out the response (e.g. the arm muscle contracts).

3. Worked examples of reflexes

Withdrawing your hand from a hot object: Heat receptors in the skin → sensory neuron to the spinal cord → relay neuron in the cord → motor neuron to the arm → effector (biceps muscle) contracts and jerks the hand away — all before you consciously feel the pain.
The knee-jerk (patellar) reflex: A tap below the kneecap stretches the muscle (stimulus) → sensory neuron → spinal cord → motor neuron → thigh muscle contracts → the lower leg kicks out. Doctors use it to check the nervous system is working.
🌟 Note: the effector in these examples is a muscle. In other reflexes (like salivating) the effector is a gland.

4. Simple reflex vs conditioned reflex

Not all reflexes are inborn. Some are learned by experience.

Simple (natural) reflexConditioned (learned) reflex
Present from birth?Yes — inborn, inheritedNo — learned through experience/training
Needs the brain?Usually just the spinal cord/medullaInvolves the cerebrum (learning)
Can it be lost?PermanentCan be forgotten if not reinforced
ExampleBlinking, knee-jerk, withdrawing from heatPavlov's dog salivating at a bell; your mouth watering at the smell of food
🌟 Pavlov's famous experiment: a bell was rung each time dogs were fed. Soon the bell alone (a new stimulus) made them salivate — a conditioned reflex built by repeated association.

🔄 PART 4 — Peripheral Nerves, Senses & Hormones

1. The peripheral nervous system (PNS)

The PNS is all the nerves that branch out from the CNS to reach every part of the body. It has two divisions based on what it controls:

  • Somatic nervous system — carries impulses to and from the skeletal (voluntary) muscles; it controls the actions you do consciously, like moving a limb.
  • Autonomic nervous system — controls the involuntary activities of internal organs (heart, gut, glands) that you cannot consciously control.

2. The autonomic nervous system — two opposites

The autonomic system has two parts that work against each other to keep the body balanced.

SympatheticParasympathetic
Overall rolePrepares the body for action — 'fight or flight'Calms the body down — 'rest and digest'
Effect on heartbeatSpeeds it upSlows it down
Effect on pupilsWidens (dilates) themNarrows (constricts) them
Effect on digestionSlows it downSpeeds it up
🌟 Sympathetic = the emergency accelerator (danger, stress). Parasympathetic = the brake that relaxes you afterwards.

3. Nervous vs hormonal (endocrine) coordination

Both systems coordinate the body, but they work very differently. This comparison is a favourite exam question.

FeatureNervous coordinationHormonal (endocrine) coordination
Transmitted byElectrical impulses along neuronsChemical hormones carried in the blood
SpeedVery fastSlower
Duration of effectShort-livedLong-lasting
Area affectedPrecise — a specific muscle/glandCan be widespread across the body
PathwayAlong nerve fibres to a targetDissolved in blood to target organs
🌟 Memory: nerves are like a phone call (instant, brief, one person); hormones are like a letter posted to many (slower to arrive, but the message lasts).

4. Sense organs — the receptors

Receptors are grouped into sense organs that detect particular stimuli and turn them into nerve impulses.

  • Eye — receptor for light (sight). Light-sensitive cells in the retina send impulses along the optic nerve.
  • Ear — receptor for sound (hearing) and also for balance (the semicircular canals).
  • Skin — receptors for touch, pressure, pain, heat and cold.
  • Nose — receptor for smell (chemicals in air); Tongue — receptor for taste (chemicals in food).

5. The endocrine glands and their hormones

Endocrine glands are ductless — they pour their hormones straight into the blood. Learn one gland-and-hormone pair at a time.

GlandA hormone it makesOne main job
Pituitary (the master gland)Growth hormoneControls growth and other glands
ThyroidThyroxineControls the rate of metabolism
Pancreas (islets)InsulinLowers blood sugar level
Adrenal glandsAdrenalineThe 'fight or flight' emergency hormone
Ovaries (female)OestrogenFemale secondary sexual characteristics
Testes (male)TestosteroneMale secondary sexual characteristics
🌟 The pituitary is called the master gland because its hormones switch other glands on and off.
