📖 Understanding Moments (Turning Effect of a Force)

1. What is a moment?

A moment is the turning effect of a force about a point (called the pivot or fulcrum). Whenever a force makes something rotate, turn, or tip, it is producing a moment.

🌟 Real life: pushing a door open, turning a spanner on a nut, a seesaw going up and down, opening a bottle cap, using scissors — all of these are moments in action.

2. The formula for a moment

Moment = Force × perpendicular distance from the pivot
M = F × d
  • M = moment, measured in newton-metre (N m)
  • F = the force, in newtons (N)
  • d = the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of the force, in metres (m)

So a moment gets bigger if you use a bigger force OR if you apply it further from the pivot.

Worked example: A force of 20 N acts 0.5 m from a pivot. Find the moment.
M = F × d = 20 × 0.5 = 10 N m

3. Why distance matters so much (the spanner trick)

The same force gives a bigger turning effect when applied further from the pivot. That is why:

  • A long spanner loosens a tight nut more easily than a short one.
  • A door handle is placed far from the hinge — push near the hinge and the door barely moves.
  • A child can lift a heavier adult on a seesaw by sitting further out.
💡 Memory hook: "Big force OR long arm = big turn." Double the distance → double the moment.

4. Clockwise and anticlockwise moments

A force can turn an object two ways about the pivot:

  • Clockwise moment — turns it like a clock’s hands (to the right / downward on the right side).
  • Anticlockwise moment — turns it the opposite way (to the left / downward on the left side).

On a seesaw, weight on the right of the pivot turns it clockwise; weight on the left turns it anticlockwise. These two fight against each other — and that fight is the heart of the principle of moments.

⚖️ The Principle of Moments

1. The statement (memorise this)

When a body is in equilibrium (balanced), the sum of CLOCKWISE moments about any point = the sum of ANTICLOCKWISE moments about that point.

In short:

Total clockwise moments = Total anticlockwise moments

“In equilibrium” means the body is balanced — it is not turning. Think of a seesaw resting level, or a beam balance that has settled.

🌟 Picture two children on a seesaw sitting perfectly still and level. The one on the left tries to turn it anticlockwise; the one on the right tries to turn it clockwise. They balance because their moments are equal.

2. The two conditions for equilibrium

For an object to be in complete (static) equilibrium, BOTH must be true:

  • ① Sum of forces = 0 (forces up = forces down; forces left = forces right). The body does not move off in any direction.
  • ② Sum of moments = 0 (clockwise = anticlockwise). The body does not rotate.

Condition ① is the principle of moments’ partner — it’s how you find support/reaction forces (see worked examples).

3. How to SOLVE any moments problem (4 easy steps)

StepWhat to do
1Find the pivot (the turning point / support / fulcrum).
2List every force and its perpendicular distance from the pivot.
3Work out each moment (F × d) and label it clockwise or anticlockwise.
4Set clockwise = anticlockwise and solve for the unknown.

Tip: a force acting at the pivot has zero distance, so its moment is zero — it doesn’t affect balance. Smart students take moments about a point where an unknown force acts, so that unknown cancels out.

4. Centre of gravity & uniform objects

The centre of gravity (c.g.) is the single point where the whole weight of an object seems to act. For a uniform rod or metre rule, the c.g. is at the middle (centre). So in problems, hang the rod’s weight at its midpoint (the 50 cm mark for a metre rule).

💡 If a uniform metre rule is balanced at its centre, its own weight acts right at the pivot → its moment is zero → you can ignore the rod’s weight in that case.

5. A couple (bonus)

A couple is two equal, parallel forces acting in opposite directions but not along the same line — they produce pure turning (rotation) with no overall movement.

Moment of a couple = Force × perpendicular distance between the two forces
Example: turning a steering wheel or tap with both hands pushing opposite ways.

🧮 Fully Worked Examples

Example 1 — Balancing a seesaw (find the force)

A child of weight 300 N sits 2 m to the LEFT of the pivot. Where another person sits 3 m to the RIGHT, what weight will balance the seesaw?

Solution:
Anticlockwise moment (left child) = 300 × 2 = 600 N m
Clockwise moment (right person) = F × 3
Balance: 600 = F × 3 → F = 600 ÷ 3 = 200 N
Answer: 200 N. (Notice: sitting further out, the person needs LESS weight to balance.)

Example 2 — Metre rule (find an unknown weight)

A light (weightless) metre rule is pivoted at its centre (50 cm mark). A 2 N weight hangs at the 20 cm mark. What weight at the 90 cm mark balances it?

Solution: distances are measured FROM the pivot (50 cm).
Left weight distance = 50 − 20 = 30 cm
Right weight distance = 90 − 50 = 40 cm
Anticlockwise = 2 × 30 = 60 (N·cm)
Clockwise = W × 40
60 = 40W → W = 60 ÷ 40 = 1.5 N
Answer: 1.5 N. (Units of distance cancel as long as both sides use the same unit — cm is fine here.)

Example 3 — Heavy (uniform) rule with its own weight

A uniform metre rule weighing 1 N is pivoted at the 30 cm mark. A 4 N weight hangs at the 10 cm mark. Find the single force needed at the 100 cm end to balance it.

Solution: The rule’s own weight (1 N) acts at its centre, the 50 cm mark. Take moments about the pivot (30 cm).
4 N at 10 cm → distance = 30 − 10 = 20 cm (anticlockwise) → 4 × 20 = 80
1 N rule weight at 50 cm → distance = 50 − 30 = 20 cm (clockwise) → 1 × 20 = 20
Unknown F at 100 cm → distance = 100 − 30 = 70 cm (clockwise) → F × 70
Anticlockwise = Clockwise: 80 = 20 + 70F
80 − 20 = 70F → 60 = 70F → F = 0.857 N ≈ 0.86 N
Answer: ≈ 0.86 N. (Always remember to include the rule’s own weight at its centre when it is NOT pivoted at the centre.)

Example 4 — Finding a support (reaction) force

A uniform beam of weight 100 N and length 4 m rests on two supports, one at each end (A on the left, B on the right). A 60 N load sits 1 m from end A. Find the reaction force at each support.

Solution: Take moments about A (so the unknown at A cancels). The beam’s 100 N acts at its centre (2 m from A).
Clockwise about A: (60 × 1) + (100 × 2) = 60 + 200 = 260 N m
Anticlockwise about A: reaction at B × 4 m = R_B × 4
R_B × 4 = 260 → R_B = 65 N
Now use condition ① (forces balance): total up = total down.
R_A + R_B = 100 + 60 = 160
R_A = 160 − 65 = 95 N
Answer: R_A = 95 N, R_B = 65 N.

Example 5 — Find the pivot position

A uniform 0.8 m plank balances when a 5 N weight is hung at one end and a 3 N weight at the other. The plank is light. How far from the 5 N end is the pivot?

Solution: Let the pivot be x metres from the 5 N end. Then it is (0.8 − x) from the 3 N end.
5 × x = 3 × (0.8 − x)
5x = 2.4 − 3x
5x + 3x = 2.4 → 8x = 2.4 → x = 0.3 m
Answer: 0.3 m from the 5 N end. (The pivot sits closer to the heavier weight — that makes sense!)

⭐ Quick exam reminders

  • Distance must be the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force.
  • Measure all distances from the pivot, not from the end of the rule.
  • For a uniform rule/beam, put its weight at the centre.
  • Forces acting at the pivot give zero moment.
  • For full equilibrium use BOTH: moments balance AND forces balance.
  • Unit of moment is N m (newton-metre) — never just N.
